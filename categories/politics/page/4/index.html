Fahmi Blames 'Opposition Confusion' for PM Tenure Bill Failure — When 8 of His OWN MPs Didn't Show Up

Said by: Fahmi Fadzil, Communications Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-05

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On March 5, 2026, Communications Minister Fahmi Fadzil told reporters in Putrajaya that the constitutional amendment bill to limit the PM’s tenure to 10 years failed because opposition MPs were “confused.” He claimed several opposition lawmakers mistakenly believed the bill was designed to give PM Anwar Ibrahim a 10-year term in office, rather than to cap any future PM’s tenure. Fahmi said: “There were MPs from the opposition who gave feedback to one of the ministers saying they thought the vote that day was meant to give Datuk Seri Anwar Ibrahim a 10-year term as prime minister.” He did not identify which opposition MPs were allegedly confused. PAS secretary-general Takiyuddin Hassan hit back, calling the claim “irresponsible” and “misplaced.”

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: The bill failed by just 2 votes — 146 in favour, 148 needed. The government commands 154 MPs. Eight government MPs were absent. The opposition had already announced it would abstain unless amendments were agreed upon. This means the bill failed entirely because the government couldn’t get its own people to show up. The math is simple: 154 minus 8 absent = 146. The opposition’s abstention was publicly announced in advance. There was no surprise. The failure is a whipping failure, not an opposition confusion problem. Blaming the opposition for being “confused” when the government had more than enough votes — if only its own MPs had bothered to attend — is a masterclass in deflection.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Let’s be clear about what happened: the government had 154 MPs — six more than the 148 needed. Eight government MPs didn’t show up. The opposition announced in advance it would abstain. The bill failed by exactly the number of absent government MPs. And Fahmi’s response? Blame the opposition for being confused. This is the Communications Minister — the person whose literal job is to communicate clearly — claiming that opposition MPs couldn’t understand what the bill was about. If that were true, it would be an indictment of the government’s own communication. If it’s false, it’s a convenient excuse to avoid accountability for a humiliating coordination failure. Fahmi also couldn’t name a single “confused” MP. Because the story isn’t about confusion — it’s about the government not having enough discipline to get 148 of its 154 MPs into the chamber for a vote it had three years to prepare for.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: This follows a well-established PH pattern of blaming others for self-inflicted failures:

  • When the Sabah election was lost, the blame went to “local factors” — not PH’s weak campaign
  • When manifesto promises went unmet, the blame went to “the mess we inherited” — three years later
  • When the MACC scandal erupted, Anwar blamed “Zionist conspiracies” and “foreign media” — not the agency’s own misconduct
  • When Bersih criticised MACC, Anwar called it “unfair” — not a legitimate concern The government that promised radical transparency can’t even take responsibility for its own attendance sheet.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You had the numbers. You didn’t have the discipline. That’s on you — not the opposition. 154 MPs. 148 needed. 8 absent. The arithmetic writes its own headline. No amount of spin about “confused” opposition MPs changes the fact that your coalition failed to deliver on your own reform agenda because your own MPs didn’t show up. If you can’t whip 148 out of 154 for a constitutional amendment you’ve been promising since 2018, what reform can you actually deliver? And if blaming opposition “confusion” is the best Communications Minister Fahmi Fadzil can manage, perhaps the communication problem is on your side of the aisle.

Deputy Minister Tells Parliament Hindu Endowments Must Be at State Level — The Constitution Says Otherwise

Said by: R Yuneswaran, Deputy National Unity Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-04

📌 What Happened

On March 4, 2026, Deputy National Unity Minister R Yuneswaran told the Dewan Negara that Hindu endowments boards must be established at the state level, claiming this was in line with constitutional provisions under the State List of the Ninth Schedule. He was responding to a proposal by senator Dr RA Lingeshwaran for a national-level Hindu endowments board to regulate temple issues. Yuneswaran said the ministry would “fully support” states in setting up their own boards, citing Penang’s model. Former Penang assemblyman Satees Muniandy and former Penang Hindu Endowments Board chairman P Ramasamy immediately called out the deputy minister, pointing to Item 15(c) of the Federal List, which explicitly places Hindu endowments under federal — not state — jurisdiction.

🔥 Why It Matters

A deputy minister gave constitutionally incorrect information in Parliament — the upper house of the country’s legislature. This is not a matter of interpretation or grey-area constitutional law. Item 15(c) of the Federal List is unambiguous: Hindu endowments fall under federal purview. When a minister responsible for national unity cannot accurately cite the constitutional provisions governing a minority community’s religious endowments, it signals either incompetence or a deliberate attempt to deflect federal responsibility onto state governments — either of which erodes trust in PH’s commitment to institutional governance and minority rights.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

PH campaigned as the coalition that would restore constitutional governance, protect minority communities, and ensure competent leadership in Cabinet. Instead, we have a deputy minister — from the national unity portfolio, no less — who cannot be bothered to read the Federal List before answering a question in the Dewan Negara. The constitution is not ambiguous on this point. The Penang Hindu Endowments Board that Yuneswaran cited as a state-level model is, as Ramasamy pointed out, actually a federal body administered by the state that reports to Parliament. Yuneswaran didn’t just get the answer wrong — he got the entire constitutional framework backwards and then used it to justify inaction at the federal level.

📜 Previous Cases

This is part of a broader pattern of PH ministers and deputy ministers making inaccurate or misleading statements in Parliament. The Madani government has repeatedly shown a tendency to push federal responsibilities down to state governments when it comes to minority community issues, while simultaneously centralising power on matters that serve its political interests. Malaysian parliamentary history is littered with ministerial answers that misstate constitutional provisions — the difference is that PH promised to be better. The national unity ministry, specifically, has been criticised for being more focused on managing optics around racial and religious tensions than on delivering substantive policy outcomes for minority communities.

🎯 PH Callout

You told Malaysians you would govern with competence, integrity, and respect for the constitution. Your deputy minister just told the Dewan Negara that Hindu endowments are a state matter when the Federal List says otherwise — in black and white. If your ministers can’t even read the Ninth Schedule before answering parliamentary questions, what exactly is the standard of governance you’re offering? The Hindu community deserves a government that knows its own constitution. Fix the record, correct the Hansard, and tell the rakyat whether you actually intend to exercise federal responsibility over Hindu endowments — or whether you’re just passing the buck.

Fahmi Says AG-PP Separation Bill Needs 3 More Months — PH's Flagship Reform Keeps Getting Pushed Back

Said by: Fahmi Fadzil, Communications Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-04

📌 What Happened

On March 4, Communications Minister Fahmi Fadzil announced that the bill to separate the Attorney General and Public Prosecutor roles requires three more months of refinement before it can be tabled. A select committee chaired by Datuk Seri Azalina Othman Said will convene on March 16 to examine feedback from MPs. This came just one day after the government failed to pass the PM tenure limit bill — another flagship constitutional reform — falling two votes short of the required two-thirds majority. The AG-PP separation was one of Anwar’s earliest and most prominent reform pledges, first announced as part of his institutional reform package.

🔥 Why It Matters

The AG-PP separation is not a nice-to-have — it is the structural reform most directly connected to prosecutorial independence and the integrity of Malaysia’s legal system. Under the current system, the Attorney General serves as both the government’s chief legal advisor and the nation’s top prosecutor, creating an inherent conflict of interest that has been exploited by every administration. PH campaigned explicitly on fixing this. Three years into power, they are still forming committees and asking for more time. With elections potentially months away, the window for passing constitutional amendments with a supermajority is closing fast — and the MACC-Azam Baki crisis has made the case for an independent prosecutor more urgent than ever.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

PH has had a parliamentary supermajority since 2022. They have had three years to draft, refine, and pass this bill. Instead, they formed a select committee, which will now meet to “examine feedback” — the bureaucratic equivalent of hitting the snooze button on the alarm you set yourself. The PM tenure bill already failed because the government couldn’t even whip its own MPs to show up for a vote. Now the AG-PP bill needs “three more months” of work. Meanwhile, Attorney General Mohd Dusuki Mokhtar — the man whose dual role this bill is supposed to reform — previously withdrew corruption charges against Deputy Prime Minister Ahmad Zahid Hamidi despite a court finding enough evidence to proceed. The conflict of interest isn’t theoretical. It is actively shaping Malaysian justice right now, and PH is asking for another quarter to think about it.

📜 Previous Cases

This is a recurring pattern. The PM tenure limit bill was announced in January 2026 as part of a reform package, tabled in March, and failed on its first attempt. The Freedom of Information Act, also promised, remains in draft. The repeal of the Sedition Act — a signature PH pledge from GE14 — was never delivered during the first PH government and has now been effectively abandoned in favour of new restrictive speech laws. The Peaceful Assembly Act reforms are similarly stalled. PH’s reform agenda reads like a graveyard of good intentions: every pledge is announced with fanfare, delegated to a committee, delayed by “consultations,” and eventually overtaken by the election cycle.

🎯 PH Callout

You told Malaysians that Madani governance means institutional reform, not just slogans. You said the AG-PP separation would end the conflict of interest that allowed previous governments to weaponise prosecution. Three years and a supermajority later, your Communications Minister is asking for three more months. Three months will become six. Six will become “after the election.” Malaysians have seen this playbook before — they lived through it under BN for six decades. If you cannot deliver the reforms you promised with the largest parliamentary majority in a generation, you do not deserve another mandate to make the same promises again.

Now in government, their own MB is doing the same thing and asking for sympathy. The entitlement transcends party lines — it’s baked into how Malaysian political elites understand their own importance.

🎯 PH Callout

You campaigned on humility. You told Malaysians that the era of feudal politics and VIP culture was over. Your own Prime Minister once made a show of flying economy class and driving himself. Now your Negri Sembilan MB is running red lights with police cover and telling the rakyat to look at his Facebook to understand why he’s too important to stop at a traffic light. You don’t get to break them and call it public service.

Anwar Condemns US-Israel Strikes on Iran in Parliament — 'We Can No Longer Swallow' Western Human Rights Lectures, PH MP Calls for US Embassy Boycott

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister / Syahredzan Johan (PH-Bangi)

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-02

📌 WHAT HAPPENED

On 2 March 2026, Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim tabled an emergency motion in the Dewan Rakyat condemning the US and Israel’s strikes on Iran, which began on 28 February and killed at least 201 people including Iran’s Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei.

Anwar called the strikes a “flagrant violation of international law” and described Western lectures on human rights and democracy as hypocrisy:

“In my engagements with the American and European leadership, I am uneasy about their lectures on human rights and democracy. We can no longer swallow such arguments from them.”

The motion passed unanimously with bipartisan support. Opposition Leader Hamzah Zainudin praised Anwar for a “stern and firm statement.”

But the most politically significant moment came from PH backbencher Syahredzan Johan (Bangi), who called on all MPs to boycott events hosted by the US embassy in Kuala Lumpur:

“Let’s not go. Even though we get invited to their Independence Day celebrations, let’s not go. We need to show that we can no longer accept what they do.”

Anwar also signaled a diplomatic repositioning toward Iran, calling for closer education and trade ties, claiming Malaysia was “the only country to show a firm, brave stance.”

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS

The condemnation itself is unremarkable — Malaysia has consistently supported Palestinian and Iranian sovereignty. What matters is the escalation in rhetoric and the gap between words and actions.

First, Anwar calling Western human rights lectures “hypocrisy” is rich coming from a prime minister whose own government is under fire for:

  • Protecting MACC chief Azam Baki despite multiple corruption allegations
  • Using police investigations to target critics under the guise of a “Zionist plot”
  • Halving Najib’s sentence while the 1MDB debt still runs into billions
  • Failing to pass even basic institutional reforms like the PM tenure bill Anwar is lecturing the West about hypocrisy while running a government that has abandoned virtually every reform promise it made.

Second, Syahredzan’s call to boycott the US embassy is performative populism with real diplomatic consequences. Malaysia’s economy depends on US trade and investment. The US is Malaysia’s third-largest trading partner. Calling for a diplomatic boycott from the floor of Parliament is not a principled stand — it’s cheap political theatre that someone else will have to clean up.

Third, the pivot toward Iran is diplomatically reckless. Iran’s Shia denomination is officially considered “deviant” in Sunni-majority Malaysia. Anwar is signaling solidarity with a theocratic state whose religious identity his own government criminalises. The contradiction is absurd.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT

Let’s count the contradictions:

On human rights hypocrisy:

  • Anwar says he can “no longer swallow” Western lectures on human rights. This is the same man who spent years lobbying Western governments, NGOs, and media outlets for his own human rights — during his imprisonment under Mahathir and Najib. His entire political comeback was built on international sympathy and foreign advocacy.

  • Now that he’s in power, foreign advocacy is a “Zionist plot” and Western human rights standards are “hypocrisy.” The only thing that changed is which side of the PM’s desk he sits on. On the US embassy boycott:

  • Syahredzan Johan is a PH backbencher calling for a diplomatic boycott while his own government maintains full diplomatic, trade, and military relations with the US. Malaysia just signed a Comprehensive Strategic Partnership with Washington in October 2025.

  • If PH is serious about opposing US actions, why stop at embassy cocktail parties? Cancel the trade deal. Recall the ambassador. Oh wait — that would cost money. On Iran solidarity:

  • Malaysia criminalises Shia practice. Anwar’s own religious affairs apparatus treats Shia Muslims as deviants. Declaring solidarity with Iran while persecuting its co-religionists at home is not principled — it’s opportunistic.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES

  • 2018-2022: Anwar’s own foreign lobbying. Anwar and PKR allies engaged Washington consultancies, international media, and foreign governments to pressure the Najib and Muhyiddin administrations. He accepted this support gratefully. Now foreign engagement is subversion.
  • October 2025: Malaysia-US Comprehensive Strategic Partnership. Just five months before calling US human rights lectures “hypocrisy,” Anwar’s government signed an upgraded bilateral deal with Washington covering trade, critical minerals, and market access.
  • Malaysia’s track record on Iran. Malaysia has historically been cautious with Iran due to the Sunni-Shia divide. Anwar’s pivot is a departure from decades of careful diplomatic hedging, driven by the emotional politics of the moment rather than strategic calculation.
  • 2024-2025: Gaza solidarity without substance. Malaysia has been vocal on Palestine but has taken no meaningful economic or diplomatic action against Israel beyond rhetoric. The pattern is clear: loud words, zero follow-through.

🎯 PH CALLOUT

Anwar, you told Parliament: “We can no longer swallow such arguments from them.”

Fair enough. But the rakyat can no longer swallow yours either.

You lecture the West on hypocrisy while your MACC chief sits on RM800,000 in undisclosed shares. You call foreign advocacy a “Zionist plot” after spending decades doing exactly the same thing. You signal solidarity with Iran while your religious authorities criminalise Shia practice at home.

And your backbencher calls for boycotting US embassy parties — the absolute lowest-cost, lowest-risk form of protest imaginable — while your government maintains a full strategic partnership with Washington.

This isn’t principled foreign policy. It’s Ramadan-season populism. You’re performing outrage for domestic consumption while doing nothing that would actually cost you anything.

If you want to be taken seriously on human rights and sovereignty, start at home. Fix your own institutions. Reform your own enforcement agencies. Stop using conspiracy theories to silence critics. Then — and only then — will anyone take your lectures to the West seriously.
