AG-Public Prosecutor Separation Bill Postponed for 3-Month Committee Review — Reform Delayed Again

Hantu Raya

March 4, 2026

Said by: Fahmi Fadzil, Communications Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-04

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 4 March 2026, the Constitution (Amendment) Bill to separate the roles of Attorney General and Public Prosecutor was referred to a parliamentary special select committee for three more months of review instead of being debated. Communications Minister Fahmi Fadzil confirmed the timeline. This is a cornerstone PH reform promise — pledged since GE14 in 2018 — still not passed after more than three years in government. Bersih called for the committee to conduct a thorough review including mechanisms for parliamentary and judicial oversight.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: The AG/PP split is not a new idea. It has been studied, debated, and promised for nearly a decade. Three more months of “review” looks less like diligence and more like delay. As long as the AG is also the public prosecutor, the person who advises the government on law is the same person who decides who gets prosecuted. This is exactly the structural conflict that enabled the Zahid DNAA and the selective enforcement critics keep flagging. Every month this bill stays unpassed is a month the government benefits from the status quo it claims to oppose.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: This bill came two days after the PM term limit vote failed — suggesting the government is sequencing reforms not by importance but by political convenience. Referring it to committee is the parliamentary equivalent of putting something in a drawer and hoping everyone forgets. PH has a supermajority. It doesn’t need three more months of study on a reform it has promised for eight years. The committee referral is a stalling mechanism dressed as due process.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: PH promised this in the GE14 manifesto (2018). It was not done during PH 1.0. It was promised again for GE15 (2022). The bill was only tabled for first reading on 23 February 2026 — more than three years into the term. Malaysia’s track record on constitutional reform is consistent: promise loudly, delay quietly, blame complexity. The IPCMC (police oversight) bill followed the same trajectory and was eventually abandoned.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You’ve had eight years and two election mandates to pass this bill. You have a supermajority. You don’t need a three-month committee. You need political will. Either pass it before the next election or admit that the AG/PP split was always a manifesto decoration, not a governance priority. The rakyat can tell the difference between reform and a review committee.
