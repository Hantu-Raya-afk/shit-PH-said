Fahmi Confirms MCMC Blocked Grindr and Blued — PH's 'Reform Government' Now Runs Digital Censorship Against Minorities

Hantu Raya

February 25, 2026

Said by: Fahmi Fadzil, Communications Minister

Category: Social

Date: 2026-02-25

📌 What Happened

On February 25, 2026, Communications Minister Fahmi Fadzil confirmed in a written parliamentary reply that the Malaysian Communications and Multimedia Commission (MCMC) has blocked the websites of Grindr and Blued — two of the world’s largest dating and networking platforms used by gay and bisexual men. Fahmi said MCMC is now “studying several legislative measures” to curb the apps from Google Play and the Apple App Store, though he acknowledged that control over foreign-owned platforms is “subject to regulations and policies set by the said platform providers.” By early March, both apps were removed from Malaysian app stores entirely. Deputy Prime Minister Ahmad Zahid Hamidi separately announced a proposed Cyber Crime Bill to expand the government’s digital enforcement powers, including over “immoral” content.

🔥 Why It Matters

This is the Madani government using state infrastructure to systematically erase digital safe spaces for a persecuted minority community — and doing it quietly through written parliamentary replies and regulatory action rather than open policy debate. Fahmi framed it under the catch-all of “lewd or immoral content, exploitation, abuse, scams, exploiting children or threats towards public safety” — a masterclass in conflating consenting adults with criminals. The blocking comes alongside Deputy Religious Affairs Minister Marhamah Rosli’s directive to replace all references to “LGBT” with “budaya songsang” (deviant culture) across government communications and social media, and a multi-agency committee targeting Muslim LGBTQ individuals with so-called “psychospiritual therapy.” This is not morality policing on the margins — it is a coordinated state apparatus targeting a specific community using censorship, surveillance, and institutional erasure.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

Pakatan Harapan ran on civil liberties, institutional reform, and the promise that Malaysia would stop using the state to persecute its own citizens. Fahmi Fadzil — a former civil society figure and one-time press freedom advocate — is now the minister signing off on internet censorship targeting a minority. The same government that screams about “freedom of expression” when its critics get summoned under Section 124B is perfectly comfortable ordering MCMC to block websites and pressure Apple and Google to delist apps. The Cyber Crime Bill that Zahid is pushing will replace the Computer Crimes Act 1997 with broader powers — the exact kind of legislative overreach PH promised to roll back. This is not conservative governance; it is a reformist government that has decided persecuting minorities is electorally convenient.

📜 Previous Cases

This follows a well-documented pattern of escalation under the Anwar administration. In May 2023, the Home Affairs Ministry raided Swatch stores nationwide and confiscated over 160 rainbow-coloured watches. In June 2025, Kelantan police raided a private property described as a “gay party” and arrested 20 men. In November 2025, 202 people were arrested in raids on men-only venues in Kuala Lumpur — detainees reported being denied legal counsel, subjected to degrading treatment, and having their identities publicly exposed. Justice for Sisters documented systematic procedural violations. In January 2026, a community organisation cancelled its “Glamping with Pride” event after receiving death threats. Each time, the government either endorsed the action, stayed silent, or expanded the legal tools available for the next crackdown.

🎯 PH Callout

You promised Malaysians a government that would defend civil liberties, roll back repressive laws, and protect the vulnerable from state overreach. Instead, you are building new censorship infrastructure, proposing broader cyber legislation, and running conversion programmes through state religious departments — all while Fahmi Fadzil, who once championed media freedom, signs the blocking orders. Every digital rights group, every international press freedom watchdog, and every human rights organisation monitoring Malaysia is watching this and drawing the obvious conclusion: Madani reform was never meant for everyone. If you cannot even leave people’s phones alone, stop pretending you are a reform government.
