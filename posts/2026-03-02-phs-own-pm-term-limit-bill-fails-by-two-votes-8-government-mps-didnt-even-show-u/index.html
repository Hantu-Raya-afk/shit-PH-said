PH's Own PM Term Limit Bill Fails by Two Votes — 8 Government MPs Didn't Even Show Up

Hantu Raya

March 2, 2026

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister / Fahmi Fadzil, Communications Minister / Anthony Loke, DAP Secretary-General

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-02

📌 What Happened

On March 2, 2026, Malaysia’s Dewan Rakyat voted on the Constitution (Amendment) Bill 2026 — PH’s long-promised reform to limit the prime minister’s tenure to 10 years (two terms). The bill needed 148 votes — a two-thirds supermajority — to pass. It got 146. Two votes short. Speaker Tan Sri Johari Abdul confirmed that 44 MPs abstained (mostly opposition), 32 were absent, and — in a telling detail — not a single MP voted against it. Among the absentees were 8 government-supporting MPs. Communications Minister Fahmi Fadzil later confirmed that only 2 of the 8 had submitted formal explanations; 6 had not even bothered. DAP Secretary-General Anthony Loke issued a strongly worded statement emphasising that all 40 DAP MPs were present, and pointedly called out the empty seats.

🔥 Why It Matters

This was supposed to be PH’s signature institutional reform — the centrepiece of a manifesto promise dating back to the 2022 general election, born directly from the trauma of Mahathir’s 22-year reign. A PM term limit is one of the most consequential democratic safeguards any government can enact. And PH couldn’t even get its own coalition to show up. The bill wasn’t defeated by opposition — it was killed by indifference, incompetence, and absenteeism from within the government’s own ranks. PKR MP Wong Chen later revealed that at least one absent MP said he “was simply not properly informed to turn up” and received no follow-up calls. This is not just a legislative failure — it is a governance failure that exposes the hollowness of PH’s reform brand.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

PH had three years to prepare for this vote. Three years to whip their own MPs, count heads, confirm attendance, and deliver the most basic requirement of parliamentary democracy — showing up. Deputy PM Fadillah Yusof is the government chief whip. His one job in this context was to ensure every government MP was present. He failed. And the excuse from absent MPs — “I wasn’t properly informed” — is breathtaking in its casualness. This was a constitutional amendment, not a backbench briefing on drainage policy. If PH cannot mobilise its own MPs for its own flagship reform bill, then the reform agenda is not a priority — it is a slogan. The opposition abstained, as expected. But the government’s absentees are the ones who killed this bill.

📜 Previous Cases

Malaysia has never had a statutory term limit for the prime minister. Mahathir served 22 years (1981–2003), then returned for another 22 months (2018–2020). The damage of unchecked executive tenure is well-documented in Malaysian governance — from the entrenchment of patronage networks to the erosion of institutional independence. PH’s 2022 manifesto explicitly promised this reform. In the 2024 Budget speech, Anwar reaffirmed the government’s commitment to institutional reform. Yet when it came time to deliver, PH’s coalition discipline — a perennial weakness since the Sheraton Move — once again proved inadequate. This is the same coalition that lost its own government in 2020 because it couldn’t hold its ranks together.

🎯 PH Callout

You made this promise. You put it in your manifesto. You told Malaysian voters that limiting PM tenure was essential to prevent another Mahathir-style power entrenchment. And when the moment came — when 148 votes were all you needed from a 222-seat house where you command a comfortable majority — you couldn’t even get your own people to walk into the chamber. Six of your eight absent MPs haven’t even explained why they weren’t there. Fahmi says “no action has been taken.” Of course it hasn’t. Because in PH, reform promises are for campaigns, not for parliament. You had one job. You failed. And every Malaysian who believed your reformasi rhetoric is now paying the price for your incompetence.
