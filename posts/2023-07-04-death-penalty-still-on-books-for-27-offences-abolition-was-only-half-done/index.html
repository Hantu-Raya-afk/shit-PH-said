Death Penalty Still on Books for 27 Offences — 'Abolition' Was Only Half-Done

Hantu Raya

July 4, 2023

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim Administration

Category: Social

Date: 2023-07-04

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 4 July 2023, PH trumpeted the “abolition” of the mandatory death penalty — replacing it with discretionary sentencing for capital offences. However, the death penalty itself remains on the books for 27 offences, including drug trafficking, murder, and firearms offences. Judges now have discretion to impose death or alternative sentences, but the gallows remain available. Amnesty International called for full abolition. In late 2025, Malaysia announced a “study” on full abolition to begin in January 2026 — three years after the initial reform.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: PH framed the mandatory death penalty reform as a landmark human rights achievement. In reality, it was a half-measure. Removing the word “mandatory” does not abolish the death penalty — it transfers the decision from statute to judicial discretion. Judges in Malaysia’s court system, which PH itself has criticised for political interference, now decide who lives and who dies. For a government that claims to champion human rights, maintaining the death penalty for 27 offences while announcing a “study” on abolition — three years after reform — is not progress. It’s procrastination branded as principle.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The “study” announced for January 2026 is the Malaysian government’s default delaying tactic: announce a study, form a committee, wait for the news cycle to move on, and never implement the findings. If PH believed in abolition, it would have abolished the death penalty entirely in 2023 when it had the parliamentary numbers and the public momentum. Instead, it chose the politically safe option — remove “mandatory,” keep the penalty, and claim credit for reform. Amnesty International saw through it immediately. The rakyat should too.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia has executed prisoners under every administration. Under BN, the death penalty was used primarily for drug trafficking — disproportionately affecting poor, marginalised, and foreign defendants. PH 1.0 initiated discussions on abolition but collapsed before acting. PH 2.0 had three years to complete the work. Instead, it settled for discretionary sentencing and a future “study.” The pattern is consistent: Malaysian governments acknowledge the death penalty is problematic, make incremental changes, and never finish the job.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You called it abolition. It wasn’t. The death penalty remains for 27 offences. You announced a “study” on full abolition — three years after your initial reform. If you need three years to study whether the state should kill its citizens, the answer is already no. Abolish the death penalty for all offences. Commute existing death sentences. Stop studying and start acting. Or admit that “abolition” was a press release, not a policy.
