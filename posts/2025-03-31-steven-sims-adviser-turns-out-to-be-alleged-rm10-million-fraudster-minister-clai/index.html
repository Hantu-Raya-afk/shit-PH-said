Steven Sim's 'Adviser' Turns Out to Be Alleged RM10 Million Fraudster — Minister Claims He Never Appointed Anyone

Hantu Raya

March 31, 2025

Said by: Steven Sim, Human Resources Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2025-03-31

📌 WHAT HAPPENED

In March 2025, a businessman with the title “Tan Sri” was arrested by Bukit Aman’s anti-money laundering team for allegedly defrauding the public of RM10 million. The 59-year-old, who had 30 years of experience in the oil and gas industry, was apprehended upon returning to his home at Jalan U Thant, KL, after a trip to Osaka, Japan.

A news portal then reported that the arrested businessman had been claiming to serve as an adviser to Human Resources Minister Steven Sim. Sim’s press secretary Abdul Hakim Ab Rahman quickly issued a denial, stating that the minister “has never appointed any individual as his advisor.”

The ministry directed all inquiries about positions within the minister’s office to “official channels” but offered no explanation for how an alleged fraudster came to be publicly associated with the minister.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS

This is a governance failure regardless of whether Sim actually appointed the man or not. If the man was falsely claiming to be the minister’s adviser, it raises questions about why Sim’s office didn’t know about it sooner and why action wasn’t taken before the man was arrested for a completely separate RM10 million fraud.

If the man did have some informal relationship with the minister — even without a formal appointment letter — then Sim’s blanket denial is misleading. In Malaysian politics, “advisers” often operate in grey zones without official documentation, precisely to maintain plausible deniability.

Either way, the fact that an alleged RM10 million fraudster was running around using a PH minister’s name to build credibility is a serious vetting and governance failure.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT

The speed of the denial is telling. Within hours of the news breaking, Sim’s office issued a statement saying the minister “has never appointed any individual as his advisor.” No investigation. No explanation. Just a flat denial.

But here’s what the denial doesn’t address: How did a man alleged to have defrauded the public of RM10 million come to be publicly associated with a sitting Cabinet minister? Was there any social contact? Any events attended together? Any photos? The denial addresses the formal appointment question but dodges the substantive question of the relationship.

PH ministers love to talk about transparency and accountability. Here’s a test: release a full accounting of everyone who has access to or works with the minister’s office, formally or informally. Until then, the denial is just PR crisis management.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES

Malaysia has a long tradition of politically connected fixers, “advisers,” and intermediaries who use their proximity to power to build business credibility. The Nurul Izzah “adviser” appointment controversy was a similar case of a PH figure being linked to questionable appointments. Under BN, the practice was endemic — “political secretaries” and “special officers” operated as unofficial gatekeepers with enormous informal power.

PH promised to clean this up. The fact that alleged fraudsters are still claiming ministerial connections suggests the cleanup hasn’t happened.

🎯 PH CALLOUT

You promised Kerajaan Madani would be different — no more cronyism, no more fixers, no more politically connected businessmen using ministerial names for personal gain. Then an alleged RM10 million fraudster turns out to be publicly claiming he’s your minister’s adviser, and the best you can do is a press release saying “we never appointed anyone.” That’s not accountability — that’s damage control. If you can’t even control who’s using your ministers’ names, how are you controlling who’s using their influence?
