PH's Own Term Limit Bill Falls 2 Votes Short — 32 MPs Absent, 44 Abstained, Zero Voted Against

Hantu Raya

March 3, 2026

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister / Pakatan Harapan Coalition

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-03

📌 What Happened

On March 3, 2026, Malaysia’s parliament voted on the Constitution (Amendment) Bill 2026, which would have instituted a two-term, 10-year limit for prime ministers — one of Anwar Ibrahim’s flagship reform promises since taking office in November 2022. The bill needed a two-thirds supermajority of 148 votes. It got 146. House Speaker Tan Sri Johari Abdul announced the results: 146 in favor, 32 absent, 44 abstained. Not a single MP voted against the bill. It failed because the government’s own coalition could not get its members into the chamber.

🔥 Why It Matters

This was supposed to be one of the defining reforms of the Madani government — a structural check on executive power that PH campaigned on for years while in opposition. The bill didn’t fail because of principled opposition. It failed because 76 MPs — nearly a third of the house — were either absent or couldn’t be bothered to take a position. In a government that commands a supermajority, that is not bad luck. That is a failure of whipping, leadership, and political will. It signals that PH’s reform agenda is not just stalled — it is being quietly sabotaged from within.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

Anwar Ibrahim has spent three years telling Malaysians that reforms take time, that the coalition is complex, that patience is required. He has a two-thirds majority. He had the bill drafted, tabled, and debated. And when the moment came, his own people didn’t show up. This is the same coalition that can mobilize its full bench for confidence votes and budget approvals but apparently cannot do the same for democratic reform. The message is clear: limiting prime ministerial power is not a priority for the people who benefit from unlimited prime ministerial power.

📜 Previous Cases

PH’s record on structural reform is dismal. The promised repeal of the Sedition Act never happened. The plan to separate the Attorney-General’s prosecutorial power from the PM’s appointment authority has gone nowhere. The proposal to liberalize university admissions by recognizing UEC results has been shelved. The Straits Times reported that DAP — PH’s largest party — has brought forward its national congress to July 2026 specifically to evaluate whether the pace of reforms justifies staying in the coalition. Tony Pua, DAP’s disciplinary chief, warned that “if we don’t withdraw, the party will lose all credibility and can expect a punishing outcome from the elections.”

🎯 PH Callout

You had the numbers. You had the bill. You had three years of promises behind you. And you couldn’t get 148 people into a room. Pakatan Harapan told Malaysians in 2022 that a vote for PH was a vote for reform. The term limit was Exhibit A — the proof that Malaysia Madani meant structural change, not just sloganeering. On March 3, 2026, in a chamber where not a single MP voted no, reformasi died of absenteeism. If you cannot pass your own signature reforms with your own supermajority, what exactly is the point of you?
