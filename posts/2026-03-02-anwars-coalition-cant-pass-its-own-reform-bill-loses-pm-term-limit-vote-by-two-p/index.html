Anwar's Coalition Can't Pass Its Own Reform Bill — Loses PM Term Limit Vote by Two, Promises to 'Try Again in June'

Hantu Raya

March 2, 2026

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister / Pakatan Harapan Coalition

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-02

📌 What Happened

On March 2, Anwar Ibrahim’s unity government failed to pass a constitutional amendment that would have limited the prime minister’s tenure to 10 years — a flagship reform promise from PH’s 2022 election manifesto. The bill received 146 votes, just two short of the 148 needed for a two-thirds majority. Forty-four MPs abstained and 32 were absent. Deputy Speaker Alice Lau confirmed eight government and independent MPs did not participate. The government has since pledged to reintroduce the bill in June, with coalition leaders promising their MPs will “actually attend this time.”

🔥 Why It Matters

This was supposed to be the easy win. The PM term limit was popular, symbolically loaded, and politically safe — designed to show that PH can still deliver on reform. Instead, Anwar’s coalition, which holds a supermajority in the 222-seat Dewan Rakyat, couldn’t even whip its own members into the chamber. The failure exposes a deeper rot: three years into government, PH cannot translate its parliamentary numbers into legislative outcomes. If the coalition can’t pass a bill it spent weeks championing, what hope is there for harder reforms — anti-hopping enforcement, IPCMC, procurement transparency, or judicial independence?

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

A government with a supermajority losing its own reform bill by two votes is not bad luck — it’s bad management. Either the whips failed to do their jobs, or parts of the coalition quietly sabotaged the vote. An unnamed PH lawmaker told the SCMP: “That bill should have easily passed. It feels like there was not enough effort to make sure everyone turned up.” That’s not an explanation — it’s a confession. And the government’s response? “We’ll try again in June.” That’s the reform equivalent of hitting snooze. Meanwhile, cameras caught Anwar mid-laugh after the vote was read out, chatting with his deputy as if his flagship bill hadn’t just died on the floor.

📜 Previous Cases

This is a pattern. PH promised IPCMC — shelved. Promised freedom of information — nothing. Promised anti-hopping law enforcement — selective at best. Promised political funding reform — in committee limbo. The 2022 manifesto was a 60-page wishlist; three years on, the hardest items remain untouched and even the easy ones are fumbled. The PM term limit was literally the lowest-hanging fruit — popular across party lines, personally backed by Anwar, and requiring only a supermajority PH already had on paper.

🎯 PH Callout

You told Malaysia you were different. You ran on “Reformasi.” You had the numbers, the mandate, and the public goodwill. And you couldn’t get your own MPs into the room to vote for a bill you spent weeks selling to the country. If you can’t pass popular, symbolic reforms with a supermajority, what exactly are you using that majority for? “We’ll try again in June” is not a reform agenda — it’s an admission that you don’t have control of your own coalition.
