Deputy Economy Minister Says Growth Steady at 4-4.5% — Amid a Regional War and Global Volatility

Hantu Raya

March 9, 2026

Said by: Mohd Shahar Abdullah, Deputy Economy Minister

Category: Economy

Date: 2026-03-09

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 9 March 2026, Deputy Economy Minister Mohd Shahar Abdullah projected 4–4.5% GDP growth for Malaysia at the Asean Banking and Finance Summit, citing domestic consumption, infrastructure investment, the digital economy, and Visit Malaysia 2026. He made this statement at the very summit whose chairman, Tan Sri Majid Khan, warned that geopolitical tensions are “repricing risk across our entire region.” The US-Iran conflict was escalating, oil prices were spiking due to Strait of Hormuz disruptions, and GPS jamming was affecting shipping across the Gulf.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: A deputy minister projecting steady growth at a summit convened to discuss regional instability suggests a dangerous disconnect between political messaging and economic reality. The growth drivers cited — tourism, digital economy, domestic consumption — are all vulnerable to the very geopolitical risks flagged at the same event. Oil price spikes may temporarily boost petroleum revenue, but they raise input costs across the economy and threaten the government’s already fragile fuel subsidy position. When your own summit chairman is sounding the alarm, doubling down on optimism reads less like confidence and more like denial.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Malaysia’s economy is heavily trade-dependent and exposed to every global shock the deputy minister chose to ignore. Projecting 4-4.5% growth while a regional war threatens shipping lanes, oil markets, and investor confidence is not leadership — it’s PR. The government has repeatedly been caught off-guard by external shocks (COVID, supply chain disruptions, commodity swings) because it prioritises messaging over contingency planning. Telling bankers “everything is fine” while the building shakes is not reassuring — it’s alarming.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysian governments have a long history of projecting optimism that doesn’t survive contact with reality. In 2020, the government insisted the economy was “resilient” weeks before COVID lockdowns crashed GDP. In 2023, growth projections were revised downward multiple times. The pattern: announce best-case projections, hope the world cooperates, scramble when it doesn’t. PH inherited this habit from BN and has done nothing to change it.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: If you’re going to host a summit about regional risk, don’t send your deputy minister to say everything is fine. Either acknowledge the risks and present contingency plans, or stop pretending your growth projections are anything more than political targets. Malaysians deserve honest economic forecasting, not election-season optimism disguised as policy analysis.
