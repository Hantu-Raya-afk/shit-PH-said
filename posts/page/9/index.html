Will 2026 Be the Year PH Fulfils Its Reform Promises? — FMT Asks, Again

Said by: FMT editorial

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-01-10

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 10 January 2026, FMT published an opinion piece titled “Will 2026 be the year PH fulfils its reform promises?” — the same question asked in January 2024 and January 2025. On 5 January, Anwar gave a New Year address promising that reform pledges would “materialise this year,” announcing the AG-PP separation bill would be “the first bill to be tabled.” The bill was indeed tabled — then immediately referred to a parliamentary committee for three more months of “review.” This promise has been pending since GE14 in 2018, now eight years unfulfilled. The reform scorecard after three full years: AG-PP separation sent to committee, UUCA “no plans” to abolish, Sedition Act used 90+ times instead of repealed, ICCPR unratified, toll abolition replaced by RM591M freezes, PM term limit bill failed by 2 votes, IPCMC not established, Freedom of Information Act not tabled, local government elections not restored.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: When the media has to ask every January whether this will finally be the year of reform, the answer is already implied. The question itself is the indictment. PH has had three full years with a commanding parliamentary majority. Every unfulfilled promise is a choice — a deliberate decision to prioritise political survival over the mandate that put them in power. Three years is more than half a parliamentary term. If reform was coming, it would have started. At some point, “this year” becomes “never.”

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The pattern is clear: announce the reform in a New Year speech, table it months later, refer it to a committee, delay it past the next budget cycle, and repeat. This is not incrementalism — it is institutional procrastination designed to maintain the illusion of reform without the substance. The Sedition Act is not only not abolished but used more aggressively than under Najib — the promise has not been delayed, it has been reversed. The PM term limit bill failed because Anwar’s own coalition didn’t show up to vote. You have a supermajority and still couldn’t limit your own power. Hope is not a governance strategy, and annual promises of “this year” are not reform.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: PH made these same promises in GE14 (2018) and had 22 months in government. They made them again in GE15 (2022). After a combined 5+ years in power across two stints, the list remains almost entirely unfulfilled. The columnist’s hopefulness reads as tragic rather than optimistic — Malaysians who marched in Bersih rallies, who donated to PKR, who voted for PH twice, are running out of patience and excuses. The manifesto has become a marketing document, not a governance contract.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Three years. Supermajority. Not a single major reform law passed. The AG-PP bill is in committee — again. UUCA stands. The Sedition Act stands. ICCPR is unsigned. Tolls are still collected. Local elections are still suspended. You have the numbers to pass every single one of these reforms tomorrow. You won’t — because reform would constrain the power you now enjoy. Stop blaming process and committees. You ARE the process. Pass the laws or admit you never intended to.

Zahid Hamidi's 47 Corruption Charges NFA'd — 'Insufficient Evidence' After 7 Years

Said by: Attorney-General’s Chambers

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-01-08

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 8 January 2026, the Attorney-General’s Chambers (AGC) ended all 47 criminal charges against Deputy Prime Minister Ahmad Zahid Hamidi, citing “insufficient evidence.” The charges — filed in 2018 after UMNO lost power — involved RM31 million from Yayasan Akalbudi, a charitable foundation Zahid controlled. The charges included criminal breach of trust, corruption, and money laundering. Reuters reported the AGC issued a discharge not amounting to an acquittal (DNAA) for some charges and a full acquittal for others. The Malaysian Bar objected. CSIS published an analysis titled “Death of Reformasi.” The Diplomat called it the dropping of charges against “a key Anwar ally.”

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Zahid Hamidi is not just any politician — he is the Deputy Prime Minister and UMNO President. UMNO’s support is what keeps the unity government alive. Dropping 47 charges against the DPM — the man whose party Anwar depends on for his parliamentary majority — is the most politically convenient prosecution outcome imaginable. The timing is not coincidental: the charges were dropped after Zahid delivered UMNO’s support for Anwar’s coalition. The AGC cited “insufficient evidence” after seven years of prosecution — but the evidence was sufficient to file 47 charges in the first place. What changed was not the evidence. What changed was the politics.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Anwar’s defence — “I don’t interfere in AG decisions” — is technically plausible and substantively absurd. The AG serves at the pleasure of the PM. Anwar appointed this AG. The AG dropped charges against Anwar’s most important coalition ally. The system is designed so that the PM doesn’t need to pick up the phone — the incentives are aligned without explicit instruction. This is the same structural problem Anwar campaigned on: the AG-PP separation was supposed to prevent exactly this scenario. Three years later, the AG and PP are still the same person, and the DPM’s 47 charges vanish. The reform that would have prevented this outcome is the reform Anwar refuses to implement.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia has a long history of politically convenient prosecution outcomes. Under Mahathir, Anwar himself was prosecuted on charges widely seen as politically motivated. Under Najib, opposition figures were charged while 1MDB principals walked free. Under Anwar, the pattern has simply reversed: coalition allies get NFA’d while the AG-PP separation that would depoliticise prosecution remains permanently “in committee.” CSIS called this the “death of Reformasi” — the moment the reform movement’s central promise (accountability for all) was definitively betrayed.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: 47 charges. RM31 million. Seven years of prosecution. Dropped for “insufficient evidence” — for the man whose party keeps you in power. If the evidence was insufficient, why were 47 charges filed? If the AG is truly independent, why does every “independent” AG decision happen to benefit your coalition? Separate the AG and PP. Now. Not in committee. Not “this year.” Now. Because as long as the AG serves at the PM’s pleasure, every dropped charge against a coalition ally looks like a political favour — and the “Reformasi” brand is worth less than the paper the NFA was printed on.

Anwar's Signature Move: Whataboutism — 'But BN Did It Too' Is Not a Reform Agenda

Said by: Parliamentary Hansard / Media Analysis

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-01-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: A clear rhetorical pattern has emerged in Anwar Ibrahim’s parliamentary and public responses to criticism: deflect to what previous governments or the opposition did. When challenged on support letters for government tenders, he pointed to Kelantan. When challenged on lobbying, he brought up PKR’s past. When challenged on reform failures, he invokes the “mess we inherited.” This is systematic whataboutism deployed as governance strategy. The Star reported Anwar “calls out opposition’s hypocrisy over support letters” — turning a question about his own government’s conduct into an attack on the opposition. CSIS, in its “Death of Reformasi” analysis, noted how Anwar consistently deflects accountability by reframing criticism as opposition bad faith.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Whataboutism is the oldest trick in the political playbook — and it’s a dead giveaway that a leader has no substantive defence. When your answer to “why haven’t you reformed X?” is “well, BN also didn’t reform X,” you’re admitting you’re no better than the government you replaced. The entire purpose of changing governments is to get DIFFERENT outcomes. Voters didn’t elect PH to be equal to BN. They elected PH to be better. Every time Anwar deflects to BN or PN, he is implicitly admitting that his government hasn’t met its own standards.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: “They did it too” isn’t accountability — it’s collective guilt used as a shield. A PM who spends parliamentary time attacking the opposition’s record instead of defending his own is a PM without a record worth defending. When asked about support letters, the correct answer is: “Here is our procurement reform policy.” When asked about the Sedition Act, the correct answer is: “Here is our timeline for repeal.” Instead, Anwar’s answer is always: “But look at what they did.” This is not governance. It is debate-team deflection from a career politician who has mastered the art of answering a different question from the one asked.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Najib used the same tactic — when confronted about 1MDB, he pointed to Mahathir-era losses. Mahathir deflected to BN’s corruption when PH 1.0 failed to deliver. It’s a Malaysian political tradition: blame your predecessors forever, and never answer for your own record. Anwar was supposed to break this cycle. Instead, he has perfected it — adding rhetorical sophistication to the same underlying dodge. The whataboutism isn’t accidental; it is the centrepiece of PH’s parliamentary communication strategy.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Stop telling us what BN did. We KNOW what BN did — that’s why we voted them out. Start telling us what YOU’VE done. And when the answer is “the same things BN did” — support letters, political appointments, selective prosecution, stalled reforms — stop pretending you’re a reform government. You’re not. You’re BN with better PR and a more eloquent PM who uses five-dollar words to dodge ten-cent questions.

Contract Doctors: 33,000 STILL Waiting for Permanent Posts — Promise Made in 2020, Still Broken in 2026

Said by: Malaysian Medical Association / Contract Doctors

Category: Health

Date: 2026-01-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: As of January 2026, approximately 33,000 contract doctors in Malaysia’s public healthcare system continue to work on temporary contracts without job security, pension benefits, or career advancement pathways available to permanent medical officers. The contract system was introduced as a “temporary” measure to absorb a surge of medical graduates. Six years later, it is still “temporary.” Contract doctors do the same work as permanent doctors — they see the same patients, work the same 84-hour weeks, handle the same emergencies — but without permanent employment status. CNA reported on the “doctor shortage, low pay, 84-hour working week” crisis. P4H Network described Malaysia’s healthcare system as a “ticking bomb.”

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: A two-tier medical workforce where thousands of doctors live with constant job insecurity is unsustainable. Many leave public service entirely — heading to Singapore, Australia, or the private sector — contributing to brain drain and worsening the doctor shortage. Malaysia is already short 11,000 specialists. Junior doctors work 84-hour weeks. Public hospitals are overwhelmed as even T20 families switch from private healthcare due to unaffordable costs. The contract system is not just unfair to doctors — it is destroying the healthcare system from within. Every contract doctor who emigrates takes decades of medical training and public investment with them.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Both BN and PH promised to resolve the contract doctor issue. In 2021, contract doctors staged the rare “Black Monday” protest — doctors protesting should alarm any government, because doctors don’t protest unless things are desperate. PH politicised the issue, attacking the PN government for mistreating healthcare workers. Then PH took power and continued mistreating healthcare workers. Three years into PH 2.0, the majority are still on contracts. The government finds RM2.6 billion for JAKIM’s religious affairs budget and RM85.5 million for J-Kom propaganda — but can’t find permanent positions for 33,000 doctors keeping the public health system alive. The priorities are explicit.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: The contract doctor crisis has been escalating since 2016 when the system was introduced. Under Muhyiddin’s PN government, the “Black Monday” protest drew national attention. PH 1.0 (2018-2020) did not resolve it. PH 2.0 promised permanent absorption — then absorbed a fraction. The pattern across administrations is identical: promise, delay, absorb a small batch for headlines, leave the rest on contracts. Meanwhile, Malaysia’s healthcare spending remains at 2.83% of GDP — far below the WHO recommendation and below regional peers like Thailand (3.8%) and Vietnam (3.0%).

🎯 PH CALLOUT: 33,000 doctors are keeping your hospitals running on temporary contracts while you fill GLC boards with political appointees on permanent salaries. You find RM2.6B for JAKIM and hundreds of millions for highway toll freezes — but can’t find positions for the doctors who save Malaysian lives every day. Every contract doctor who leaves for Singapore takes decades of training and public money with them. You’re not just breaking a promise — you’re dismantling the healthcare system one departure at a time. Absorb all 33,000 or explain to the rakyat why religious bureaucracy matters more than the people keeping them alive.

No GE16 Accountability Framework — PH Has No System for Voters to Track Manifesto Progress

Said by: Governance Reform Advocates

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-01-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: Unlike some democracies where government manifesto progress is publicly tracked, PH has established no formal system for voters to monitor delivery against the approximately 60 promises made in GE15. There is no public dashboard, no annual manifesto progress report, and no structured mechanism for civil society to hold the government accountable to its specific commitments. Malaysiakini created an independent manifesto tracker because the government wouldn’t. The Straits Times tracked PH 1.0’s progress and found most promises “still had some way to go” after just one year.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Democracy functions on accountability. When voters can’t track promise delivery, politicians face no consequences for breaking commitments. The manifesto becomes a marketing document rather than a governance contract. Without structured tracking, broken promises disappear into the news cycle, and by GE16, PH can make the SAME promises again — because nobody has a clear record of what was delivered and what wasn’t. The absence of a tracking system is not an oversight — it is a strategy.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Malaysiakini created an independent manifesto tracker because the government wouldn’t. Civil society organisations track promises because PH won’t track itself. A government confident in its delivery would WELCOME transparency — publishing regular progress reports, inviting scrutiny, and holding itself accountable. PH’s silence on manifesto tracking tells you everything: they know the scorecard is damning, so they ensure no official scorecard exists. When independent trackers show the majority of promises unfulfilled after three years, the government’s response is not to accelerate delivery but to ignore the trackers.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: In 2019, the Straits Times tracked PH 1.0’s manifesto progress and found most promises “still had some way to go” after just one year. Independent tracking of PH 2.0 tells a similar story — AG-PP separation pending, UUCA intact, Sedition Act expanded, ICCPR unsigned, tolls still collected, local elections still suspended. The pattern: big promises, no tracking, no accountability, then new promises at the next election. Malaysian democracy has become a manifesto laundering scheme — the same commitments recycled every five years with no mechanism to hold anyone accountable for the previous round of failures.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Publish a manifesto progress report. Go through every one of your 60 promises and tell Malaysians where each one stands — delivered, in progress, delayed, or abandoned. You won’t — because the report would be a catalogue of broken commitments. A government that won’t track its own promises has no intention of keeping them. Voters deserve better than a quadrennial con job where the same promises are made, broken, and made again with nobody keeping score.

Social Media Platforms Forcibly 'Deemed Licensed' — MCMC Tightens Grip on Internet

Said by: Fahmi Fadzil, Communications Minister

Category: Social

Date: 2026-01-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: From 1 January 2026, social media and instant messaging platforms with 8 million or more Malaysian users were automatically “deemed licensed” under MCMC’s regulatory framework — whether they applied for a licence or not. This means platforms like Meta (Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp), TikTok, Telegram, and X are now subject to Malaysian government licensing requirements, content takedown obligations, and penalties for non-compliance. Meta publicly warned the scheme “lacks clarity and threatens innovation.” Soyacincau reported that MCMC’s approach effectively bypassed the normal licensing application process by unilaterally deeming platforms as licensed. Reuters reported Meta’s concerns about the regulatory framework’s vagueness.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Forced licensing gives the Malaysian government regulatory leverage over every major platform used by Malaysians. Licensed entities must comply with government directives — including content removal requests, user data obligations, and operational requirements set by MCMC. This creates a legal framework where the government can threaten to revoke or suspend licences if platforms don’t comply with takedown orders. The scheme bypassed normal regulatory processes: platforms were not asked whether they wanted licences — they were told they had them, along with all the obligations that come with them.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: PH in opposition criticised the BN government for using MCMC to control online discourse. In government, PH has built the most comprehensive internet regulation framework in Malaysian history: the Online Safety Act with RM10M penalties, the reinstated CMA “grossly offensive” provision, and now forced licensing of every major platform. The “child safety” framing is the Trojan horse — the infrastructure being built gives the government tools to regulate ANY content, not just harmful content. Who decides what content violates licensing conditions? MCMC, which answers to Communications Minister Fahmi Fadzil, who answers to Anwar. The same minister who says “free speech doesn’t mean freedom to offend” now has licensing power over every platform Malaysians use.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Under BN, MCMC was used to block websites critical of the government — most notably during the 1MDB scandal when The Sarawak Report and other outlets were blocked. Under PH, the tools are more sophisticated but the intent is the same: control the information environment. The deemed licensing approach follows Singapore’s model, where platform licensing has been used to compel content removal on politically sensitive topics. Malaysia is building the same infrastructure while claiming it’s about protecting children.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You forced licensing on every major platform without asking them, without clear rules, and without independent oversight. Meta — one of the largest tech companies on earth — publicly warned your approach “lacks clarity and threatens innovation.” When the platforms you’re regulating tell you your regulation is unclear, that’s not a feature — it’s a design choice. Vague rules give you maximum discretion. Maximum discretion means maximum censorship potential. If this is really about child safety, publish clear, specific, enforceable rules with independent oversight. If you can’t do that, admit this is about control.

TikTok Generation Lost — Malaysian Youth Screen Time Among Highest in the World While Government Plans to Ban Under-16s

Said by: MCMC / Online Safety Act

Category: Social

Date: 2026-01-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: Malaysian youth have among the highest screen times and social media usage rates in the world. The government’s response was the Online Safety Act, which bans social media use for under-16s from 2026. The law places enforcement responsibility on platforms, with penalties up to RM10 million for non-compliance. However, implementation details remain vague — no age verification infrastructure exists, no technical framework has been announced, and enforcement mechanisms are untested. Freedom House’s 2024 report flagged Malaysia’s declining internet freedom trajectory under PH.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Excessive social media use among youth is linked to declining mental health, cyberbullying, exposure to inappropriate content, and reduced academic performance. Malaysian PISA scores correlate with screen time data — students spending more hours on devices perform worse academically. The K-pods drug crisis is also facilitated through social media distribution networks. But the issue isn’t just screen time — it’s that the government has failed to provide alternatives: a functional education system, employment prospects, mental health support, or safe public spaces for young people.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The under-16 social media ban is performative legislation — it looks tough but is virtually unenforceable. How do you verify age for social media accounts? VPNs are trivially easy to use. Parental controls are easily circumvented by tech-savvy children. Without robust age verification infrastructure — which doesn’t exist and would raise massive privacy concerns — the law is decoration. Australia passed similar legislation and immediately faced implementation challenges. The EU focused on platform accountability rather than age bans. Malaysia chose the most attention-grabbing but least effective approach. More importantly, the same government building age-verification infrastructure is also forcibly licensing social media platforms and expanding MCMC’s censorship powers — tools that can easily be repurposed for broader internet control.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Previous MCMC “enforcement” of online content has been selective — targeting political critics while struggling to address scams, fraud, and actual harmful content. The government’s track record on tech regulation is one of overreach on speech and underperformance on safety. MCMC can find and arrest social media users who mock politicians within hours, but can’t shut down scam call centres operating from Malaysian soil for years. The priorities tell you everything about what this legislation is really for.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You’re banning TikTok for kids while your education system can’t teach them math. You’re “protecting” them online while bullying cases surge 17% in schools. You’re regulating social media while 10,160 students didn’t even show up for SPM. Maybe address the real-world failures that drive youth to screens in the first place: give them a functional education system, employment prospects, and mental health support. A TikTok ban without systemic reform is a headline, not a policy.

DPM Ahmad Zahid Hamidi — who had 47 corruption charges dropped in an NFA decision that shocked the legal community — now effectively holds veto power over government policy. UMNO’s support is essential for PH’s parliamentary majority, and UMNO knows it. Anwar’s defenders argue coalition politics requires compromise. But compromising on corruption and accountability isn’t pragmatism — it’s capitulation. The unity government isn’t a grand coalition; it’s UMNO’s rehabilitation programme funded by PH’s credibility. Anwar gets to be PM. Under Muhyiddin’s Perikatan Nasional, the Sheraton Move coalition was held together by ministerial appointments and GLC posts. Anwar’s unity government follows the same logic — the coalition survives not because it governs well, but because every partner gets something: UMNO gets legal protection, GPS gets Sabah/Sarawak autonomy rhetoric, DAP gets a seat at the table (increasingly a smaller one). The formula is identical across administrations. Only the brand changes.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You told voters UMNO was the enemy. Then you made UMNO your deputy. You told voters corruption must end. Then you dropped Zahid’s charges. You told voters Najib must pay. Then you halved his sentence. At what point does “coalition compromise” become “everything we fought against”? You passed that point when Zahid’s 47 charges were NFA’d. Every day you govern with UMNO holding veto power over reform is another day Reformasi dies — not with a bang, but with a coalition meeting.

Wages Haven't Kept Up with Costs — Real Purchasing Power Has DECLINED Under PH

Said by: DOSM / BNM / Consumer Groups

Category: Economy

Date: 2026-01-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: While nominal wages have increased slightly under PH — minimum wage raised to RM1,500, modest salary increments across sectors — the cost of living has risen faster. Diesel is up 56%. SST was expanded to cover more essentials. Food prices are the highest in Southeast Asia at $1,940 per person per year according to USDA data. Electricity costs are up. Property prices are up. The result: real purchasing power — what your ringgit actually buys — has declined for most Malaysians. A 2025 consumer survey found 77% of Malaysians say costs have risen significantly, while official inflation claims just 1.3%.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Purchasing power is the truest measure of economic wellbeing. GDP growth, investment figures, and export numbers are abstractions. What matters to a family is whether they can afford the same basket of groceries this month as last month. Under PH, the answer is increasingly no. When 71.6% of household income goes to daily necessities, disposable income for savings, education, and emergencies shrinks to almost nothing. Families are one medical emergency away from crisis. The government celebrates macro numbers while the rakyat counts sen at the checkout counter.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government points to headline inflation of around 1.3% and claims prices are “stable.” But official CPI calculations underweight items that have risen most sharply — food, fuel, services — and don’t capture the “felt” inflation that families experience. When 77% of Malaysians say costs have risen significantly but official inflation says 1.3%, somebody is lying — and it’s not the 77%. Food away from home inflation runs at 4.3-4.7%, far exceeding the headline rate. The government is using statistical methodology to gaslight the population about their own lived experience. Meanwhile, PH abolished GST (which zero-rated essentials) and replaced it with SST, which taxes MORE daily items. Under BN, diesel was RM2.15. Under PH, it’s RM3.35.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Under BN’s GST system, essentials were zero-rated — consumers paid no tax on basic food, domestic electricity, and public transport. PH abolished GST as a populist move and replaced it with SST, which is less efficient and taxes more daily items. The uncomfortable truth: by many daily-life cost measures, BN’s baseline was better than PH’s performance. Every Malaysian government has faced cost-of-living complaints, but PH uniquely promised to make life cheaper — then made it more expensive through diesel float, SST expansion, and subsidy rationalisation without adequate targeted relief.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You promised to make life better for ordinary Malaysians. Life is MORE expensive. Diesel costs MORE. Food costs MORE. Services cost MORE through expanded SST. Electricity costs MORE. And wages haven’t kept up. Every Malaysian family knows this — they see it every time they buy groceries, fill their tank, or pay their bills. Your 1.3% inflation statistic is an insult to the 77% of Malaysians who know their money buys less than it did three years ago. Stop hiding behind macro numbers and address the lived reality of the rakyat.

Public Transport 'Functional But Fragile' — One Disruption and Everyone Drives

Said by: Khazanah Research Institute (KRI)

Category: Economy

Date: 2025-12-31

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: A Khazanah Research Institute (KRI) study published in late December 2025 found that Greater KL’s public transport system is “functional but fragile” — minor disruptions cause commuters to abandon it entirely for private vehicles. KRI concluded that rail was “never meant to fix Malaysia’s traffic problem.” The study found that fuel remains artificially cheap, parking is abundant and affordable, walking environments are fragmented and hostile, and last-mile connectivity is inadequate. FMT’s analysis argued it is “an unfinished system, not infrastructure, that fails Malaysian commuters.” Yet the government continues announcing mega rail projects — MRT3, LRT3, ECRL — as if building more tracks alone will solve mobility.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Successive governments, including PH, keep announcing multi-billion ringgit rail projects as if infrastructure alone solves mobility. KRI’s own study — funded by the government’s own sovereign wealth fund — says it doesn’t. Without simultaneously reforming urban planning, parking policy, fuel pricing, and last-mile connectivity, every new rail line is just an expensive band-aid on a systemic failure. Billions are spent on tracks while the system remains one disruption away from collapse. Commuters who try public transport once and get stranded never come back.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government subsidises fuel (keeping driving artificially cheap), allows abundant free parking in urban areas (removing the cost incentive to use public transport), neglects pedestrian infrastructure (making it dangerous to walk to stations), and fails to invest in last-mile solutions (leaving commuters stranded between stations and destinations). Then it announces another RM50 billion rail project and claims it is “solving traffic.” This is not a transport policy — it is an infrastructure spending programme that benefits construction companies and politicians who cut ribbons, not commuters who need to get to work. LRT3 has been delayed repeatedly. MRT3 costs keep escalating. The ECRL’s ridership projections are fantasy. KRI told the government the approach doesn’t work, and the government responded by announcing more of the same.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia has been building rail infrastructure for decades — KTM Komuter, LRT, Monorail, MRT1, MRT2 — yet car ownership rates keep climbing and traffic congestion keeps worsening. Each new project is announced with the same promise: this one will finally solve traffic. None has. The reason is structural: Malaysia’s urban planning is car-centric by design. Without changing the design — restricting parking, raising fuel costs, improving walkability, building bus networks — adding rail lines is like building a bigger bucket while the tap keeps running.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Your own government-funded think tank says rail alone doesn’t solve traffic. Yet your transport policy is: build more rail. Reform parking policy. Reform fuel pricing. Fund bus networks. Fix pedestrian infrastructure. These are the boring, politically difficult changes that would actually make public transport work. But they don’t generate ribbon-cutting photo ops, so they don’t happen. Stop building expensive monuments to political vanity and start building a transport SYSTEM.

Sources: Malay Mail | FMT
