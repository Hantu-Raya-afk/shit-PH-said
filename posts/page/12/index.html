PH Wins 1 Out of 20 Seats in Sabah — Anwar Says Defeat Won't Stop Delivery

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2025-11-30

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 30 November 2025, Pakatan Harapan was demolished in the Sabah state election. The coalition won just 1 out of 20 seats it contested — down from 7 previously. That is a 95% loss rate. Sabah-based parties dominated, driven by anti-federal sentiment and demands for greater state autonomy — particularly after a High Court ruling supporting Sabah’s claim to 40% of federal revenue. Political analyst Lee Kuok Tiung said plainly: “The Sabah election outcome shows Anwar’s coalition lost badly.” Anwar’s response: the defeat “won’t stop delivery” of pledges to Sabah.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: This was the first electoral test ahead of GE16 — and PH failed it catastrophically. Sabahans didn’t just reject PH — they rejected the entire idea of being governed by Peninsular-based parties. The results exposed a fundamental disconnect: Putrajaya’s priorities do not resonate in East Malaysia. Anwar appealed the High Court ruling on Sabah’s revenue share, directly antagonising the voters he needed. When you fight a state’s legal claim to its own resources and then ask the same state to vote for you, the outcome is predictable.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Anwar’s response — “defeat won’t stop delivery” — is the political equivalent of losing a match 19-1 and insisting the training programme is working. If PH cannot win seats in Sabah, it cannot claim a mandate to govern Sabah. The promise to “deliver” to a state that overwhelmingly rejected you is not reassurance — it’s tone-deafness. Sabahans told PH exactly what they thought. PH’s response was to ignore the message and repeat the same platitudes. That’s not resilience; it’s denial.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: PH’s East Malaysia problem is not new. In GE15, PH won minimal seats in Sabah and Sarawak, relying on GPS and GRS for coalition numbers. The pattern is consistent: Peninsular parties treat East Malaysia as a vote bank rather than an equal partner. BN did this for decades. PH promised to be different — the Malaysia Agreement 1963 review, devolution of powers, revenue sharing. Three years later, Sabah got a court ruling in its favour and PH appealed it. The lesson from every Sabah election is the same: respect state autonomy or lose.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You won 1 out of 20 seats. One. Sabahans gave you the clearest possible message: you do not represent them. Instead of listening, you said “defeat won’t stop delivery.” Drop the appeal against Sabah’s revenue share ruling. Implement MA63 commitments. Devolve real power to East Malaysian states. Or keep winning 1 out of 20 and pretending it’s fine — because GE16 will deliver the same verdict if nothing changes.

Amirudin Rolls Out Flood Recovery Plan — After Selangor's Worst Floods Under His Watch

Said by: Amirudin Shari, Selangor Menteri Besar

Category: Social

Date: 2025-11-29

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 29 November 2025, Amirudin rolled out a ‘major mobilisation’ for post-flood recovery as evacuee numbers surged across Selangor. Water from northern Selangor flooded into Kuala Langat and Sepang. JPS installed four pumps in Kuala Selangor with six more planned. A police officer went missing after a car skidded into a storm drain near KLIA during the floods. The Met Department withdrew severe rain warnings, but damage was extensive.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Selangor experiences catastrophic flooding almost every year. The December 2021 floods killed dozens and displaced hundreds of thousands. Amirudin promised improved mitigation — RM billions in flood infrastructure, better early warning systems, and drainage upgrades. Four years later, evacuees are still being mobilised, pumps are still being installed reactively, and people are still losing their lives. The flood response is reactive, not preventive.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Post-flood recovery is not flood management — it’s disaster response to a management failure. Installing pumps after the water arrives is not mitigation. The question Amirudin never answers: where are the flood retention ponds, upgraded drainage systems, and development controls that would prevent flooding in the first place? Selangor continues to approve hillside and lowland development that worsens flood risk. The state profits from development approvals, then spends public money on cleanup.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: The December 2021 Shah Alam floods, the 2022 Taman Sri Muda disaster, the 2023 Klang floods — Selangor floods are now annual events. Each time, Amirudin announces mobilisation, recovery plans, and future improvements. Each year, the same areas flood again. The definition of insanity applies.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Stop announcing reactive flood responses as if they’re achievements. A pump installed after the flood is a failure, not a success. Publish Selangor’s flood mitigation capital expenditure vs. development approval revenue for the last 7 years. Show the rakyat whether the state is spending more on preventing floods or profiting from the development that causes them.

Perlis Floods 2025 — 34,000 Evacuated, New Mother Forced to Flee Weeks After Birth

Said by: Government / NADMA

Category: Other

Date: 2025-11-25

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: In late November 2025, severe flooding across northern Malaysia — particularly in Perlis, Kedah, and Kelantan — displaced over 34,000 people. Reuters described scenes where floodwaters turned entire communities into what looked “like an ocean.” Among those evacuated was a new mother forced to flee just weeks after giving birth. A police officer went missing after a vehicle skidded into a storm drain near KLIA. The government announced a Climate Resilience Plan in response, and NADMA coordinated relief operations across affected states.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Malaysia floods catastrophically every year during the monsoon season. Every year, the government announces recovery plans, mobilises resources, and promises better mitigation. Every year, the same communities are displaced again. The 2025 Perlis floods — 34,000 evacuated — came just three years after the 2021 floods that killed dozens and displaced hundreds of thousands. The human cost is real and recurring: a mother with a newborn fleeing her home is not a statistic — it’s a governance failure measured in human suffering.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government’s Climate Resilience Plan was announced after the floods hit — not before. That’s not resilience; that’s reaction. Resilience means infrastructure that prevents flooding: retention ponds, upgraded drainage, enforced development controls, and early warning systems that actually work. Instead, Malaysia spends billions on flood relief every year while continuing to approve development in flood-prone areas, deforest catchment zones, and under-invest in drainage infrastructure. The cycle is predictable because the government chooses not to break it.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: The December 2021 floods killed over 50 people and displaced 400,000. The 2022 and 2023 monsoon seasons brought more flooding to the same areas. Selangor, Pahang, Kelantan, and Perlis are perennial flood zones — and the government knows exactly which areas will flood every year. After the 2021 disaster, Anwar (then opposition) criticised the Ismail Sabri government for reactive flood management. Now in government, PH’s approach is identical: mobilise after the water rises, announce plans after the damage is done, and promise improvements that never materialise before the next monsoon.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You criticised BN and PN for reactive flood management. Three years later, your flood response is the same: react, mobilise, promise, repeat. Publish a national flood mitigation capital expenditure report showing what has actually been built since GE15. Enforce development controls in flood-prone zones. Fund drainage infrastructure before the monsoon, not after. A new mother fleeing her home with a newborn weeks after birth is not an act of God — it’s a policy failure. Own it.

Social Media Ban for Under-16s — Enforcement Plan: TBD

Said by: Fahmi Fadzil, Communications Minister

Category: Social

Date: 2025-11-24

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: Communications Minister Fahmi Fadzil announced in November 2025 that Malaysia would ban social media access for children under 16, following Australia’s lead. The ban was set for implementation in 2026. However, the NYT noted the announcement was “light on details” — no age verification infrastructure exists, no enforcement mechanism was outlined, and no timeline for implementation was provided. The government framed it as protecting children from online harms, but offered no technical plan for making it work.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: A social media ban for under-16s sounds decisive. But without enforcement infrastructure, it’s a headline, not a policy. Age verification online is technically difficult — even Australia, which passed its ban first, has struggled with implementation. Malaysia has neither the digital infrastructure, the regulatory capacity, nor the tech industry cooperation to enforce such a ban. Meanwhile, the announcement distracts from the government’s own record of using social media regulations to suppress dissent — including the arrest of FMT journalist Rex Tan and the expansion of MCMC’s content moderation powers.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government that can’t enforce basic traffic laws, stop illegal waste dumping, or verify the integrity of its own procurement processes is going to verify the age of every social media user in Malaysia? This is performative governance at its worst. Fahmi announced a ban with no enforcement plan — because the plan doesn’t exist. It cannot exist without massive investment in digital ID infrastructure, cooperation from global tech platforms, and privacy safeguards that Malaysia has never implemented. The announcement was designed to generate a positive news cycle, not to protect children.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia has a long history of announcing tech regulations without enforcement mechanisms. The Anti-Fake News Act was passed and repealed. MCMC’s licensing regime for social media platforms was announced with fanfare but implementation details remain vague. The Online Safety Bill has been in discussion for years. Each announcement generates headlines; none generates meaningful change. Meanwhile, Malaysia’s youth screen time is among the highest in the world, and the government’s response is a ban it cannot enforce rather than investment in digital literacy it could actually deliver.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You announced a ban with no enforcement plan. That’s not policy — it’s a press release. If you’re serious about protecting children online, invest in digital literacy programmes in schools. Fund parental education on screen time management. Build the age verification infrastructure before announcing the ban, not after. Or admit that this was a headline-grabbing announcement designed to match Australia’s news cycle, with no intention of following through.

Penang PTMP Procurement: No Open Tenders, Just 'Request for Proposals' for Billions in Contracts

Said by: Chow Kon Yeow / DAP Penang / Civil Society Critics

Category: Economy

Date: 2025-11-21

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: Multiple reports, including from FMT and the academic community, documented that DAP’s Penang Transport Master Plan (PTMP) relied on ‘request-for-proposal’ (RFP) procurement mechanisms rather than open competitive tenders for billions of ringgit in infrastructure contracts. Civil society groups criticised this as opaque and developer-friendly. The PTMP includes the undersea tunnel (now under corruption trial), the Mutiara LRT, and various road projects — all procured without the standard open tender process that PH demands from the federal government.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Open competitive tendering is the gold standard for public procurement — it ensures the best price, prevents corruption, and allows public scrutiny. DAP demands open tenders from the federal government for every contract above RM50 million. But in Penang, where DAP has total control, billions in infrastructure contracts were awarded through RFP mechanisms that allow the state to pre-select bidders. The hypocrisy is structural, not incidental.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: DAP’s position on procurement is: open tenders for everyone else, RFPs for us. The party that lectured BN for decades about procurement transparency adopted the same opaque mechanisms when it controlled the purse strings. The undersea tunnel — the PTMP’s flagship project — is now a corruption case. MACC witnesses described it as a ’land scam.’ These are the outcomes of opaque procurement. DAP created the conditions for corruption by avoiding the safeguards it demands from others.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Every major corruption scandal in Malaysian infrastructure — from 1MDB to PKFZ to the Pandora Papers — involved opaque procurement. DAP’s response to each was to demand transparency and open tenders. Yet in Penang, the same party used RFPs for its most expensive projects. The evidence from the Guan Eng trial suggests the procurement process was exploited for personal benefit.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You demand open tenders from Putrajaya. You use RFPs in Penang. Your former CM is on trial over a project procured through your preferred opaque method. The lesson is obvious: adopt open competitive tendering for all state projects above RM10 million. Publish all procurement documents. And stop using a procurement method that your own corruption case demonstrates can be exploited.

Perak River Turns BLUE from Rare Earth Mining — Operations Suspended

Said by: Johari Abdul Ghani, Environment Minister

Category: Other

Date: 2025-11-20

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: In November 2025, a stretch of the Perak River turned bright blue due to chemical discharges from a rare earth mining site. Environment Minister Johari Abdul Ghani confirmed the contamination and ordered operations suspended pending investigation. The incident occurred as the Malaysian government was aggressively pushing rare earth expansion — signing deals with the US and positioning Malaysia as a rival to China’s dominance in critical minerals. The blue river became a viral symbol of the environmental cost of the rare earth push.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Malaysia is betting its economic future on rare earth minerals — a strategic commodity in the US-China tech war. But rare earth processing generates toxic waste: radioactive thorium, heavy metals, and chemical byproducts that contaminate water, soil, and ecosystems. The Perak River incident exposed the gap between the government’s rare earth ambitions and its environmental enforcement capacity. If a single mining site can turn a river blue with impunity, what happens when Malaysia scales up to rival China’s output? The environmental cost could be catastrophic.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government suspended operations after the river turned blue — not before. Environmental enforcement in Malaysia is reactive, not preventive. The same government that renewed Lynas’s licence for 10 years (with radioactive waste still stored in Pahang) is now pushing for more rare earth mining without demonstrating it can regulate the existing operations. Johari’s response — suspend and investigate — is the standard Malaysian playbook: act after the damage, announce an investigation, and hope the news cycle moves on. The river turned blue because enforcement failed. Suspension is not accountability.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: The Lynas rare earth plant in Gebeng, Pahang has been controversial since 2012. Radioactive waste remains stored on-site with no permanent disposal solution despite a decade of promises. The Bukit Merah rare earth processing site in Perak (operated by Mitsubishi in the 1980s) left a legacy of elevated cancer rates and birth defects that took decades to clean up. Malaysia’s rare earth history is a history of environmental damage — and the government is now repeating the pattern at a larger scale.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You want Malaysia to be a rare earth superpower. But you can’t stop a single mine from turning a river blue. Before signing more rare earth deals with Washington, demonstrate that you can regulate the industry you already have. Publish environmental monitoring data for every active rare earth site. Resolve the Lynas waste issue. Fund independent environmental audits. Or keep chasing rare earth revenue while rivers change colour — and let the rakyat downstream pay the health costs.

Data Centre Boom Drains Water and Power — Johor Told to Wait Until 2027 for Water Supply

Said by: Johor state government and Anwar Ibrahim

Category: Economy

Date: 2025-11-18

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: Malaysia’s data centre gold rush hit a wall in late 2025 when Johor — ground zero for the country’s silicon valley ambitions — told investors to postpone water-cooled projects for at least 18 months because the state couldn’t supply enough water. By February 2026, Anwar froze all non-AI data centre approvals entirely. Between 2023 and 2025, the government had approved billions in data centre projects from Microsoft, Google, AWS, Oracle, ByteDance, and GDS Holdings — without ensuring the infrastructure could support them. Malaysia’s national non-revenue water rate is 37.1%, meaning over a third of treated water is lost to leaking pipes before reaching consumers.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: The government celebrated billions in data centre investment commitments as proof that the Madani economy was attracting global capital. Now it’s telling those same investors to wait because there’s no water and not enough electricity. You cannot approve massive industrial projects that each consume as much water as a small city without ensuring the infrastructure can support them. This is basic governance, and the government failed at it because investment announcements make better headlines than infrastructure audits. The freeze creates investor uncertainty at exactly the wrong moment — companies that committed billions based on government approvals are now being told to wait.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government simultaneously approved a Johor special economic zone designed to attract even more industrial investment, creating a direct conflict between economic ambition and infrastructure reality. Malaysia loses 37.1% of treated water to leaking pipes. States like Perlis and Kelantan waste 50-60%. Building water-intensive industries on top of a crumbling water system is negligence. The irony is suffocating: the government that can’t maintain water pipes for its own citizens approved water-guzzling data centres for foreign corporations, then had to tell both groups to wait. Governance by headline, failure by infrastructure.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia has a pattern of approving mega-projects before building the infrastructure to support them. The Iskandar Malaysia development zone promised a “world-class city” but still struggles with basic services. Forest City was approved as a RM400 billion development but sits largely empty. Each time, the government prioritises investment announcements over infrastructure planning, then scrambles when reality catches up. The data centre freeze is the latest iteration of the same pattern: approve first, plan never.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You approved billions in data centre investment without ensuring water and electricity supply. Now you’re telling investors to wait 18 months. Fix the pipes first — 37.1% non-revenue water is a national embarrassment. Publish a national infrastructure capacity assessment before approving any new industrial projects. Stop announcing investment wins that you can’t deliver on. Or keep building a silicon valley on a foundation of leaking pipes and overloaded grids — and watch investors move to Thailand and Indonesia instead.

PM's Political Secretary Caught Issuing Support Letters for Hospital Contractors — Anwar Says a Reprimand Is Enough

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2025-11-18

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 18 November 2025, Anwar confirmed in Parliament that he had reprimanded his senior political secretary, Shamsul Iskandar Mohd Akin, over support letters listing six contractors for a hospital project in Johor. Anwar described the letters as “vague.” On 25 November, Shamsul resigned after mounting media pressure and calls from Rafizi Ramli for a MACC investigation. A Sabah businessman then claimed he provided RM152,000 to Shamsul — money allegedly spent on home furniture, premium cigars, and tailored suits. On 4 December 2025, MACC charged Shamsul with corruption over RM176,829 in gratification related to the hospital project.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: The PM’s office was literally issuing contractor support letters. This is the swamp Anwar promised to drain. Shamsul Iskandar was not a minor figure — he was a PKR veteran since the party’s founding, operating within the Prime Minister’s Department with direct influence over government procurement. When your own political secretary is funnelling contractor names to procurement officers and receiving RM152,000 in kickbacks spent on premium cigars and tailored suits, the anti-corruption brand is not just damaged — it is a punchline.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Anwar’s first instinct was to minimise. “The letters were vague.” “A reprimand is sufficient.” The Edge reported that the support letters listed specific contractors by name — contradicting Anwar’s claim. Naming specific companies in a government support letter is not vague — it is a recommendation, and anyone in procurement knows exactly what it means. The timeline is damning: Shamsul issues letters → Anwar reprimands privately → media reports emerge → Anwar defends the reprimand in Parliament → businessman reveals RM152K payments → Shamsul resigns → MACC charges him. At every stage, Anwar was behind the curve — reacting to pressure rather than leading on accountability. If Rafizi had not publicly demanded action, would Anwar have done anything beyond a private reprimand? The evidence suggests no.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Support letters from political offices are one of Malaysia’s oldest patronage mechanisms. Under BN, political secretaries routinely monetised their proximity to power. Najib’s aide Jho Low operated with impunity for years before 1MDB collapsed. Under Muhyiddin, aide Ahmad Maslan faced corruption charges. The pattern is consistent across administrations: political aides operate in a grey zone of influence, and PMs look the other way until media pressure forces action. PH promised to end this culture. Instead, they replicated it — different party, same premium cigars.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Your senior political secretary was charged with corruption for issuing support letters from your office. Your first response was a private reprimand. Your second response was to call the letters “vague.” Your third response — after RM152,000 in kickbacks surfaced — was to accept a resignation you should have demanded weeks earlier. Implement mandatory asset declarations for all political appointees. Ban support letters from ministerial offices. Publish procurement records for every project where support letters were issued. Or keep discovering corruption in your own office after the media does your job for you.

Selangor MB's Aide Accused of Unexplained Wealth — RM3M Bungalow, Luxury Trips, and Too Many GLC Posts

Said by: Amirudin Shari, Selangor Menteri Besar

Date: 2025-11-15

The administrator questioned how the leader could afford a RM3 million bungalow, luxury overseas trips, and high-end hotel stays while holding numerous positions in state-linked agencies. The group asked: ‘Why is one person appointed to so many positions, as if there are no other qualified people in Selangor?’

When a single aide holds multiple GLC positions and displays a lifestyle inconsistent with public service income, the public deserves answers. The Selangor MB’s office dismissed the claims as ‘baseless’ and ‘politically motivated’ — the standard deflection used by every Malaysian politician since independence.

If the wealth is legitimate, declare it. If the multiple GLC appointments are merit-based, publish the selection criteria. PH promised transparency — threatening legal action against people who ask questions about wealth is the opposite of transparency.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Unexplained wealth among political aides is a recurring pattern in Malaysian politics. Under BN, political secretaries and officers routinely accumulated wealth through proximity to power. PH criticised this relentlessly in opposition. Under Amirudin, the pattern continues — different coalition, same playbook.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: If the allegations are false, prove it with asset declarations. If they’re true, investigate. But threatening reporters and WhatsApp administrators with legal action while refusing to answer the actual question — ‘how does a public servant afford this lifestyle?’ — is exactly what BN used to do. You called it corruption then. What do you call it now?

Penang Undersea Tunnel Project 'Still Active' — 14 Years, Zero Construction, RM6.3B Price Tag

Said by: Chow Kon Yeow, Penang Chief Minister

Category: Economy

Date: 2025-11-14

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 14 November 2025, Chow Kon Yeow told media that the Penang Undersea Tunnel Project is ’not an abandoned project but is still active and in the technical review and design completion phase.’ The RM6.3 billion project was proposed in 2011 under the Penang Transport Master Plan (PTMP). Fourteen years later, not a single metre of tunnel has been constructed. Meanwhile, the corruption trial of former CM Lim Guan Eng over the project continues, with MACC witnesses describing it as ‘akin to a land scam.’

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: A RM6.3 billion project proposed 14 years ago with zero construction is either a failed project or a phantom one. Chow’s insistence that it’s ‘still active’ strains credibility to breaking point. The project was supposed to solve Penang’s traffic crisis — which has only worsened during the 14-year wait. Meanwhile, the former CM who approved the project is on trial for corruption related to it. At what point does ‘still active’ become ‘still pretending’?

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: ‘Technical review and design completion phase’ — after 14 years. For comparison, China built the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macau Bridge (the world’s longest sea crossing) in 9 years. Singapore built the Thomson-East Coast MRT line in less time. Penang can’t complete the design phase for a tunnel that was supposed to be built a decade ago. The project exists on paper to justify the PTMP’s existence and the land reclamation that was supposed to fund it. The tunnel is Schrödinger’s infrastructure — simultaneously active and non-existent.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: The PTMP was criticised from the start for its opaque procurement — using request-for-proposal mechanisms instead of open tenders. The Penang South Islands reclamation was conceived as the funding mechanism for the PTMP. So the logic is: destroy the sea to fund a tunnel that hasn’t been built in 14 years. Each project justifies the other; neither delivers for the public.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Fourteen years. RM6.3 billion. Zero metres built. Former CM on trial for corruption related to the project. Current CM insists it’s ‘still active.’ At what point do you admit this project is a failure? Cancel it, audit the spending, and redirect the funds to transport solutions that can actually be built — bus rapid transit, cycling infrastructure, traffic management. Penang’s commuters need solutions, not phantom tunnels.
