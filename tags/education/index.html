[BM] Pelajar Tunjuk Kad Merah Kepada Adam Adli Atas Janji AUKU Yang Dimungkiri — Anak Emas PH Sendiri Jadi Pemungkir

Said by: Adam Adli Abdul Halim, Deputy Higher Education Minister

Category: Education

Date: 2026-02-09

📌 APA YANG BERLAKU: Pada 9 Februari 2026, pelajar universiti berarak ke Parlimen menuntut pemansuhan Akta Universiti dan Kolej Universiti 1971 (AUKU). Sasaran mereka? Timbalan Menteri Pengajian Tinggi Adam Adli Abdul Halim — pernah menjadi aktivis pelajar paling terkenal Malaysia, kini penjelmaan hidup janji PH yang dimungkiri. Memansuhkan AUKU merupakan janji manifesto utama Pakatan Harapan dalam PRU14 (2018) dan PRU15 (2022). Pada lewat Januari 2026, Menteri Pengajian Tinggi Zambry Abd Kadir memberitahu Parlimen kerajaan “tidak bercadang” memansuhkan AUKU — pembalikan dasar yang disampaikan tanpa gembar-gembur. Pelajar menunjukkan kad merah kepada Adam Adli dan memanggilnya “pemungkir.” Zambry enggan menerima memorandum mereka. Adam Adli, 33, ditahan pada 2013 kerana menurunkan bendera di perhimpunan pelajar — tindakan yang menjadikannya simbol gerakan pelajar. PH memperjuangkan kesnya sebagai bukti AUKU tidak adil. Kini beliau duduk dalam kementerian yang menguatkuasakannya.

🔥 KENAPA INI PENTING: PH berjanji memansuhkan AUKU dalam dua pilihan raya berturut-turut. Empat tahun berkuasa, Menteri Pengajian Tinggi kata “tidak bercadang” memansuhkannya. Ini bukan penangguhan — ini pembohongan. Janji itu tidak pernah dimaksudkan untuk ditunaikan; ia direka untuk menuai undi pelajar. Perjalanan peribadi Adam Adli ialah metafora paling memusnahkan untuk kerajaan PH: pelajar yang ditahan kerana menurunkan bendera sebagai bantahan kini duduk dalam kementerian yang mengekalkan undang-undang yang menjenayahkan bantahan pelajar. Pelajar yang mengundi PH berdasarkan janji ini kini membantah timbalan menteri PH sendiri. Keengganan Zambry menerima memorandum adalah tamparan simbolik — apabila kerajaan tidak sanggup menerima sehelai kertas daripada penunjuk perasaan pelajar, mesejnya jelas: undi anda berguna, suara anda tidak. AUKU menentukan sama ada 1.3 juta pelajar universiti Malaysia boleh mengambil bahagian dalam kehidupan politik negara. Mengekalkannya bermakna PH percaya pelajar patut mengundi untuk reformasi tetapi tidak menyokongnya.

💩 KENAPA INI KARUT: Di bawah AUKU, pelajar boleh berdepan penggantungan atau penyingkiran kerana menyertai aktiviti politik. Undang-undang ini digubal pada 1971 selepas bantahan pelajar terhadap kemiskinan luar bandar — ia direka dari awal untuk menyekat belia yang sedar politik. PH tahu ini. PH berkempen menentangnya. Timbalan menteri PH sendiri menjadi mangsa. Dan kini PH mengekalkannya. Straits Times mencatat bantahan itu “menambah cabaran reformasi prapilihan raya Anwar” — merangka AUKU sebagai sebahagian daripada corak janji tergendala yang lebih luas yang menghakis sokongan belia. Jika anda tidak boleh menunaikan janji kepada pelajar yang percaya pada anda, jenama reformasi sudah mati.

📜 KES TERDAHULU: Reformasi AUKU dijanjikan oleh setiap gabungan pembangkang sejak tsunami politik 2008. PH 1.0 (2018-2020) menjanjikan reformasi tetapi runtuh sebelum bertindak. PH 2.0 kini mempunyai lebih tiga tahun dan secara jelas membalikan janji. Corak sama seperti penghapusan tol, pemisahan AG-PP, dan pemansuhan Akta Hasutan: janji berani dalam pembangkang, tangguh dalam kerajaan, diam-diam tinggalkan. AUKU menyertai senarai ikrar manifesto yang merupakan hiasan kempen, bukan komitmen tadbir urus.

🎯 SERUAN KEPADA PH: Timbalan menteri anda ditahan di bawah undang-undang ini. Beliau membina kerjaya melawannya. Kini beliau mempertahankan kementerian yang menguatkuasakannya. Jika anda tulis ini sebagai fiksyen, ia akan dikritik sebagai terlalu tepat. Mansuhkan AUKU atau pinda untuk membuang sekatan terhadap aktiviti politik pelajar — seperti yang anda janji dua kali. 1.3 juta pelajar di universiti Malaysia sedang memerhati. Dan mereka mengundi.

Students Flash Red Cards at Adam Adli Over Broken UUCA Promise — PH's Own Poster Boy Turns Reneger

Said by: Adam Adli Abdul Halim, Deputy Higher Education Minister

Category: Education

Date: 2026-02-09

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 9 February 2026, university students marched to Parliament demanding the abolition of the Universities and University Colleges Act 1971 (UUCA). Their target? Deputy Higher Education Minister Adam Adli Abdul Halim — once Malaysia’s most famous student activist, now the living embodiment of PH’s broken promise. Abolishing UUCA was a key manifesto promise by Pakatan Harapan in both GE14 (2018) and GE15 (2022). In late January 2026, Higher Education Minister Zambry Abd Kadir told Parliament the government had “no plans” to abolish UUCA — a policy reversal delivered without fanfare. Students flashed red cards at Adam Adli and called him a “reneger.” Zambry refused to receive their memorandum. Adam Adli, 33, was arrested in 2013 for lowering a flag at a student rally — an act that made him a symbol of the student movement. PH championed his case as proof that UUCA was unjust. Now he sits in the ministry that enforces it.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: PH promised to repeal UUCA in two consecutive elections. Four years into government, the Higher Education Minister says there are “no plans” to abolish it. This is not a delay — it is a lie. The promise was never intended to be kept; it was designed to harvest student votes. Adam Adli’s personal arc is the most devastating metaphor for the PH government: the student who was arrested for lowering a flag in protest now sits in the ministry that maintains the law that criminalises student protest. The students who voted for PH based on this promise are now protesting PH’s own deputy minister. Zambry’s refusal to receive the memorandum is a symbolic gut punch — when the government won’t even accept a piece of paper from student protesters, the message is clear: your vote was useful, your voice is not. UUCA determines whether 1.3 million Malaysian university students can participate in the political life of their country. Keeping it in place means PH believes students should vote for reform but not advocate for it.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Under UUCA, students can face suspension or expulsion for participating in political activities. The law was enacted in 1971 after student protests against rural poverty — it was designed from the start to suppress politically conscious youth. PH knew this. PH campaigned against it. PH’s own deputy minister was a victim of it. And now PH is keeping it. The Straits Times noted the protest “adds to Anwar’s pre-election reform challenges” — framing UUCA as part of a broader pattern of stalled promises eroding youth support. If you cannot keep promises to students who believed in you, the reform brand is dead.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: UUCA reform has been promised by every opposition coalition since the 2008 political tsunami. PH 1.0 (2018-2020) promised reform but collapsed before acting. PH 2.0 has now had over three years and explicitly reversed the promise. The pattern is identical to toll abolition, AG-PP separation, and the Sedition Act repeal: promise boldly in opposition, delay in government, quietly abandon. UUCA joins a growing list of manifesto pledges that were campaign decorations, not governance commitments.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Your deputy minister was arrested under this law. He built his career fighting it. Now he defends the ministry that enforces it. If you wrote this as fiction, it would be criticised as too on-the-nose. Abolish UUCA or amend it to remove restrictions on student political activity — as you promised twice. The 1.3 million students in Malaysian universities are watching. And they vote.

[BM] Bahasa Melayu dan Sejarah Wajib Merentas SEMUA Sistem Pendidikan — Pengkritik Kata Ia Menyekat Kepelbagaian

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister

Category: Education

Date: 2026-01-21

📌 APA YANG BERLAKU: Pada Januari 2026, Perdana Menteri Anwar Ibrahim mengumumkan bahawa Bahasa Melayu dan Sejarah akan diwajibkan merentas semua sistem pendidikan di Malaysia — termasuk sekolah vernakular (SJKC dan SJKT), sekolah antarabangsa, dan institusi swasta. Anwar berkata keperluan ini bertujuan “mengukuhkan kedudukan Bahasa Melayu sebagai bahasa kebangsaan” dan memberikan “pemahaman bersama tentang sejarah negara.” Pengkritik berhujah bahawa ini menjejaskan perlindungan perlembagaan untuk pendidikan vernakular di bawah Perkara 152(1)(a) dan menyekat kepelbagaian yang menjadikan sistem pendidikan Malaysia unik. Analisis mendalam CNA menyatakan “politik dan kesukaran” pembaharuan pendidikan Malaysia, menggambarkan bagaimana “bahasa dan agama” kekal sebagai bidang dasar paling mudah terbakar.

🔥 MENGAPA IA PENTING: Kebanyakan pelajar sekolah vernakular sudah pun belajar Bahasa Melayu — ia merupakan mata pelajaran wajib di semua sekolah bantuan kerajaan. Perubahan dasar ini adalah mengenai memperluaskan mata pelajaran wajib ke aliran yang secara tradisinya menetapkan keutamaan akademik sendiri. Sementara itu, krisis pendidikan sebenar langsung tidak ditangani: skor PISA terus merosot, enrolmen pengajian tinggi hanya 42.57% berbanding purata dunia 51.98%, guru melaporkan pelajar “lebih kurang ajar, lebih gelisah, kurang ingin tahu,” kes buli melonjak 17%, dan bahagian belanjawan pendidikan telah jatuh daripada 6.8% kepada 4.3% KDNK. 💩 MENGAPA IA KARUT: Perpaduan nasional melalui kurikulum wajib adalah idea era BN yang memakai sut era PH. Mahathir mendesak inisiatif serupa. Begitu juga Muhyiddin. Bingkaian berubah; dasar tidak. Kiraan politik jelas: Bahasa Melayu dan Sejarah wajib bermain baik dengan pangkalan pengundi Melayu-Islam menjelang pilihan raya. Merit pendidikan adalah sekunder kepada isyarat politik. Ini adalah politik identiti yang menyamar sebagai pembaharuan kurikulum. Isu sebenar bukan sama ada pelajar belajar Bahasa Melayu — ia adalah sama ada kerajaan menggunakan retorik “perpaduan” untuk menyeragamkan pendidikan sambil mengabaikan kegagalan sistemik yang benar-benar memusnahkan sistem: kadar kegagalan matematik 25%, 10,160 calon tidak hadir SPM, dan guru yang terlalu terbeban dengan 30+ pelajar setiap kelas tanpa sokongan struktur langsung.

📜 KES TERDAHULU: Setiap PM Malaysia telah menggunakan dasar bahasa dan kurikulum sebagai alat politik. Inisiatif Sekolah Wawasan Mahathir pada awal 2000-an cuba menempatkan sekolah vernakular dan kebangsaan bersama. Ia ditentang dan sebahagian besarnya gagal. Dasar PPSMI (mengajar matematik dan sains dalam Bahasa Inggeris) diperkenalkan, ditarik balik, diperkenalkan semula sebagai DLP, dan kekal sebagai bola politik. Coraknya konsisten: gunakan dasar pendidikan untuk isyarat politik, hadapi tentangan, berundur atau lemahkan, kemudian cuba lagi dengan nama berbeza. PH hanyalah kerajaan terkini yang menggunakan tuas “perpaduan nasional melalui kurikulum wajib.”

🎯 TEGURAN PH: Sistem pendidikan anda sedang runtuh — skor PISA merosot, buli melonjak, guru kewalahan, pelajar tidak terlibat dan menatal di bawah meja — dan jawapan anda adalah menambah lebih banyak mata pelajaran wajib? Baiki pengajaran dahulu. Biayai sekolah dengan betul. Tangani 33% pelajar yang gagal sekurang-kurangnya satu mata pelajaran SPM. Kemudian baru bercakap tentang penambahan kurikulum. Menambah Bahasa Melayu dan Sejarah wajib kepada sekolah yang sudah mengajar Bahasa Melayu sambil mengabaikan keruntuhan kualiti pengajaran bukan pembaharuan — ia adalah pengalihan.

Mandatory Malay and History Across ALL Education Systems — Critics Say It Stifles Diversity

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister

Category: Education

Date: 2026-01-21

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: In January 2026, Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim announced that Bahasa Melayu and History would be mandatory across all education systems in Malaysia — including vernacular schools (SJKC and SJKT), international schools, and private institutions. Anwar said the requirement aims to “bolster the status of Bahasa Melayu as the national language” and provide “a common understanding of national history.” Critics argued this undermines constitutional protections for vernacular education under Article 152(1)(a) and stifles the diversity that makes Malaysia’s education system unique. CNA’s in-depth analysis noted the “politics and difficulties” of Malaysian education reform, describing how “language and religion” remain the most combustible policy areas.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Most vernacular school students already learn Bahasa Melayu — it is a compulsory subject in all government-aided schools. The policy change is about expanding mandatory subjects into streams that have traditionally set their own academic priorities. Meanwhile, the actual education crisis goes completely unaddressed: PISA scores keep declining, tertiary enrollment is only 42.57% versus the global average of 51.98%, teachers report students are “ruder, more restless, less curious,” bullying cases surged 17%, and the education budget share has dropped from 6.8% to 4.3% of GDP. Adding mandatory subjects to already overcrowded curricula does not improve education quality — it increases workload without fixing pedagogy, infrastructure, or teacher training.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: National unity through compulsory curriculum is a BN-era idea wearing a PH-era suit. Mahathir pushed similar initiatives. So did Muhyiddin. The framing changes; the policy doesn’t. The political calculus is transparent: mandatory Malay and History plays well with the Malay-Muslim base ahead of elections. The educational merit is secondary to the political signalling. This is identity politics disguised as curriculum reform. The real issue isn’t whether students learn Malay — it’s whether the government is using “unity” rhetoric to homogenise education while ignoring the systemic failures that are actually destroying the system: 25% math failure rates, 10,160 SPM no-shows, and teachers overwhelmed by 30+ students per class with zero structural support.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Every Malaysian PM has used language and curriculum policy as a political tool. Mahathir’s Vision Schools initiative in the early 2000s attempted to co-locate vernacular and national schools. It was resisted and largely failed. The PPSMI policy (teaching math and science in English) was introduced, reversed, reintroduced as DLP, and remains a political football. The pattern is consistent: use education policy for political signalling, face backlash, retreat or water down, then try again under a different name. PH is simply the latest government to reach for the “national unity through mandatory curriculum” lever.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Your education system is in freefall — declining PISA scores, surging bullying, overwhelmed teachers, students disengaged and scrolling under desks — and your response is to add more mandatory subjects? Fix the teaching first. Fund the schools properly. Address the 33% of students failing at least one SPM subject. Then talk about curriculum additions. Adding mandatory Malay and History to schools that already teach Malay while ignoring the collapse in teaching quality is not reform — it is deflection. Students don’t need more subjects. They need better education.

Malaysia's Schooling System in 'Decline' — Students Ruder, Less Curious, Teachers Overwhelmed

Said by: FMT Opinion / Teachers

Category: Education

Date: 2025-10-18

📌 WHAT HAPPENED

On 18 October 2025, Free Malaysia Today published an opinion piece by frontline teachers describing a schooling system in visible decline. The accounts were blunt: students scrolling TikTok under desks during lessons, snapping back at teachers when corrected, refusing to cooperate with classroom activities, and showing little to no curiosity about what’s being taught.

Teachers described fighting a losing battle — not against students, but against the algorithm. TikTok’s dopamine loop has become the real classroom authority. And structurally, teachers have nothing to fight back with: no updated pedagogy, no device management policy, no mental health support, and no institutional acknowledgment that the problem even exists.

The piece painted a picture of a profession in quiet crisis — underpaid, under-respected, and now outgunned by a phone screen.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS

Malaysia’s education system was already in trouble before TikTok. PISA scores have been stagnant or declining for over a decade. The 2022 PISA results placed Malaysian students below the OECD average in reading, mathematics, and science — a pattern that has not meaningfully improved since Malaysia first participated.

What the FMT piece describes is not a new problem. It is the visible symptom of decades of systemic neglect:

  • Overcrowded classrooms with 35–40 students per teacher in many urban and semi-urban schools
  • Outdated curricula that prioritise rote memorisation over critical thinking
  • Teacher burnout driven by administrative overload, low pay, and zero autonomy
  • No digital literacy framework — students have devices but no structured guidance on using them productively
  • Parental abdication — the phone has become the babysitter, and schools are expected to fix what homes have abandoned The decline isn’t coming. It’s here. And no one in Putrajaya is treating it as a crisis.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT

Education Minister Fadhlina Sidek has spent her tenure announcing feel-good initiatives — coding programmes, STEM hubs, “joy of learning” campaigns — while the structural foundations of the system rot underneath.

On teacher welfare:

  • Teachers are buried in clerical work, data entry, and co-curricular admin that has nothing to do with teaching. The ministry’s response has been to promise “reducing teacher workload” — a promise made by every education minister since at least 2015. Nothing has changed. On classroom discipline:

  • There is no coherent national framework for managing device use in schools. Some schools ban phones outright; others ignore the issue entirely. The ministry has issued no binding directive, leaving every teacher to fend for themselves against a billion-dollar attention economy. On curriculum reform:

  • The national curriculum (KSSM) was last updated in 2017. It still emphasises exam performance over genuine learning. The system produces students who can pass SPM but cannot write a coherent paragraph, evaluate a source, or hold a sustained conversation about anything that isn’t on a screen. On funding:

  • Malaysia spends roughly 3.2% of GDP on education — below the global average of 4.3% and well below regional peers like Vietnam (4.1%) and Thailand (3.8%). PH promised to prioritise education spending. The 2025 and 2026 budgets tell a different story. The ministry’s approach is cosmetic. Announce a pilot programme. Take photos. Move on. Meanwhile, a generation of students is being failed in real time.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES

  • 2022 PISA Results: Malaysia scored 398 in mathematics, 388 in reading, and 398 in science — all below the OECD average. Performance has flatlined since 2012 despite billions in education spending.
  • 2019: Teacher workload crisis. The National Union of the Teaching Profession (NUTP) publicly warned that teachers were spending more time on paperwork than teaching. The ministry promised reform. Teachers report no meaningful change.
  • 2023: Phone ban debate. After multiple viral incidents of students using phones in class, calls for a national phone ban gained traction. The ministry opted for “guidelines” rather than a directive, leaving enforcement to individual schools.
  • 2024: Graduate unemployment. Bank Negara and DOSM data showed that roughly 1 in 4 Malaysian graduates under 30 were either unemployed or underemployed. The education system is producing graduates the economy doesn’t need with skills the market doesn’t want.
  • Malaysia Education Blueprint 2013–2025. The blueprint promised to bring Malaysia into the “top third” of international education benchmarks by 2025. The deadline has passed. Malaysia is nowhere near the target.

🎯 PH CALLOUT

Fadhlina, your teachers are telling you — publicly, in national media — that they are losing their classrooms. Students are ruder. Less curious. Less engaged. And the system offers teachers nothing to fight back with.

Your response has been pilot programmes, photo ops, and vague promises about “21st-century learning.” None of it reaches the teacher standing in front of 38 teenagers who would rather watch a 15-second TikTok than learn anything.

You inherited a broken system. That’s true. But you promised to fix it. Two years in, the PISA scores haven’t moved, the teachers are more burned out than ever, and the curriculum still treats memorisation as mastery.

If PH wants to claim it governs differently, start with the one institution that touches every Malaysian family. Fund it properly. Free teachers from bureaucratic busywork. Implement a real digital literacy and device management framework. Reform the curriculum so it rewards thinking, not regurgitation.

Or keep doing what you’re doing — and watch an entire generation scroll its way to irrelevance.

Your minister has been in the job since 2022. Results have gotten WORSE, not better. Instead of accountability, you give her photo opportunities. Instead of funding schools, you fund JAKIM (RM2.6B) and J-Kom propaganda (RM85.5M). Malaysian children deserve a world-class education, not a world-class communications plan about education. Fire your education minister or fix the system — preferably both.

📜 Previous Cases

BN used vernacular school allocations as political patronage — offering funds in exchange for community support. MCA and MIC existed primarily to secure these allocations, perpetuating dependency. PH was supposed to break this cycle by funding schools equitably regardless of medium of instruction. Instead, PH has maintained the BN model: Chinese and Indian schools get what PH decides to give them, when PH decides to give it.

🎯 PH Callout

Non-Malay Malaysians didn’t vote PH to continue BN’s patronage model with better optics. They voted for EQUAL treatment. Fund vernacular schools at the same per-student rate as national schools. Not as a favour. Not as a budget announcement photo op. As a constitutional obligation under Article 152 and basic educational equity. Or admit that ‘Madani for all’ has an asterisk: *terms and conditions apply based on ethnicity.

Digital Divide Persists — Rural Communities Still Without Reliable Internet While Government Talks 5G

Said by: MCMC / Rural Development Ministry

Category: Education

Date: 2025-01-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: As of early 2025, millions of Malaysians in rural Sabah, Sarawak, and the east coast of Peninsular Malaysia still lack reliable internet access. While the government spent billions on the 5G DNB versus Telekom Malaysia dual-network fiasco, the JENDELA programme to improve basic digital infrastructure faced delays and coverage gaps. Students in rural schools were severely disadvantaged during COVID-19 distance learning, and the digital divide has not meaningfully narrowed since. MCMC’s own data shows connectivity gaps between urban and rural areas remain significant, with many rural schools relying on unstable satellite connections or no connectivity at all.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: The digital divide compounds every other form of marginalisation. Rural students can’t access online learning resources. Small businesses can’t participate in e-commerce. Healthcare providers can’t use telemedicine. Government services that have moved online are inaccessible to communities without internet. Kids in longhouses and kampungs can’t load a basic web page while KL gets 5G. The irony of spending billions on cutting-edge infrastructure while basic connectivity remains unavailable to millions is not lost on anyone except apparently the government.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government chose to spend billions on a dual 5G network — solving a political problem between telecom companies — while JENDELA, the basic connectivity programme, faces delays. For Orang Asli and indigenous communities in particular, digital exclusion compounds existing educational and economic marginalisation. A government that prioritises 5G headlines over universal basic connectivity has its priorities backwards. A truly “Madani” government would ensure every Malaysian can access basic internet before giving KL residents faster streaming.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Digital infrastructure promises have been made under every Malaysian government. Under BN, the 1Malaysia Internet Centre programme was launched — many centres were built but poorly maintained or unstaffed. Under PH 1.0, digital infrastructure was briefly prioritised before the Sheraton Move. Under PH 2.0, JENDELA has continued but with persistent delays. The pattern is consistent: announce connectivity programmes, build some infrastructure, under-maintain it, and move on to the next headline technology while basic access gaps persist.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You spent billions on 5G so KL can stream faster. Meanwhile, a kid in a Sarawak longhouse can’t submit homework online. That’s not a digital divide — it’s digital apartheid. Prioritise JENDELA completion. Ensure every school in Malaysia has reliable broadband. Fix basic connectivity before chasing 5G headlines. Or keep building luxury infrastructure for urban areas while rural Malaysians — the communities that vote for you in every election — remain disconnected from the digital economy you keep promoting.
