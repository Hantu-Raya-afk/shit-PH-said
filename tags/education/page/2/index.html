Higher Education Budget Keeps Shrinking — From 6.8% in 2010 to Just 4.3% in 2025

Said by: New Mandala / Academic Analysis

Category: Education

Date: 2025-01-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: Malaysia’s higher education budget has dropped from 6.8% of the total national budget in 2010 to just 4.3% in 2025, with the lowest point at 3.9% in 2023. Meanwhile, tertiary enrollment stands at only 42.57% — far below Singapore’s 93% and the global average of 51.98%. Academic analysis describes a “widening disjuncture between reformist discourse and actual financial commitment” to education under PH. While the government talks about the knowledge economy and human capital development, the budget tells a different story.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: A country that reduces education spending while talking about becoming a knowledge economy is lying to itself. The 4.3% allocation means fewer university places, less research funding, lower academic salaries, and deteriorating campus infrastructure. The tertiary enrollment gap — Malaysia at 42.57% vs Singapore at 93% — represents millions of young Malaysians who could be in higher education but aren’t, because the system doesn’t have the capacity to accommodate them. Every percentage point of budget decline translates into fewer graduates, less innovation, and slower economic growth.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The same government that cut higher education to 4.3% allocated RM2.6 billion to JAKIM — the highest allocation in JAKIM’s history. The civil service budget grows every year while education shrinks. When your budget priorities tell students they matter less than bureaucrats and religious administration, students respond by leaving the country. The brain drain — 1.86 million Malaysians abroad — is directly connected to an education system that receives declining investment. You can’t build human capital while cutting the budget for building it.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Under BN, education received a larger budget share but was plagued by inefficiency and political management. Under PH 1.0, education reform was briefly prioritised. Under PH 2.0, the budget has consistently declined. New Mandala’s analysis noted that PH’s education rhetoric is more progressive than any previous government but its funding commitment is among the worst. The gap between words and money is the gap between aspiration and reality.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You talk about human capital. You talk about the knowledge economy. You talk about innovation and competitiveness. Then you cut higher education to 4.3% of the budget — the lowest in modern Malaysian history. Singapore spends three times as much proportionally and has 93% tertiary enrollment. You have 42.57%. The numbers don’t lie. Reverse the education budget decline. Commit to reaching 6% within three years. Or stop talking about the knowledge economy while defunding the institutions that are supposed to build it.

PTPTN Loan Crisis — Millions Owe Billions, Repayment Rates Abysmal, Graduates Trapped in Debt

Said by: PTPTN / Auditor General Reports

Category: Education

Date: 2025-01-01

📌 What Happened

The National Higher Education Fund Corporation (PTPTN) faces a chronic repayment crisis, with billions of ringgit in outstanding loans and repayment rates that consistently fall short of targets. Millions of Malaysian graduates carry PTPTN debt that limits their ability to save, invest, or purchase homes. The fund’s sustainability is questionable without major reform.

🔥 Why It Matters

PTPTN debt creates a generational poverty trap: graduates enter the workforce already indebted, face an economy with 12.28% youth unemployment, earn wages that barely cover living costs (RM1,500 minimum wage), and must somehow repay student loans while building a life. Many can’t — which is why repayment rates are so low. The cycle feeds itself: unaffordable education → debt → low wages → inability to repay → creditworthiness issues → inability to get mortgages → permanent renter status.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

PH promised various PTPTN reforms including debt relief for low-income graduates, extended repayment flexibility, and making education more affordable to reduce future borrowing. Progress has been marginal. The government talks about education as an investment but treats PTPTN as a debt collection agency. Students who borrowed to get degrees that the economy doesn’t value (because the education system doesn’t produce employable graduates) are being punished for a system-level failure that isn’t their fault.

📜 Previous Cases

BN’s approach was also inadequate — carrot-and-stick approaches to loan recovery without addressing the fundamental issues of education quality and labour market mismatch. PH 1.0 promised PTPTN reform but collapsed before implementation. PH 2.0 has made incremental adjustments but the structural crisis remains. The money lent produces graduates who can’t find matching employment, who then can’t repay loans, which undermines the fund’s ability to lend to future students. It’s a doom loop.

🎯 PH Callout

Your education system produces graduates who can’t find jobs (12.28% youth unemployment). Your economy doesn’t pay graduates enough to repay their loans (RM1,500 minimum wage). And your PTPTN continues lending for degrees that don’t lead to employment. You’ve created a perfectly designed poverty machine: borrow for education → get a degree → find no matching job → can’t repay loan → can’t buy house → stay in poverty. Fix the pipeline or stop pretending the problem is lazy graduates.

Teacher Shortage in Rural Schools — Kids in Kampungs Get Second-Class Education

Said by: Education Ministry / NUTP

Category: Education

Date: 2025-01-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: Rural schools across Malaysia face chronic teacher shortages in mathematics, science, and English — the three subjects most critical for economic competitiveness. Indigenous communities in Sabah, Sarawak, and Orang Asli settlements face the worst gaps, with some schools lacking qualified teachers entirely for key subjects. The National Union of the Teaching Profession (NUTP) has repeatedly raised the issue. The Education Ministry acknowledges the shortage but has not implemented structural reforms to address it.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: A child’s postcode determines the quality of education they receive. Urban schools in KL, Penang, and JB have qualified teachers, resources, and facilities. Rural schools — especially in Sabah, Sarawak, and Orang Asli areas — have empty classrooms, unqualified substitute teachers, and crumbling infrastructure. This two-tier system perpetuates generational poverty: rural children receive inferior education, score lower on SPM, access fewer university places, and earn less throughout their lives. The education gap is not a resource problem — it is a political priorities problem.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government spends RM2.6 billion on JAKIM and shrinks the higher education budget to 4.3%. Rural teacher shortages have been documented for decades. The solution is known: incentivise qualified teachers to serve in rural areas with housing, salary bonuses, and career advancement. Singapore does this. Thailand does this. Malaysia talks about it. Every education blueprint mentions rural schools. None solves the problem because solving it requires redirecting money from urban constituencies to rural areas — and urban constituencies have more political leverage.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Rural teacher shortages have been a known problem since the 1980s. Under BN, the “Guru Cemerlang” programme was supposed to attract top teachers to underserved areas — it didn’t. Under PH 1.0, rural education was briefly prioritised before the Sheraton Move. Under PH 2.0, the same shortage persists. Each government launches a programme with a new name. None provides the structural incentives — housing, salary, career progression — that would actually convince qualified teachers to serve in rural Sabah or Orang Asli settlements.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Kids in kampungs deserve the same quality teachers as kids in KL. They don’t get them because your government won’t pay for it. Provide housing and salary incentives for rural teachers. Build partnerships with local universities to train teachers for their own communities. Fund rural school infrastructure to the same standard as urban schools. Or keep producing two classes of Malaysian graduates — one educated, one abandoned — and wonder why the development gap never closes.

PISA Scores Collapse — Malaysia Loses 1.6 YEARS of Learning, Now 8 Years Behind Singapore in Math

Said by: OECD PISA 2022 Report / ISIS Malaysia Analysis

Category: Education

Date: 2024-12-06

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: The 2022 PISA report, released in December 2024, revealed Malaysia suffered among the top five largest declines globally across all three subjects. Mathematics dropped 31 points (440 to 409), reading fell 27 points (415 to 388), and science declined 22 points (438 to 416). ISIS Malaysia estimated this equates to 1.6 years of lost learning in mathematics alone. 59% of Malaysian students are now below basic proficiency in math — up from 41%. Only 1% of Malaysian students achieved high proficiency. The socioeconomic gap is widening, with wealthy students outperforming disadvantaged ones by 82 points.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Malaysia is now 8 years behind Singapore and 3 years behind Vietnam in mathematics educational attainment. This isn’t just a statistic — it’s a generation of young Malaysians who cannot compete in the global knowledge economy. The decline is not evenly distributed: rural students, B40 families, and students in underperforming states bear the worst impact. When 59% of your 15-year-olds can’t do basic math, the consequences ripple through the economy for decades — in lower productivity, higher unemployment, and reduced innovation capacity.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government blamed COVID-19 school closures, but the OECD average only dropped 15 points in math — Malaysia dropped double at 31 points. Other countries with similar closures performed better. The decline predates COVID: Malaysia has been falling since 2015. The pandemic accelerated a rot that was already there — underfunded schools, poorly trained teachers, and a curriculum that prioritises rote memorisation over critical thinking. Malaysia pulled out of PISA entirely in 2003 and 2006 because the results were embarrassing. The Malaysia Education Blueprint 2013-2025 was supposed to fix everything — it fixed nothing. A decade of “blueprints” and “transformation plans” produced the worst results in history.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Under BN, Malaysia maintained mediocre but stable PISA scores. Under PH, scores collapsed. The percentage of students below basic proficiency increased from 41% to 59%. Every education minister launches a blueprint. None delivers improvement. The fundamental problem — a curriculum designed around political and ethnic considerations rather than educational excellence — remains untouched because no government has been willing to pay the political cost of genuine reform. JAKIM receives RM2.6 billion. Higher education gets 4.3% of the budget.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Your own minister admitted the system “failed both teachers and students.” You allocate RM2.6 billion to JAKIM for religious affairs but cut higher education to 4.3% of the budget. Your students are 8 years behind Singapore. 59% can’t do basic math. Only 1% achieve high proficiency. Your priorities are clear — and education isn’t one of them. Reverse the education budget decline. Invest in teacher quality. Reform the curriculum for critical thinking. Or keep producing the worst PISA results in Malaysian history and wonder why the brain drain accelerates.

30% of SPM Candidates FAIL At Least One Subject — A Pass Grade of 20% in Math Is Not Education, It's a Lie

Said by: Education Ministry Data / Independent Analysis

Category: Education

Date: 2024-05-27

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: In SPM 2023 results released in May 2024, approximately 30% of candidates failed at least one subject. Mathematics had the highest failure rate at 25.9%, followed by science at 25%. The best-performing subject was Quran and Sunnah at 84%. Reports indicate the pass threshold for mathematics may be as low as 20% — meaning a student who gets 4 out of 5 questions wrong still “passes.” The government had previously abolished UPSR (Year 6) and PMR (Year 9) examinations, removing the only diagnostic checkpoints that could identify struggling students early.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: When you set the pass mark at 20%, you’re not measuring education — you’re manufacturing the illusion of it. A student who “passes” mathematics with a 20% score cannot do basic arithmetic reliably. These students enter the workforce, attempt further education, or become statistics in the 12.28% youth unemployment figure. The education system is producing graduates with certificates but without competence. Under BN, Malaysia maintained mediocre but stable PISA scores (440 in math in 2018). Under PH, scores crashed 31 points to 409. The percentage of students below basic proficiency increased from 41% to 59%.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government abolished UPSR and PMR — removing the diagnostic checkpoints that would catch struggling students at ages 12 and 15 — then acts surprised when 30% fail at SPM. As one analyst put it: “The journey seems to be just to promote incompetence from one stage to the other until students reach SPM.” When the government removes tests and lowers pass marks, it’s not reforming education — it’s hiding failure. The education system is producing religious knowledge better than mathematics, science, or English — and the government’s response is more blueprints, more jargon, and more press conferences.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: The Malaysia Education Blueprint 2013-2025, continued by PH, has been a decade-long failure by its own metrics. PISA scores have declined. SPM failure rates remain high. Youth unemployment suggests the system doesn’t prepare students for work. Every education minister launches a “transformation.” None transforms anything. The fundamental problem — a curriculum designed around political and ethnic considerations rather than educational excellence — remains untouched because reforming it would require confronting interests no government has been willing to challenge.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Your education system produces religious knowledge better than mathematics. Your students are 8 years behind Singapore and 3 years behind Vietnam. You abolished the tests that would have caught this earlier. You lowered the pass mark to hide the failure. And you call this “Madani civilisation”? A civilisation that can’t teach its children to count is not a civilisation — it’s a civilisational collapse in slow motion. Restore diagnostic checkpoints. Raise pass standards to meaningful levels. Reform the curriculum for competence, not compliance. Or keep producing SPM certificates that employers don’t trust and the world doesn’t recognise.

Over 10,000 SPM Students Didn't Even Sit for the Exam — Education System Haemorrhaging

Said by: Education Ministry / DOSM

Category: Education

Date: 2024-03-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: More than 10,160 registered SPM candidates — 2.6% of the total — did not sit for Malaysia’s most important school examination in 2023. This is not a rounding error; it represents over ten thousand young Malaysians who disengaged from the education system entirely. Combined with a tertiary enrollment rate of just 42.57% (versus Singapore’s 93% and the global average of 51.98%), youth unemployment stuck at 12.28%, a higher education budget share that dropped from 6.8% in 2010 to 4.3% in 2025, and 7,681 reported bullying cases, the system is haemorrhaging students at every level.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: When over 10,000 students don’t even show up for SPM, it signals a system that has lost legitimacy in the eyes of the people it’s supposed to serve. These are not students who failed — they are students who decided the system wasn’t worth showing up for. The reasons are multi-layered: financial hardship forcing teenagers into the workforce, mental health struggles, disillusionment with an education system perceived as irrelevant, and the growing sense that a Malaysian degree may not lead to meaningful employment given 12.28% youth unemployment.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government allocated a declining share of the national budget to education — down to 4.3% for higher education in 2025 from 6.8% in 2010. When the Education Minister reports 7,681 bullying cases and rising student disengagement, the response should be structural reform, not communication plans. But structural reform requires money and political will — both of which are directed elsewhere. The civil service budget grows every year. The education budget shrinks. When your budget priorities tell students they matter less than bureaucrats, students respond by walking away.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Student dropout and disengagement have been rising in Malaysia for years. The COVID pandemic accelerated the trend — thousands of students from B40 families never returned to school after lockdowns. The government’s response has been programmes and initiatives that generate press releases but don’t address root causes: poverty, irrelevant curricula, teacher quality, and a qualification system that employers don’t value. Every education minister announces a “transformation.” None transforms anything.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Ten thousand students gave up on your education system. They didn’t fail — they decided it wasn’t worth showing up for. When your budget for education keeps shrinking while your budget for civil service salaries keeps growing, you’re telling the next generation exactly where your priorities are. And they’re responding by walking away. Reverse the education budget decline. Reform the curriculum for relevance. Address bullying as a systemic crisis, not a statistic. Or keep losing students — and wonder why the brain drain starts before they even graduate.

Malaysia's PISA Scores Decline Most Among Southeast Asian Countries — Anwar Says 'Cannot Be in Denial'

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister

Category: Education

Date: 2024-01-15

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: Malaysia’s 2022 PISA results — measuring education quality among 15-year-olds — showed the biggest decline among all Southeast Asian countries tested. Scores dropped across mathematics, science, and reading. Anwar responded by saying Malaysia “must know the state of our affairs and cannot be in a state of denial.” The Education Minister admitted the system “failed both teachers and students.” CNA reported that politics — particularly the tension between Malay language requirements and English proficiency — is behind the difficulty of education reform. Three years later, experts say nothing has changed on the ground.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Education determines a nation’s future. Malaysia’s education system is failing — and the government knows it. PISA results have stagnated for three consecutive assessment cycles while regional competitors like Vietnam and Indonesia have improved. FMT observed: “Every few years, the ministry launches a new education blueprint. It’s got a sleek logo and pages of jargon. But on the ground, nothing changes.” The gap between Malaysia and Singapore in mathematics is now equivalent to approximately 8 years of learning. A generation of Malaysian students is being left behind by a system that prioritises political considerations over educational outcomes.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: “Cannot be in denial” is an admission, not a policy. Anwar acknowledged the problem in January 2024. It is now March 2026. Where are the results? The politics of language, religion, and race make education reform nearly impossible in Malaysia’s coalition system — and PH has shown zero willingness to confront those politics. Mandatory Malay and History requirements across all education systems were expanded rather than reformed. UUCA — which restricts student political participation — remains intact despite being a manifesto promise. The education system is failing because fixing it requires confronting entrenched interests that PH depends on for political survival.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia’s education decline is not new. PISA scores have been mediocre since Malaysia first participated in 2009. Every education minister launches a new blueprint: the National Education Blueprint 2013-2025, various STEM initiatives, TVET expansions. Each generates reports and press conferences. None generates measurable improvement in student outcomes. The fundamental problem — a system designed around political and ethnic considerations rather than educational excellence — remains untouched because no government has been willing to pay the political cost of reforming it.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You said Malaysia “cannot be in denial.” Then you stayed in denial. PISA scores are declining. The education gap with Singapore is 8 years of learning. Your response was to acknowledge the problem and do nothing about it. Reform the curriculum to prioritise critical thinking over rote memorisation. Invest in teacher training and compensation. Address the language policy bottleneck honestly. Or keep launching education blueprints with sleek logos while Malaysian students fall further behind their regional peers — and wonder why the brain drain accelerates.

Sources: CNA | FMT
