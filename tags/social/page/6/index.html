Police Threaten Bersih Rally Participants — PH Government Uses Same Tactics It Once Condemned

Said by: Bersih / Lawyers for Liberty

Category: Social

Date: 2024-02-26

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: In February 2024, the PH government sent police warnings that Bersih’s planned demonstration was illegal. Officers visited participants’ homes and issued warnings ahead of the rally. Lawyers for Liberty (LFL) condemned it as “a betrayal by PH,” citing the government’s use of the same intimidation tactics PH had once fought against. CSIS noted that civil society groups are “torn between their historical alliance with PH and the government’s failure to implement its reform agenda.” Bersih — the electoral reform coalition that was PH’s closest ally during the BN era — was now being treated as a threat.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Bersih’s rallies were the oxygen of PH’s rise to power. The Bersih 2.0 through 5.0 rallies built the mass movement that eventually toppled BN. PH leaders didn’t just support these rallies — they marched in them, were tear-gassed at them, and used them to build their democratic credentials. Now the same government is sending police to people’s homes to warn them against attending a Bersih rally. The message is unmistakable: freedom of assembly is only acceptable when it serves PH’s interests.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: LFL’s condemnation specifically cited a 2014 Court of Appeal ruling — from PKR’s own Nik Nazmi’s case — that struck down restrictive provisions of the Peaceful Assembly Act. PH’s own legal victory against protest suppression is now being ignored by PH’s own government. Nik Nazmi, who was literally charged under the PAA for organising a protest in 2013, was a PH minister when police threatened Bersih participants. The hypocrisy is not subtle — it is the defining feature of PH’s approach to civil liberties: demand them in opposition, suppress them in government.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Under BN, police intimidation of rally participants was standard practice. Home visits, phone calls, and social media monitoring were used to discourage attendance at Bersih rallies. PH condemned these tactics as authoritarian. Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, and every Malaysian civil liberties organisation documented BN’s suppression of assembly rights. Now the same organisations are documenting the same tactics under PH — and the same civil society groups that once marched with PH are being targeted by PH.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You marched with Bersih. You were tear-gassed with Bersih. You built your movement on the right to peaceful assembly. Now you’re sending police to people’s homes to warn them against attending a Bersih rally. Lawyers for Liberty calls it a betrayal. CSIS says civil society is torn. The rakyat who marched for you are now being warned against marching for accountability. Guarantee the right to peaceful assembly without police intimidation. Or admit that freedom of assembly was only useful when you were the ones assembling.

HRW 2024: 'Reform Agenda Stalled' — Government Failed to Uphold Pledges in First Year

Said by: Human Rights Watch

Category: Social

Date: 2024-01-11

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: In January 2024, Human Rights Watch released its 2024 World Report, delivering a damning verdict on Anwar Ibrahim’s first year in office: “Reform Agenda Stalled.” HRW stated that Anwar had “largely failed to uphold his pledges to address repression and corruption” and called on the government to “recommit to a rights-respecting agenda by turning its sidelined campaign promises into action.” The report documented failures across freedom of expression, assembly, media freedom, and institutional reform. A year later, HRW’s 2025 assessment was even harsher: “Rights Backslide.”

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Two consecutive years of HRW condemnation is not a bump in the road — it is a trajectory. Year 1: stalled. Year 2: backslide. Year 3: expanded internet controls, 90+ sedition arrests, and journalist arrests. When the world’s most prominent human rights organisation says your reform agenda has stalled — and then says it’s actively going backwards — the problem is not perception. It is policy. PH was elected on a reform mandate. The international human rights community is documenting, in real time, the abandonment of that mandate.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: PH’s response to HRW’s assessment was predictable: “They don’t understand our context.” “Coalition constraints.” “We need time.” But HRW gave PH a year before issuing the “stalled” verdict. Then another year before escalating to “backslide.” The organisation is not being impatient — it is documenting a government that promised reform and delivered regression. The Sedition Act was not just left on the books — it was expanded. Media freedom was not just neglected — journalists were arrested. Assembly rights were not just unprotected — Bersih rallies were suppressed. This is not stalling. This is reversal.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: HRW has criticised every Malaysian government. Under Najib, the assessments focused on 1MDB, repressive laws, and crackdowns on civil society. Under Muhyiddin and Ismail Sabri, the focus was pandemic-era restrictions and democratic backsliding. But PH was supposed to be different — it was the government that HRW’s recommendations aligned with. When the human rights organisation that should be your natural ally says you’ve stalled and backslid, the problem is not the critic. It’s the government.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Human Rights Watch gave you a year. Then another year. The verdict got worse, not better. “Stalled” became “backslide.” If the trajectory continues, Year 4 will be “regression.” Implement the reforms HRW identified: repeal the Sedition Act, strengthen media freedom protections, guarantee assembly rights, and restore MACC independence. Or keep dismissing international criticism as “not understanding context” — while the context is that you’re doing exactly what BN did, with better English.

PADU Database Launches as Privacy Nightmare — Anyone With Your IC Number Can Access Your Data

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister

Category: Social

Date: 2024-01-02

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 2 January 2024, Anwar launched the Central Database Hub (PADU) — a system meant to revolutionise subsidy delivery by targeting aid to those who need it. Over 233,000 users registered on launch day. Within 48 hours, security researchers discovered the authentication was laughably weak: anyone armed with another person’s IC number and home postcode could register and access their data. CybersecurityAsia called it a “privacy nightmare.” Sarawak halted its participation entirely in March 2024, citing concerns over state autonomy and citizens’ personal data. By mid-2025, PADU was quietly sidelined — the RON95 subsidy reform bypassed it entirely, using JPJ vehicle registration data instead.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: The government launched a national database with authentication so weak it was effectively open access. IC numbers are not secrets — they appear on every government form and are routinely leaked in data breaches. Using them as the sole authentication factor for a database containing income, household, and employment data is not a design flaw — it is a systemic failure of digital governance. ISEAS noted that PADU “ignited a firestorm of debate over issues of privacy, federalism, and the very nature of the social contract between citizens and the state.” When Sarawak refused to participate and built its own system, the implied message was devastating: we don’t trust you with our citizens’ data.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: PADU was meant to be the backbone of Malaysia’s entire subsidy reform agenda. The government spent years arguing that blanket subsidies needed to be replaced with targeted ones, and PADU was the mechanism. When the biggest test came — RON95 reform — PADU was bypassed entirely. Billions were spent building a system that was abandoned without public acknowledgment or accountability. No postmortem was published. No accountability was assigned. No cost accounting was provided. This is what happens when policy is announcement-driven rather than implementation-driven — PADU made a great headline on launch day, and the fact that it was insecure, incomplete, and ultimately unusable was treated as everyone’s forgotten problem.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia’s government IT track record is a graveyard of expensive failures. The MyKad system has been plagued by data issues for years. The MySejahtera COVID app was mired in ownership and data privacy controversies. Government websites are routinely hacked or defaced. Each project follows the same pattern: launch with fanfare, discover flaws, patch reactively, and eventually abandon. PADU is the most expensive iteration of this cycle — a national database built without adequate security, launched without adequate testing, and shelved without adequate explanation.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You spent hundreds of millions building a national database. It launched with security so weak anyone could access anyone’s data. Sarawak refused to participate. And when your biggest subsidy reform came, you didn’t even use it. Publish the total cost of PADU. Explain why it was sidelined. Conduct a public postmortem on what went wrong. Or keep launching headline-grabbing digital initiatives that cost billions and deliver nothing — while the rakyat’s personal data sits in a database that couldn’t even verify who was accessing it.

Sources: CybersecurityAsia | ISEAS/Fulcrum | Malay Mail

Death Penalty Still on Books for 27 Offences — 'Abolition' Was Only Half-Done

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim Administration

Category: Social

Date: 2023-07-04

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 4 July 2023, PH trumpeted the “abolition” of the mandatory death penalty — replacing it with discretionary sentencing for capital offences. However, the death penalty itself remains on the books for 27 offences, including drug trafficking, murder, and firearms offences. Judges now have discretion to impose death or alternative sentences, but the gallows remain available. Amnesty International called for full abolition. In late 2025, Malaysia announced a “study” on full abolition to begin in January 2026 — three years after the initial reform.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: PH framed the mandatory death penalty reform as a landmark human rights achievement. In reality, it was a half-measure. Removing the word “mandatory” does not abolish the death penalty — it transfers the decision from statute to judicial discretion. Judges in Malaysia’s court system, which PH itself has criticised for political interference, now decide who lives and who dies. For a government that claims to champion human rights, maintaining the death penalty for 27 offences while announcing a “study” on abolition — three years after reform — is not progress. It’s procrastination branded as principle.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The “study” announced for January 2026 is the Malaysian government’s default delaying tactic: announce a study, form a committee, wait for the news cycle to move on, and never implement the findings. If PH believed in abolition, it would have abolished the death penalty entirely in 2023 when it had the parliamentary numbers and the public momentum. Instead, it chose the politically safe option — remove “mandatory,” keep the penalty, and claim credit for reform. Amnesty International saw through it immediately. The rakyat should too.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia has executed prisoners under every administration. Under BN, the death penalty was used primarily for drug trafficking — disproportionately affecting poor, marginalised, and foreign defendants. PH 1.0 initiated discussions on abolition but collapsed before acting. PH 2.0 had three years to complete the work. Instead, it settled for discretionary sentencing and a future “study.” The pattern is consistent: Malaysian governments acknowledge the death penalty is problematic, make incremental changes, and never finish the job.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You called it abolition. It wasn’t. The death penalty remains for 27 offences. You announced a “study” on full abolition — three years after your initial reform. If you need three years to study whether the state should kill its citizens, the answer is already no. Abolish the death penalty for all offences. Commute existing death sentences. Stop studying and start acting. Or admit that “abolition” was a press release, not a policy.

Sources: Amnesty International | Reuters | Bernama
