Nearly RM1 Billion Paid in Court Damages for Government Negligence — Anwar's Fix? Letters of Reprimand

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister and Finance Minister

Category: Economy

Date: 2026-03-04

📌 What Happened

Prime Minister and Finance Minister Anwar Ibrahim disclosed in a written parliamentary reply — responding to PH’s own William Leong (Selayang) — that the Malaysian government paid out RM933,360,633 in court-awarded damages between 2020 and 2025. The payouts were the result of negligence, misconduct, and other wrongdoing by government officials and agencies. Anwar outlined measures including instructing ministries to conduct investigations into the cases and “recommend disciplinary action” against those found negligent — and, spectacularly, issuing letters of reprimand.

🔥 Why It Matters

Nearly RM1 billion of public money was handed over because government officials were negligent, irresponsible, or outright corrupt in carrying out their duties — and the government’s proposed accountability mechanism is a sternly worded letter. This figure spans the administrations of Muhyiddin, Ismail Sabri, and now Anwar himself. The fact that this disclosure came buried in a written parliamentary reply rather than proactively announced tells you everything about how seriously this government takes fiscal accountability. PH promised to end the culture of impunity. Instead, it’s inherited it and furnished it with a fresh coat of bureaucratic language.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

Letters of reprimand. For nearly a billion ringgit in losses caused by government negligence. Let that sink in. In the private sector, a single case of professional negligence resulting in significant financial loss would trigger terminations, lawsuits, and regulatory action. In Anwar’s Malaysia, it triggers a stern memo. This isn’t accountability — it’s theatre. If PH were serious about reform, it would name the ministries involved, disclose the individual cases, surcharge the officers responsible, and refer criminal negligence to the relevant authorities. Instead, the Prime Minister filed a quiet written reply and moved on. The rakyat pays the bill; nobody pays the price.

📜 Previous Cases

Malaysia has a long, inglorious history of government negligence costing taxpayers billions — from the PKFZ scandal (RM12.5 billion in cost overruns and soft loans) to the endless saga of abandoned government projects and botched procurement exercises under successive administrations. PH itself campaigned on ending this cycle in both 2018 and 2022. During PH 1.0 under Mahathir, the government conducted an audit of 1MDB-era spending but quietly shelved follow-up enforcement. Now under PH 2.0, Anwar’s government discloses the losses but proposes remedies so weak they amount to an invitation to keep doing it.

🎯 PH Callout

You promised the rakyat you would be different. You said there would be consequences. You said clean governance wasn’t a slogan — it was a commitment. So where are the consequences for nearly RM1 billion in court-ordered damages caused by your own government machinery? Letters of reprimand are what you give someone who parks in the wrong spot, not officials whose negligence cost the nation hundreds of millions. Name the ministries. Publish the cases. Surcharge the officers. Show the country that “Madani” means something beyond a logo on a government letterhead.

5G Dual Network Fiasco — DNB vs TM Contract War Over Termination

Said by: Digital Nasional Bhd / Telekom Malaysia

Category: Economy

Date: 2026-03-03

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 3 March 2026, Telekom Malaysia (TM) attempted to terminate its 5G wholesale agreement with Digital Nasional Berhad (DNB), the government entity managing Malaysia’s 5G rollout. DNB rejected the termination, saying the contract runs until 2032 and remains binding. Malaysia’s dual 5G network strategy — introduced after Anwar’s government reversed the original single-network plan — has been plagued by policy flip-flops, RM2 billion in DNB debt, and the controversial awarding of the second 5G network to U Mobile.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Malaysia’s 5G policy has become a case study in how political interference destroys industrial strategy. The original single-network plan under DNB was already controversial. Anwar’s government then introduced a “dual network” approach — splitting the rollout between DNB and a consortium — which created contractual chaos, duplication of infrastructure, and uncertainty for telcos. Now TM and DNB are fighting in public over who owes what to whom. While politicians argue about contracts, Malaysians still have patchy 5G coverage and telcos still don’t have clarity on their obligations.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The dual network decision was widely seen as a political move to accommodate UMNO-linked business interests rather than a technical improvement. U Mobile’s selection for the second network was questioned by industry analysts. DNB’s RM2 billion debt was accumulated under a plan the government itself designed. Now the government is watching its own creation implode in a contractual dispute — and Malaysians are paying for it through delayed rollout and higher costs. This is what happens when telecoms policy is driven by patronage rather than engineering.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia’s telecoms sector has a long history of politically motivated decisions. The award of 3G and 4G licences was repeatedly criticised for favouring politically connected firms. DNB itself was created under Muhyiddin’s government with minimal transparency. Anwar’s dual network decision was supposed to fix this — instead, it doubled the problems. The pattern: create a government entity, load it with debt, let it fight with the private sector, blame the previous administration.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You inherited a flawed 5G plan and made it worse. The dual network was your decision. The contractual chaos is your responsibility. While DNB and TM fight over termination clauses, Malaysians are stuck with incomplete 5G infrastructure and a RM2 billion bill. Fix the policy, resolve the contracts, and stop letting political interests dictate telecoms strategy. Or admit that 5G in Malaysia is just another patronage project with better branding.

The Scoreboard: Things That Were BETTER Under BN Than PH — An Uncomfortable Truth

Said by: Comparative Economic Data / Various Sources

Category: Economy

Date: 2026-03-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: By multiple measurable metrics, ordinary Malaysians were objectively better off under certain BN policies than under PH: (1) GST was more efficient and less regressive than SST; (2) Diesel was RM2.15 vs RM3.35; (3) Festive toll-free periods existed; (4) PISA scores were 31 points higher in math; (5) Food prices were lower; (6) RON95 subsidy was universal without the anxiety of ’targeted’ reform; (7) Electricity for domestic use was GST-exempt.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: The fundamental promise of changing government is that things will get BETTER, not worse. When multiple cost-of-living and quality-of-life indicators are worse under PH than under the government they replaced, the entire rationale for PH’s existence comes into question. Voters didn’t march for Reformasi so they could pay more for diesel.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: PH will argue context — global inflation, inherited debt, COVID aftermath. These are real factors. But they don’t explain why Malaysia’s food prices are the HIGHEST in Southeast Asia (surpassing richer Singapore), why PISA decline was DOUBLE the OECD average, or why diesel had to rise 56% in one shot while JAKIM kept RM2.6 billion. Context explains some decline; it doesn’t excuse all of it. Choices were made, and those choices made life worse.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: BN was voted out for corruption, abuse of power, and 1MDB — all legitimate reasons. But the implicit promise was that PH would maintain or improve the baseline while cleaning up governance. Instead, governance remains corrupt (Shamsul Iskandar, MACC scandal, GLC patronage) AND daily life costs more. You got the worst of both worlds: BN-level corruption with higher costs.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Here’s your report card. Diesel: WORSE. Food prices: WORSE. Education outcomes: WORSE. Press freedom: SAME or WORSE. Corruption: SAME. Cost of living: WORSE. Toll abolition: BROKEN. EPF protection: WEAKER. Healthcare: WORSE. The one metric where you’re better? PR and speechwriting. Congratulations — your press conferences are excellent. Too bad Malaysians can’t eat press conferences.

Category: Economy

Date: 2026-02-23

Zafrul accused Rafizi of ‘distorting the facts’ and told him to ‘stop spinning — it’s exhausting to keep responding.’ Rafizi had argued the ART locked Malaysia into unfavourable terms. Zafrul countered that the 19% tariff no longer applied after the US Supreme Court struck down Trump’s tariff framework.

This isn’t policy debate; it’s a proxy war between PH factions using trade policy as ammunition. When your own former ministers can’t agree on whether a deal you signed was good or bad, the rakyat has every right to ask: who was actually in charge of trade policy?

Rafizi is right that the broader deal surrendered too much. But neither is addressing the real problem: the deal was rushed, poorly communicated, and published on the White House website before Malaysian MPs saw it. The public spat between two PH elites is entertainment, not accountability.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: The Zafrul-Rafizi rivalry predates this deal. Rafizi publicly warned that Farhash allegations undermined the PM’s credibility; Zafrul stayed silent. Now Rafizi attacks Zafrul’s signature achievement. PH’s internal dysfunction is becoming its defining feature — they fight each other more effectively than they fight corruption.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: When your former ministers are publicly calling each other liars over a trade deal you signed four months ago, the problem isn’t the tariff rate — it’s the coalition. Fix your internal communications before you negotiate with superpowers. And stop making the rakyat watch your dirty laundry air on Facebook.

MAHB Delisted After GDA Takeover — Five Directors Resign in Protest

Said by: Bursa Malaysia / GDA Consortium

Category: Economy

Date: 2026-02-18

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 18 February 2026, Malaysia Airports Holdings Berhad (MAHB) — the public airport operator managing 39 airports nationwide — was officially delisted from Bursa Malaysia after the GDA consortium completed its takeover at RM11 per share. The consortium was led by Khazanah Nasional and EPF, with BlackRock’s Global Infrastructure Partners (GIP) as a key partner. All five independent directors resigned in protest, having deemed the offer “unfair and unreasonable” to minority shareholders. Hong Leong Investment Bank’s independent assessment concluded the offer was “not fair but reasonable” — a diplomatic way of saying shareholders were getting shortchanged but had no better option. Civil society groups protested BlackRock’s GIP involvement amid Palestine boycott concerns.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: A strategic national asset — the operator of every commercial airport in Malaysia — is now privately controlled with drastically reduced public accountability. When MAHB was listed, its financials, board decisions, and performance were subject to Bursa disclosure rules and public scrutiny. That transparency is gone. The mass resignation of all five independent directors is extraordinary and nearly unprecedented in Malaysian corporate history. These are professionals who staked their reputations on saying the deal was bad for minority shareholders. Yet the government-linked consortium pushed it through anyway. When the people whose job is to protect shareholders resign in protest, the message is unambiguous.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government used public money (Khazanah and EPF are government-controlled) to take private a public asset — reducing accountability while using rakyat’s retirement savings to fund the deal. EPF manages the savings of millions of Malaysian workers. Those workers are now involuntary investors in a delisted company with no market price transparency and no public reporting requirements. The “not fair but reasonable” assessment is corporate speak for “you’re getting a bad deal but you can’t stop it.” And the Palestine boycott angle — BlackRock’s GIP involvement drew protests from the same communities Anwar courts on Gaza solidarity. The government that lectures the world about Palestine partnered with BlackRock to privatise Malaysian airports. The hypocrisy is breathtaking.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia has a long history of controversial privatisations and delistings of strategic assets. Tenaga Nasional, Telekom Malaysia, and various highway concessionaires have all been subject to ownership restructuring that reduced public oversight. The MAHB delisting follows the same playbook: government-linked entities acquire public companies at prices independent assessors deem unfair, minority shareholders are squeezed out, and accountability disappears behind private boardroom doors. Under BN, this was called cronyism. Under PH, it is called “strategic restructuring.”

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Five independent directors resigned saying the deal was unfair. Hong Leong said it was “not fair.” You used EPF — workers’ retirement money — to fund a deal that independent voices said was bad for shareholders. MAHB manages every airport in Malaysia; Malaysians deserve transparency over how it is run. Commit to publishing MAHB’s financials annually even after delisting. Commit to independent board oversight. Or admit that this was a deal designed to benefit the consortium at the expense of the public — exactly the kind of crony capitalism you promised to end.

Malaysia Study: Cost-Benefit Analysis of US Trade Deal Shows 'Concerns' But Government Says Don't Quit

Said by: Malaysian Government

Category: Economy

Date: 2026-01-27

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: In January 2026, Bloomberg reported that the Malaysian government announced a formal cost-benefit analysis of the controversial US-Malaysia Agreed Reciprocal Trade (ART) agreement — following months of mounting criticism from former AG Tommy Thomas (who called it “the worst agreement since independence”), former PM Mahathir, think tanks, and opposition politicians. But in the same breath, the government warned lawmakers not to quit the pact. East Asia Forum published a detailed analysis titled “The Art of the Deal Lost in US-Malaysia Pact,” arguing Malaysia surrendered critical mineral export controls, digital services taxation rights, and independent trade policy in exchange for a tariff reduction from 25% to 19% — still punitive by any standard. The deal’s details were first published on the White House website before being shared with Malaysian Parliament.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Announcing a cost-benefit analysis while pre-empting its conclusion is not governance — it is political theatre. “Study but don’t quit” tells you everything about this administration’s approach: acknowledge problems, change nothing. If the analysis shows the deal hurts Malaysia, will the government act? The answer is already in the framing. The study exists to absorb pressure, not to inform decisions. The fact that Parliament learned about the deal from the White House website is a governance failure of the first order — trade agreements that constrain national sovereignty should require parliamentary approval before signing, not after.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: “Study but don’t quit” is the Madani playbook in miniature: acknowledge the problem, promise a process, change nothing. It is the same approach applied to the AG-PP separation, UUCA repeal, toll abolition, and every other stalled reform. The cost-benefit analysis will either be buried, selectively quoted, or used to justify minor adjustments while the core agreement remains untouched. Anwar bypassed the legislature on the most consequential economic agreement in decades. When your own former AG calls your trade deal the worst since independence and your response is “we’ll study it but we’re keeping it,” the study is performative.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia has a history of trade agreements where the government commits first and reviews later. The TPPA under Najib faced similar criticism — Malaysia signed on to provisions critics said undermined sovereignty, pharmaceutical pricing, and labour rights. PH 1.0 withdrew from the TPPA in 2018, calling it unfair. PH 2.0 signed the ART — which critics say contains even more restrictive provisions — and now wants to “study” whether it was a good idea. The pattern of committing to deals under pressure, then conducting post-hoc reviews that change nothing, is a bipartisan Malaysian tradition.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: If you’re serious about the cost-benefit analysis, publish the full terms of the ART — not the White House summary, but the actual agreement. Let independent economists, not government-appointed task forces, evaluate the deal. If the analysis shows Malaysia got a bad deal, have the courage to renegotiate. “Study but don’t quit” is not a policy — it is a confession that you already know the answer and don’t want to act on it. Parliament deserves a vote on trade agreements that constrain national sovereignty. Give them one.

Wages Haven't Kept Up with Costs — Real Purchasing Power Has DECLINED Under PH

Said by: DOSM / BNM / Consumer Groups

Category: Economy

Date: 2026-01-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: While nominal wages have increased slightly under PH — minimum wage raised to RM1,500, modest salary increments across sectors — the cost of living has risen faster. Diesel is up 56%. SST was expanded to cover more essentials. Food prices are the highest in Southeast Asia at $1,940 per person per year according to USDA data. Electricity costs are up. Property prices are up. The result: real purchasing power — what your ringgit actually buys — has declined for most Malaysians. A 2025 consumer survey found 77% of Malaysians say costs have risen significantly, while official inflation claims just 1.3%.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Purchasing power is the truest measure of economic wellbeing. GDP growth, investment figures, and export numbers are abstractions. What matters to a family is whether they can afford the same basket of groceries this month as last month. Under PH, the answer is increasingly no. When 71.6% of household income goes to daily necessities, disposable income for savings, education, and emergencies shrinks to almost nothing. Families are one medical emergency away from crisis. The government celebrates macro numbers while the rakyat counts sen at the checkout counter.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government points to headline inflation of around 1.3% and claims prices are “stable.” But official CPI calculations underweight items that have risen most sharply — food, fuel, services — and don’t capture the “felt” inflation that families experience. When 77% of Malaysians say costs have risen significantly but official inflation says 1.3%, somebody is lying — and it’s not the 77%. Food away from home inflation runs at 4.3-4.7%, far exceeding the headline rate. The government is using statistical methodology to gaslight the population about their own lived experience. Meanwhile, PH abolished GST (which zero-rated essentials) and replaced it with SST, which taxes MORE daily items. Under BN, diesel was RM2.15. Under PH, it’s RM3.35.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Under BN’s GST system, essentials were zero-rated — consumers paid no tax on basic food, domestic electricity, and public transport. PH abolished GST as a populist move and replaced it with SST, which is less efficient and taxes more daily items. The uncomfortable truth: by many daily-life cost measures, BN’s baseline was better than PH’s performance. Every Malaysian government has faced cost-of-living complaints, but PH uniquely promised to make life cheaper — then made it more expensive through diesel float, SST expansion, and subsidy rationalisation without adequate targeted relief.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You promised to make life better for ordinary Malaysians. Life is MORE expensive. Diesel costs MORE. Food costs MORE. Services cost MORE through expanded SST. Electricity costs MORE. And wages haven’t kept up. Every Malaysian family knows this — they see it every time they buy groceries, fill their tank, or pay their bills. Your 1.3% inflation statistic is an insult to the 77% of Malaysians who know their money buys less than it did three years ago. Stop hiding behind macro numbers and address the lived reality of the rakyat.

Public Transport 'Functional But Fragile' — One Disruption and Everyone Drives

Said by: Khazanah Research Institute (KRI)

Category: Economy

Date: 2025-12-31

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: A Khazanah Research Institute (KRI) study published in late December 2025 found that Greater KL’s public transport system is “functional but fragile” — minor disruptions cause commuters to abandon it entirely for private vehicles. KRI concluded that rail was “never meant to fix Malaysia’s traffic problem.” The study found that fuel remains artificially cheap, parking is abundant and affordable, walking environments are fragmented and hostile, and last-mile connectivity is inadequate. FMT’s analysis argued it is “an unfinished system, not infrastructure, that fails Malaysian commuters.” Yet the government continues announcing mega rail projects — MRT3, LRT3, ECRL — as if building more tracks alone will solve mobility.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Successive governments, including PH, keep announcing multi-billion ringgit rail projects as if infrastructure alone solves mobility. KRI’s own study — funded by the government’s own sovereign wealth fund — says it doesn’t. Without simultaneously reforming urban planning, parking policy, fuel pricing, and last-mile connectivity, every new rail line is just an expensive band-aid on a systemic failure. Billions are spent on tracks while the system remains one disruption away from collapse. Commuters who try public transport once and get stranded never come back.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The government subsidises fuel (keeping driving artificially cheap), allows abundant free parking in urban areas (removing the cost incentive to use public transport), neglects pedestrian infrastructure (making it dangerous to walk to stations), and fails to invest in last-mile solutions (leaving commuters stranded between stations and destinations). Then it announces another RM50 billion rail project and claims it is “solving traffic.” This is not a transport policy — it is an infrastructure spending programme that benefits construction companies and politicians who cut ribbons, not commuters who need to get to work. LRT3 has been delayed repeatedly. MRT3 costs keep escalating. The ECRL’s ridership projections are fantasy. KRI told the government the approach doesn’t work, and the government responded by announcing more of the same.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia has been building rail infrastructure for decades — KTM Komuter, LRT, Monorail, MRT1, MRT2 — yet car ownership rates keep climbing and traffic congestion keeps worsening. Each new project is announced with the same promise: this one will finally solve traffic. None has. The reason is structural: Malaysia’s urban planning is car-centric by design. Without changing the design — restricting parking, raising fuel costs, improving walkability, building bus networks — adding rail lines is like building a bigger bucket while the tap keeps running.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Your own government-funded think tank says rail alone doesn’t solve traffic. Yet your transport policy is: build more rail. Reform parking policy. Reform fuel pricing. Fund bus networks. Fix pedestrian infrastructure. These are the boring, politically difficult changes that would actually make public transport work. But they don’t generate ribbon-cutting photo ops, so they don’t happen. Stop building expensive monuments to political vanity and start building a transport SYSTEM.

Toll Abolition Promise Broken — Govt Pays RM591M to FREEZE Hikes Instead

Said by: Fahmi Fadzil, Communications Minister

Category: Economy

Date: 2025-12-18

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: PH promised to abolish highway tolls in both GE14 and GE15. Instead, in December 2025, the government announced it would spend RM591 million just to prevent toll increases on 10 highways in 2026 — paying concessionaires with public money to not raise prices. The Works Minister admitted that fully abolishing tolls would cost “billions.” Gerakan mocked the broken promise. FMT asked whether Anwar would “repeat PH’s pledge to abolish tolls” ahead of GE16. Malaysian drivers continue paying tolls AND their taxes now subsidise the freeze. Festive toll-free periods were also cancelled.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Toll abolition was one of PH’s most concrete, measurable manifesto promises — specific enough that voters could easily verify delivery. The reality: not only were tolls not abolished, the government is paying highway concessionaires hundreds of millions annually just to freeze rate hikes. The RM591M spent on freezing could have funded actual infrastructure improvements. Instead, it flows to concessionaire shareholders — politically connected companies that profit from a captive public. This is the worst possible outcome: broken promise, continued tolls, AND public money transferred to private companies.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The Works Minister’s admission that abolition would cost “billions” is an admission that PH made the promise knowing it was undeliverable. You can’t campaign on toll abolition twice, win government twice, and then say it costs too much. Either you knew the cost before promising (in which case you lied to voters) or you didn’t bother calculating it (in which case you’re incompetent). The toll system represents a regressive tax on mobility — lower-income workers who commute long distances to factory jobs pay a disproportionate share of their income on tolls. PH’s response to this inequality: pay concessionaires with tax money to maintain the status quo.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: The toll abolition promise has been made by every opposition coalition since the early 2000s. PH 1.0 (2018-2020) didn’t abolish tolls either — despite 22 months in government. The concession agreements, many awarded under previous administrations through questionable processes, are structured to guarantee private profits at public expense. No government has had the political will to renegotiate or buy back concessions. The concessionaires know this, which is why they keep raising toll rates and governments keep paying them not to.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You promised to abolish tolls. Twice. In two general elections. You’ve now been in power for over three years. Tolls are still there. You’re spending RM591M of public money to FREEZE them — not abolish them, freeze them. That RM591M goes to concessionaire shareholders, not to the rakyat. Stop making promises you know you can’t keep. If toll abolition is unaffordable, say so honestly — and explain your alternative plan. Or better yet, renegotiate the concession agreements that lock Malaysia into paying private companies forever. You won’t, because the concessionaires are politically connected. So the rakyat pays twice: once at the toll booth, once through taxes.

LRT3 Delayed AGAIN — Software Glitches, Failed Test Runs, RM475M Penalties Looming

Said by: Anthony Loke, Transport Minister

Category: Economy

Date: 2025-12-15

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: The LRT3 line from Bandar Utama to Johan Setia — a multi-billion ringgit project intended to transform connectivity in the Klang Valley — has been postponed repeatedly due to signalling software failures and the inability to complete fault-free test runs. Transport Minister Anthony Loke warned contractors may face penalties exceeding RM475 million for the delays. The opening date remains unconfirmed. The Edge and Lowyat both reported the latest round of testing setbacks as the system failed to achieve the required reliability benchmarks.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: LRT3 was supposed to be operational years ago. Repeated delays mean commuters continue relying on cars, worsening congestion and pollution in the Klang Valley. Public money is tied up in a system nobody can use — billions invested with zero return to commuters. The signalling software failures suggest deeper issues with project management, contractor capability, and government oversight. Meanwhile, commuters who were told LRT3 would change their lives continue sitting in traffic.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Loke’s penalty threats ring hollow. The government has a track record of renegotiating rather than enforcing consequences on mega-project contractors. Penalties get waived, deadlines get extended, cost overruns get absorbed by the public, and nobody is held accountable. Infrastructure delays under PH are no different from infrastructure delays under BN — the “reform” government delivers the same results with the same contractors and the same excuses. If the RM475M in penalties are actually enforced, it would be the first time in Malaysian infrastructure history. Don’t hold your breath.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia’s mega-infrastructure project history is a catalogue of delays, cost overruns, and renegotiated contracts. MRT2 was delayed. The ECRL was renegotiated (costs ballooned from RM55B to reportedly higher). The Penang Transport Master Plan is stalled. LRT3 now joins the list. The pattern across administrations: announce ambitious projects, award contracts to politically connected firms, miss deadlines, threaten penalties, renegotiate quietly, and repeat. The contractors get paid regardless. The public gets delays regardless. Only the minister cutting the ribbon changes.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: If RM475M in penalties are owed, collect them. If the contractor can’t deliver, replace them. If the signalling software doesn’t work after years of testing, find out why and publish the findings. Stop threatening consequences you never enforce. Infrastructure accountability starts with one simple principle: contracts that aren’t met should have real consequences. Under BN, they didn’t. Under PH, they still don’t. Prove us wrong. Enforce the RM475M. We dare you.
