US-Malaysia Trade Deal Called 'Worst Agreement Since Independence' and 'Act of Surrender'

Said by: Tommy Thomas, Former Attorney General (and critics)

Category: Economy

Date: 2025-10-26

The Deal That Sold Malaysia’s Sovereignty

On 26 October 2025, Anwar Ibrahim and Donald Trump signed the US-Malaysia Agreement on Reciprocal Trade (ART) at the ASEAN Summit. Former Attorney-General Tommy Thomas called it the worst agreement Malaysia has entered since independence in 1957. He was not exaggerating.

The Facts

Fact: The deal maintains a 19% US tariff on Malaysian goods — down from 25%, but still punitive by any standard. Malaysia’s effective tariff on US goods before this deal was roughly 6%. The US got the better end by a factor of three.

Fact: Malaysia committed to: enforce US tariff evasion rules against third countries, align with US export controls and sanctions regimes, not ban or restrict exports of critical minerals or rare earths to the US, refrain from imposing digital services taxes on US tech companies, and purchase additional American goods and services.

Fact: A termination clause allows the US to reimpose the original 25% tariff if Malaysia enters a trade deal that “jeopardizes essential US interests.” This effectively gives Washington veto power over Malaysia’s independent trade policy — including with China, Malaysia’s largest trading partner.

Fact: The deal’s full text was first published on the White House website before being shared with Malaysian Parliament. Malaysian lawmakers learned the terms of their own trade agreement from a foreign government’s press release.

Fact: Former PM Mahathir said the provisions amounted to “handing over” the country’s independence. Even for a man who oversaw his own share of questionable deals, this one was a bridge too far.

Fact: East Asia Forum’s analysis, titled “The Art of the Deal Lost in US-Malaysia Pact,” argued Malaysia surrendered critical mineral export controls, digital services taxation rights, and independent trade policy in exchange for a tariff cut that still left Malaysia paying three times what the US pays.

Fact: The Guardian reported that Anwar “defended” the deal by arguing it was “the best Malaysia could get under the circumstances” — an argument that concedes the deal is bad while claiming it could have been worse.

Fact: SCMP reported Anwar “slammed” critics for accusing him of surrendering sovereignty — but never addressed the substance of their arguments, only their tone.

Why It Matters

  • This is not a reciprocal deal by any definition of the word. Malaysia makes extensive, binding concessions across minerals, digital taxation, sanctions alignment, and procurement. The US reduces a punitive tariff by 6 percentage points and retains the right to reimpose it at will. In any negotiation textbook, this is called capitulation.
  • The sovereignty clause is the most dangerous provision. If Malaysia wants to deepen trade ties with China — its largest trading partner — the US can terminate the ART and reimpose 25% tariffs. Malaysia has effectively given Washington a veto over its foreign economic policy.
  • Publishing the details on the White House website before informing Parliament is not just embarrassing — it is a constitutional affront. Trade agreements that constrain national sovereignty should require parliamentary debate and approval before signing. Anwar bypassed the legislature on the most consequential economic agreement in decades.
  • Every ASEAN neighbour is watching. Indonesia walked away from a similar US proposal. Vietnam negotiated harder. Thailand delayed. Malaysia signed first and asked questions later. This weakens Malaysia’s negotiating position in every future bilateral and multilateral trade discussion.
  • The “best we could get” defence is an admission of weakness, not a justification. If this was truly the best outcome, it means Malaysia’s diplomatic leverage is weaker than the government has claimed — which raises questions about the entire Madani foreign policy brand. *Sources: The Guardian · East Asia Forum · SCMP · *Straits Times

77% of Malaysians Struggle With Rising Living Costs — But Anwar Says Economy Is 'Strong'

Said by: Consumer Frontiers International survey

Category: Economy

Date: 2025-10-15

The 77% vs The GDP

In October 2025, a survey found that 77% of Malaysians said they struggled with rising living costs. In the same month, the government was citing GDP growth and investment figures as evidence that the economy was strong.

The Facts

Fact: Consumer Frontiers International found 77% of Malaysians struggled with rising costs despite rising wages.

Fact: DOSM reported average monthly disposable household income increased 3.2% to RM7,584 in 2024.

Fact: But economist Lee Heng Guie warned: “We must also recognise that the cost of living continues to rise.” Real gains were being eroded by inflation.

Fact: The Star headlined it: “Rising cost of living dampens income gains.”

Why It Matters

  • GDP growth and approved investments are macro numbers. They do not reflect what 77% of the population is experiencing.
  • When three-quarters of your citizens say they’re struggling, citing investment figures is tone-deaf.
  • The gap between government statistics and lived experience is the gap that creates protest movements. *Sources: Business Today · *The Star

MITI Accused of Stealing World Expo Osaka Pavilion Concept — Creative Agency Serves Letter of Demand

Said by: Tengku Zafrul Abdul Aziz, MITI Minister / Current Media Group

Category: Economy

Date: 2025-08-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: In August 2025, MITI under Tengku Zafrul was served a letter of demand by Current Media Group (CMG), a creative consultancy that claimed the ministry stole its concept for Malaysia’s World Expo 2025 Osaka pavilion without providing recognition or payment. The allegation was that CMG’s original pitch was used as the basis for the pavilion design without a contract or credit. Zafrul addressed the matter in a parliamentary reply to Kepong MP Lim Lip Eng.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: If a government ministry used a private company’s intellectual property without payment or credit, that is theft — regardless of whether it’s called ‘inspiration’ or ‘parallel development.’ World Expo pavilions are high-profile national showcases with significant budgets. The allegation that MITI took a concept without paying for it raises serious questions about procurement integrity and intellectual property respect within the ministry.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: The ‘reformist’ government that lectures the world about good governance and fair dealing allegedly stole a creative concept from a Malaysian company. PH came to power promising transparent procurement and respect for due process. If MITI can’t even commission a pavilion design without a letter of demand from the original creator, what does that say about how other, larger contracts are handled behind closed doors?

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Government IP theft from private contractors is a recurring complaint in Malaysia. Creative agencies, architects, and consultants have long reported that government pitches are treated as free work — ideas are taken, contracts go elsewhere, and the original creators are ghosted. Under BN, this was standard. Under PH, it was supposed to change.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You represent Malaysia on the world stage at Osaka and you can’t even pay the people who designed it? If the allegation is true, compensate CMG and investigate how this happened. If it’s false, release the procurement documents. Transparency works both ways — you don’t get to lecture the world about governance while your own ministry is served letters of demand for IP theft.

Sources: Marketing Interactive

Electricity Tariffs Hiked for Industry on Same Day as SST Expansion — 'Hotpot of Bad News'

Said by: Ministry of Finance

Category: Economy

Date: 2025-07-01

The Hotpot of Bad News

On 1 July 2025, Malaysia was hit by a double whammy: the expanded SST took effect, covering rental, healthcare, education, and more, alongside increased electricity tariffs for the industrial sector. The Straits Times called it a “hotpot of bad economic news.”

The Facts

Fact: SST expansion took effect 1 July 2025, taxing six new service categories including rental, construction, financial services, private healthcare, and education.

Fact: On the same day, electricity tariffs increased for the industrial sector.

Fact: The government was also planning to reduce cooking gas subsidies for commercial eateries and implement the RON95 subsidy reform by year end.

Fact: The Straits Times noted the “widening of the SST is just the latest controversial move” by the government.

Why It Matters

  • Any one of these measures is manageable. All of them on the same timeline is a cost of living assault.
  • The cumulative effect on small businesses is devastating: higher tax, higher electricity, higher fuel, and reduced subsidies.
  • The government says each measure is “necessary.” But necessity without sequencing is still bad governance. *Source: *Straits Times
