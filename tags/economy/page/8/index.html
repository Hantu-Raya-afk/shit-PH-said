Immigration Corruption Syndicates Thrive — Foreign Worker Exploitation a Multi-Billion Ringgit Industry

Said by: Enforcement Agencies / Media Investigations

Category: Economy

Date: 2025-03-01

📌 What Happened

Malaysia’s immigration system remains plagued by corruption syndicates that exploit millions of foreign workers, extracting billions of ringgit through illegal levies, document fraud, and labour trafficking. Despite repeated government promises to clean up the system, investigations continue to reveal deeply entrenched networks involving immigration officials, labour agents, and employers.

🔥 Why It Matters

Foreign workers — numbering an estimated 2-3 million documented and millions more undocumented — pay exorbitant recruitment fees, often becoming indentured servants trapped in cycles of debt bondage. The multi-tier foreign worker levy system has been delayed repeatedly, creating uncertainty that benefits corrupt intermediaries. US State Department Trafficking in Persons reports have consistently flagged Malaysia’s forced labour issues, particularly in plantation agriculture, construction, and manufacturing.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

The government’s response cycle is predictable: announce a crackdown, conduct a few high-profile raids, issue stern statements, then allow the system to return to business as usual. Immigration corruption is a structural problem requiring systemic reform of recruitment processes, levy collection, and worker protection — not periodic enforcement theatre.

🎯 PH Callout

You inherited a broken system and promised to fix it. Instead, the syndicates still operate, the workers still suffer, and the billions still flow to corrupt intermediaries. If you can’t clean up your own immigration department, stop pretending you’re a reform government.

Proton Still Dependent on Government Protections — 40 Years of 'National Car' and Still Can't Compete

Said by: Automotive Industry Analysts

Category: Economy

Date: 2025-01-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: After 40 years and billions in public subsidies, tax exemptions, and protectionist trade policies, Proton still cannot compete internationally without government protection. High import duties — excise duties of up to 100% on foreign cars — inflate vehicle prices for 33 million Malaysians. The same car costs half as much in Thailand or Indonesia. Anwar’s government shows no inclination to reform automotive trade policies or phase out protections that cost Malaysian consumers billions annually.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Car ownership is not a luxury in Malaysia — it is a necessity in a country with inadequate public transport outside KL. High import duties mean Malaysian families spend a disproportionate share of their income on vehicles. A Honda City that costs RM65,000 in Thailand costs over RM100,000 in Malaysia. A Toyota Corolla in Indonesia costs about RM80,000; in Malaysia, over RM130,000. The “national car” policy forces 33 million people to subsidise one company’s inability to compete — through higher prices on every vehicle they buy.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Proton was supposed to be a temporary infant industry — protected for a few years until it could stand on its own. Forty years later, it still can’t stand on its own. The company was sold to Geely (China) in 2017 because it couldn’t survive without a foreign partner. Yet import duties remain, protecting a company that is no longer even fully Malaysian-owned. The protection no longer serves a national champion — it serves a joint venture with a Chinese automaker while Malaysian consumers pay inflated prices for every car on the road.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: The national car project has been a political sacred cow since Mahathir launched Proton in 1983. Every government has maintained protections because the automotive lobby — dealers, suppliers, workers — represents votes. BN protected Proton for 35 years. PH 1.0 made no changes. PH 2.0 has made no changes. The policy survives not because it works but because dismantling it would anger connected interests. Meanwhile, Thailand and Indonesia — without national car protections — have become major automotive manufacturing hubs. Malaysia has not.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Malaysian families pay double for cars because of a 40-year-old policy that protects a company now half-owned by China. Public transport is inadequate. Car ownership is mandatory. And you maintain import duties that inflate prices for everyone. Phase out automotive protections over 5-10 years. Invest the savings in public transport. Let Malaysian consumers buy cars at regional prices. Or keep forcing 33 million people to subsidise a failed industrial policy — and wonder why cost of living is your biggest political problem.

Nga Kor Ming Blows RM4 Million on 4 'BMW-Class Smart Toilets' While Rakyat Struggles With Cost of Living

Said by: Nga Kor Ming, Housing and Local Government Minister

Category: Economy

Date: 2024-11-14

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: In November 2024, Housing and Local Government Minister Nga Kor Ming announced that RM4 million had been allocated to build four “BMW-class” smart toilets in Kuala Lumpur and Putrajaya — RM1 million per toilet. He made the announcement at the Toilet of the Year Award (TOTYA) 2024 ceremony, describing it as part of a broader RM180 million allocation over three years for public toilet upgrades. By June 2025, former MCA vice-president Ti Lian Ker demanded an update, noting zero progress reports since the announcement.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: RM1 million per toilet. The average Malaysian household income is around RM7,000-8,000 per month. The government is spending the equivalent of over 10 years of a household’s income on a single toilet, while telling the rakyat to tighten their belts. The RM180 million national toilet upgrade is a legitimate use of funds. But turning four toilets into “BMW-class” showpieces at RM1 million each — in KL and Putrajaya, the places that already have the best infrastructure — is the kind of tone-deaf spending that makes people lose faith in reform governments.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Nga Kor Ming is the same minister who threatened a journalist for asking about event naming, and whose MyKiosk programme failed MACC review for lacking a needs assessment. “BMW-class” toilets — the branding alone tells you this isn’t about serving the public, it’s about making the minister look good. And when asked for an update months later? Silence. No progress report. No accountability. Just a vanity announcement followed by a black hole of implementation. Meanwhile, regular public toilets across Malaysia remain filthy and broken. But sure, build luxury toilets in areas that already have the best infrastructure.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia has a long history of inflated government procurement. The RM600 toilet seats and RM1,200 screwdrivers of the BN era were bread-and-butter corruption stories. PH screamed about wasteful spending for decades. Now they’re spending RM1 million per toilet and calling it innovation. Nga himself has a pattern of grand announcements with poor follow-through — the MyKiosk programme being the most recent example of MACC flagging his ministry’s projects for governance failures.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You promised fiscal discipline and responsible governance. You told Malaysians every ringgit would be spent wisely under Kerajaan Madani. Then you spend RM1 million per toilet and can’t provide a progress report when asked. The rakyat can’t afford eggs, but your minister is building BMW toilets. If this is reform, the rakyat would like a refund.

Rice Shortages Deepen Food Security Fears — Anwar Wants 'More Monitoring'

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister

Category: Economy

Date: 2024-11-01

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: In late 2024, sporadic rice shortages hit Malaysia alongside devastating floods. Supermarket shelves emptied of local rice varieties in multiple states. Anwar’s response was to call for “frequent monitoring and enforcement” — the government’s default when it has no plan. Malaysia imports over 30% of its rice supply, and the domestic padi industry has been neglected for decades. Global rice prices hit multi-year highs due to India’s export restrictions, El Niño impacts on Southeast Asian harvests, and supply chain disruptions.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Rice is the staple food for 33 million Malaysians. When rice shelves are empty, food security is not theoretical — it is immediate. Malaysia’s 30%+ import dependency means any global supply disruption directly threatens domestic food security. The padi industry has been underfunded, fragmented, and politically managed for decades. Smallholder farmers receive insufficient support. Irrigation infrastructure is crumbling. Yields per hectare lag behind Thailand, Vietnam, and Indonesia. The rice shortage was predictable — and the government was not prepared.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: “Monitoring and enforcement” is what you do when you have no plan. It is the bureaucratic equivalent of watching the problem get worse while looking busy. PH promised food security reform. Three years later, Malaysia’s rice import dependency hasn’t decreased, domestic production hasn’t meaningfully increased, and the government’s response to shortages is enforcement — punishing traders for hoarding rather than addressing the structural supply deficit. You can’t enforce rice into existence. You need to grow it, and that requires investment the government hasn’t made.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Malaysia has faced periodic rice shortages under every government. Under BN, the response was always the same: release strategic reserves, blame hoarders, promise investment in padi. Under PH 1.0, food security was briefly elevated as a policy priority before the Sheraton Move. Under PH 2.0, the government launched a “food security” initiative that generated press conferences but no measurable improvement in self-sufficiency ratios. The structural problem — declining padi acreage, aging farmers, insufficient irrigation, and reliance on imports — remains identical to what it was 20 years ago.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: Rice shelves are empty. Your response is “monitoring.” Malaysia imports 30%+ of its rice and you’ve done nothing to reduce that dependency in three years. Invest in padi infrastructure — irrigation, mechanisation, yield improvement. Support smallholder farmers with guaranteed minimum prices. Build strategic reserves that can absorb global supply shocks. Or keep “monitoring” while Malaysians can’t find rice at the supermarket — and hope they don’t notice that “monitoring” is not food security.

Foreign Worker Levy Multi-Tier System — Announced 2023, Still Not Fully Implemented

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister

Category: Economy

Date: 2024-10-18

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: In September 2023, Anwar announced a multi-tier levy system for foreign workers designed to reduce Malaysia’s dependency on imported labour. The system was repeated in Budget 2025 with further details. The Malaysian Employers Federation (MEF) complained the system was still unfair and insufficiently implemented. Anwar’s promise to cut foreign worker dependency to 15% of the workforce by 2025 was not met. As of late 2025, Malaysia still employed approximately 2.2 million documented foreign workers, with an estimated 1-2 million undocumented — a dependency that has not meaningfully changed under PH.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: Foreign worker dependency suppresses local wages, particularly for B40 Malaysians who compete directly with imported labour in construction, agriculture, manufacturing, and services. The multi-tier levy was supposed to make it progressively more expensive for employers to hire foreign workers, incentivising automation, productivity improvements, and local hiring. Two years after announcement, the system is still not fully implemented. Meanwhile, industries continue importing workers at levels that undercut the wage floor for Malaysian citizens.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Announcing a policy and implementing it are two different things — and PH excels at the former while failing at the latter. The multi-tier levy was announced in 2023, repeated in 2024, and included in Budget 2025. At each stage, it was presented as a new initiative. The 15% target was quietly dropped when it became clear it wouldn’t be met. MEF’s complaints suggest that even the implemented portions are poorly designed. The foreign worker lobby — representing plantation companies, construction firms, and manufacturers with political connections — has successfully delayed meaningful reform while the announcements continue.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Every Malaysian government has promised to reduce foreign worker dependency. Under Mahathir 1.0, the promise was made in the 1990s. Under Najib, various levy increases were announced. Under PH 1.0, a freeze on new foreign worker permits was briefly implemented before being reversed. Under PH 2.0, the multi-tier levy was announced with fanfare but implementation has been glacial. The pattern is consistent: announce reform, face industry pushback, delay implementation, announce it again in the next budget. Meanwhile, 3-4 million foreign workers remain in the country.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You announced the multi-tier levy in 2023. It’s 2026 and it’s still not fully implemented. Your 15% target was missed. MEF says the system is unfair. Industries keep importing workers at the same rate. Either implement the levy with teeth — real enforcement, meaningful cost increases for employers who over-rely on foreign labour — or admit that the announcement was for headlines, not for policy. Malaysian workers deserve local wage protection. They’re not getting it because your government is more afraid of upsetting the plantation lobby than protecting the rakyat.

Sources: Bernama | FMT | MEF
