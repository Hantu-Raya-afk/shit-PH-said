Anwar Signals Openness to PAS Engagement — The Man Who Ran Against Them Now Courts Them

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-08

📌 WHAT HAPPENED: On 8 March 2026, while defending his government’s RM15.5 billion anti-corruption recovery figure, Anwar Ibrahim simultaneously signalled openness to closer engagement with PAS — the Islamist party his coalition was built to oppose. This came as DAP was publicly reviewing its coalition ties and PH had just been wiped out in the Sabah state election, winning only 1 of 20 seats.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: The entire premise of Pakatan Harapan was that it represented the alternative to PAS’s religious extremism and UMNO’s ethno-nationalist patronage. Anwar’s willingness to court PAS — even as DAP, his most loyal coalition partner, is hemorrhaging support — signals that electoral survival has overtaken ideology entirely. If PH was built to oppose PAS, and PH’s leader is now courting PAS, what exactly does PH stand for anymore?

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT: Rather than fixing the internal fractures that caused the Sabah wipeout — DAP’s marginalisation, PKR’s Rafizi split, manifesto promises left unmet — Anwar is hedging toward the same political accommodation he once condemned. This is not pragmatism. It’s desperation dressed as statesmanship. You don’t fix a coalition built on reform by inviting the anti-reform party to the table. You fix it by actually reforming.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES: Mahathir courted PAS during PH 1.0 behind the scenes, contributing to the coalition’s collapse in the 2020 Sheraton Move. UMNO has flirted with PAS repeatedly when politically convenient, forming Muafakat Nasional in 2019. Every Malaysian coalition that courts PAS ends up making concessions on secular governance. Anwar watched this playbook destroy PH 1.0 — and is now running it himself.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You spent GE15 telling Malaysians that PAS was a threat to multicultural Malaysia. Your own coalition partners marched against PAS’s brand of politics. Now that you’re losing elections, PAS is suddenly worth engaging? Either PAS is a threat to Malaysian democracy or it isn’t. Pick one and stick with it. Your voters deserve consistency, not electoral desperation.

PKR Issues Show-Cause Letter to Rafizi, Then Backs Down — Party Can't Even Discipline Its Most Prominent Critic

Said by: Rafizi Ramli, Pandan MP / Fuziah Salleh, PKR Secretary-General

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-08

📌 What Happened

On February 19, Pandan MP Rafizi Ramli revealed he had received a show-cause letter from PKR secretary-general Fuziah Salleh after telling Sin Chew Daily he would contest in Pandan at GE16 but likely not as a PKR candidate. Rafizi responded saying the meaning of his comments was “understandable to anyone with a good command of Malay” and that he was free to make his own plans after Parliament was dissolved. On March 6, PKR conveyed its decision by email — no action taken, just a reminder to adhere to the party constitution and code of ethics. On March 8, Rafizi publicly described this as a “U-turn,” saying the show-cause letter was issued “with the intention to give him the boot” but the party ultimately blinked.

🔥 Why It Matters

This episode exposes the institutional rot inside PKR — the party that once styled itself as the vanguard of reformasi. A show-cause letter is supposed to be the start of a disciplinary process, not a performative threat followed by a quiet retreat. Rafizi has been openly critical of Anwar and the PKR leadership on podcasts, in media interviews, and on social media for weeks. PKR’s inability to either meaningfully engage with his criticisms or enforce its own disciplinary mechanisms shows a party that is paralysed — too afraid to sack its most prominent critic, but too proud to admit he has a point.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

PKR wanted Rafizi gone but didn’t have the spine to do it. As Rafizi himself pointed out, expelling him wouldn’t cost him his parliamentary seat — only a voluntary resignation would trigger a vacancy. So PKR was trapped: sack him and he stays in Parliament as a free agent embarrassing them daily, or back down and look weak. They chose weak. The “reminder to follow party rules” is the political equivalent of a passive-aggressive Post-it note. This is the same party that lectures the nation about institutional reform and good governance while it cannot even manage a basic internal disciplinary process without folding under pressure.

📜 Previous Cases

PKR has a long and inglorious history of factional purges disguised as discipline — and discipline that collapses into farce. The party expelled its own deputy president Azmin Ali in 2020 after the Sheraton Move, but only after the damage was done. Before that, leaders like Zuraida Kamaruddin and Baru Bian walked out with minimal consequences. Rafizi himself was once PKR’s golden boy — the whistleblower who exposed the National Feedlot Corporation scandal. Now he is the party’s most inconvenient voice, and PKR’s playbook remains the same: threaten, posture, and then quietly pretend nothing happened when the political cost of following through becomes clear.

🎯 PH Callout

Anwar and PKR promised Malaysians a new politics — transparent, principled, and accountable. But when your own former deputy president publicly says he won’t contest under your banner, and the best your party can muster is a show-cause letter followed by a meek email reminder, you are not running a political party — you are managing a hostage situation. If PKR cannot enforce discipline on a single MP who has openly challenged the president, how does it expect the public to believe it can enforce standards on an entire government? “Reformasi” used to mean something. Now it means sending sternly worded emails and hoping the problem goes away.

Teresa Kok Says Coalition Unity More Important Than RCI Into MACC — DAP's Own MP Undermines the Accountability Push

Said by: Teresa Kok, MP for Seputeh (DAP)

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-08

📌 What Happened

On March 8, DAP’s Seputeh MP Teresa Kok publicly stated that maintaining unity within the Madani coalition government is more important than her own party’s push for a Royal Commission of Inquiry (RCI) into the Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission (MACC). This came just days after DAP secretary-general Anthony Loke told Malaysiakini the government’s response to MACC misconduct allegations had been “inadequate” and called for an independent inquiry — a position endorsed by DAP’s national leadership and reinforced by the party’s decision to bring forward its special congress to July 2026.

🔥 Why It Matters

DAP is the single largest party in Anwar’s ruling coalition with 40 MPs, and its entire political identity rests on reform, accountability, and anti-corruption. When the party’s own backbenchers publicly break with the leadership to prioritize coalition survival over institutional accountability, it signals that the internal pressure campaign is already fracturing. Teresa Kok’s statement gives Anwar political cover to continue ignoring calls for an RCI — he can point to voices within DAP itself saying unity matters more. It weakens DAP’s negotiating position at the exact moment the party is supposed to be leveraging the July congress as a credible threat.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

This is the oldest trick in the coalition playbook: reframe the demand for accountability as a threat to stability. Teresa Kok is effectively saying the government should not be held to its own standards because doing so might rock the boat. But DAP didn’t enter government to keep the boat steady — it entered to fix the engine. The MACC under Azam Baki has been credibly accused by Bloomberg of running a “corporate mafia,” breaching civil service shareholding rules multiple times, and weaponizing investigations against political targets. If an RCI into that is too destabilizing for the coalition, then the coalition has already failed its stated purpose. Unity without accountability is just complicity with better PR.

📜 Previous Cases

This is not the first time a DAP MP has publicly undercut the party’s reform stance to protect coalition harmony. The pattern runs deep: DAP stayed silent for months on Azam Baki’s first shareholding scandal in 2021-2022 when it was in opposition, then renewed his contract three times after entering government. The party’s leadership accepted the government’s internal task force instead of pushing harder for an RCI when Bloomberg’s “corporate mafia” exposé dropped in February. And in the Sabah state election last November, DAP was wiped out — winning zero seats — partly because voters saw a party that had traded its principles for Cabinet positions.

🎯 PH Callout

You told Malaysian voters you were the reform coalition. You told the Chinese community you were the only credible check on corruption and cronyism. You told civil society that institutional accountability was non-negotiable. And now, when your own party demands a basic accountability mechanism — an RCI into an anti-corruption agency accused of being a corporate mafia — your own MPs say unity is more important. If DAP cannot hold its own line on the MACC, what exactly is the party for? The July congress is supposed to be a reckoning. If this is the signal DAP’s backbench is sending — that keeping ministerial seats matters more than keeping promises — then voters already have their answer.

Anwar Warns of 'Wealthy Elites' Manipulating Media — While His Own Government Shields the MACC Chief From Accountability

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-07

📌 What Happened

On March 7, 2026, Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim used an iftar event in Kulim to accuse unnamed Malaysian elites of “corruptly using their wealth to exert influence on international media, online narratives and local politics.” He said he was not referring only to the opposition but to “everyone,” and urged Malaysians to “remain vigilant” against “foreign schemes and conspiracies.” The statement came just four days after he told Parliament about an alleged plot — involving a former senior minister’s wife, foreign PR firms, and Bloomberg — to destabilise his government.

🔥 Why It Matters

This is not a one-off remark. It is part of a deliberate, escalating narrative by Anwar to reframe legitimate scrutiny of his government — particularly over the Azam Baki shareholding scandal — as a foreign-funded conspiracy by shadowy elites. By keeping the accusation vague (“not just the opposition — everyone”), Anwar gives himself maximum rhetorical cover to dismiss any critic, journalist, or institution that holds his government accountable. This is the same playbook used by Najib Razak during the 1MDB scandal: when the evidence gets uncomfortable, blame foreign interference.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

Anwar spent decades in opposition accusing previous PMs of exactly this — using vague “national security” rhetoric to silence critics and avoid accountability. Now he’s doing it himself, from an iftar stage no less, while his government refuses to suspend an MACC chief credibly accused of breaching shareholding laws. The man who once led reformasi rallies is now warning Malaysians not to be “trapped and used as tools” — by which he means: stop asking hard questions about why my anti-corruption agency looks compromised. The hypocrisy is not subtle. It is structural.

📜 Previous Cases

This fits a pattern as old as Malaysian politics itself. Mahathir blamed “Jewish speculators” during the 1997 Asian financial crisis to deflect from governance failures. Najib dismissed the Wall Street Journal’s 1MDB reporting as a conspiracy by “certain people” who wanted to “topple a democratically elected government.” Anwar himself, when he was in opposition, called these deflections authoritarian. Now in power, he has adopted the identical rhetorical framework — foreign agents, shadowy elites, media manipulation — with only the names changed.

🎯 PH Callout

You ran on transparency, institutional reform, and media freedom. You told Malaysians that under your government, the press would not be intimidated and corruption would be fought without fear or favour. Now your Prime Minister is standing at a podium accusing unnamed elites of weaponising the media — while Bloomberg’s reporting on Azam Baki’s shareholdings remains unanswered, while Bersih gets publicly scolded for calling the MACC a political tool, and while Rafizi — your own former economy minister — has to take to the streets to demand accountability. If “wealthy elites” are the problem, start with the ones in your own Cabinet who benefit from the system you promised to dismantle.

Said by: CNA Analysis

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-07

An anti-graft crisis at MACC (Bloomberg exposés on Azam Baki), PKR internal fractures following Rafizi Ramli’s departure from cabinet, and coalition partners openly reconsidering their ties. CNA described it as a “perfect storm” of political instability. UMNO’s secret Bangkok meeting with opposition parties, DAP reviewing coalition ties, and the PKR factional war all threaten to bring down the Unity Government before its term ends.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS: When CNA uses the phrase “perfect storm” to describe your political situation, you are in serious trouble. The convergence of MACC credibility collapse, PKR internal warfare, and coalition instability is unprecedented in Anwar’s tenure. Each crisis alone would be manageable; together, they threaten the government’s survival. If early elections are called, PH would face voters with most manifesto promises unmet, corruption perceptions unchanged, and the cost of living still rising. The political capital Anwar accumulated through years of struggle has been spent on coalition survival rather than reform delivery.

Sideline him. DAP unhappy? Reshuffle them into weaker portfolios. Bersih criticising? Call them unfair. Consolidation only works when you have political capital to spend — and that capital is depleting fast. A PM who spends all his energy managing crises he created through inaction has no energy left for governance.

Ismail Sabri lasted barely a year. Malaysia has had five PMs in eight years. The pattern of coalition governments imploding under internal pressure is not new — what’s new is that PH was supposed to break this cycle. Instead, it’s repeating it.

🎯 PH CALLOUT: You came to power promising stability and reform. Three years later, your coalition is fracturing, your anti-corruption agency is under fire, your own party is split, and analysts are talking about snap elections. This is not the “stability” you promised. Fix the MACC. Reconcile with Rafizi. Deliver on your manifesto. Or call elections and let the rakyat decide if three years of broken promises deserve a second term.

Rafizi Tells PH Voters to 'Wait Till June' — Hints at Breaking Away from PKR and 'Sailing His Own Ship'

Said by: Rafizi Ramli, Pandan MP / Former Economy Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-07

📌 What Happened

Former Economy Minister Rafizi Ramli, who quit the cabinet in May 2025 after losing the PKR deputy presidency contest, told Pakatan Harapan supporters in a Sin Chew Daily interview to “wait till June,” hinting that a new political option would be unveiled. Rafizi said he and his allies are open to “sailing their own ship” and are exploring “different political configurations” — which could involve a new party, coalition, or political platform. He confirmed he will defend his Pandan parliamentary seat in the next general election but definitively not under the PKR banner. Separately, Reuters reported that several PKR lawmakers are considering contesting as independents, having lost confidence in Anwar’s leadership.

🔥 Why It Matters

This is the most serious internal threat to Pakatan Harapan since the Sheraton Move. Rafizi isn’t a backbencher — he’s the most popular reformist figure in Malaysian politics among younger voters, the man who exposed the NFC and 1MDB scandals, and the architect of the “Ayuh Malaysia” campaign that helped deliver GE15 for Anwar. If Rafizi launches a third-force vehicle in Peninsular Malaysia — potentially aligned with Shafie Apdal’s Parti Warisan, which already swept Chinese votes in Sabah — it could split PH’s urban and reform-minded voter base right before a general election that sources say could come as early as late 2026.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

Not Rafizi’s move — Anwar’s response to it. Instead of addressing the legitimate grievances that pushed Rafizi out (the rigged party election, the refusal to act on Azam Baki, the abandonment of reform), Anwar’s camp has reportedly weaponized MACC to harass Rafizi’s former aide James Chai and used government machinery to undermine the Pandan MP. The Prime Minister who built his career on reformasi is now using the exact same playbook Mahathir and Najib used against him. The man who once stood trial for challenging his boss is now the boss who punishes anyone who challenges him.

📜 Previous Cases

Malaysian politics has seen this movie before. In 1998, Anwar himself was the reformist insider who broke away and formed a movement that eventually became PKR. In 2015, Muhyiddin Yassin was sacked for questioning Najib over 1MDB and later formed Bersatu. The pattern is consistent: Malaysian prime ministers treat internal dissent as treason rather than accountability. What makes this case uniquely damaging for PH is that Rafizi’s departure was entirely self-inflicted by Anwar — he reverted PKR’s party elections from a direct member vote to a delegate system specifically to remove Rafizi from the deputy president position, prioritizing dynastic loyalty over competence.

🎯 PH Callout

Anwar, you made Rafizi. He exposed the scandals you campaigned on. He built the ground game that got you to Putrajaya. And you repaid him by rigging your party’s internal elections, sidelining him from cabinet, and siccing the anti-corruption agency on his people. Now he’s building an alternative — and you’re terrified, because you know better than anyone what happens when a popular reformist breaks away from a corrupt establishment. You wrote that playbook in 1998. The difference is: Rafizi has data, youth support, and the receipts. You just have the machinery. And machinery didn’t save Najib either.

238 Political Appointments to FSBs and GLCs Under Anwar — IDEAS Report Exposes 'Connections Over Competence'

Said by: IDEAS Report / Anwar Ibrahim Administration

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-06

📌 What Happened

The Institute for Democracy and Economic Affairs (IDEAS) released a report titled Who Sits at the Table: From Political to Public Appointees in Malaysia’s Federal Statutory Bodies on March 6, 2026, revealing that 238 political appointments were made to federal statutory bodies (FSBs) and government-linked companies (GLCs) under Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim’s administration between November 2022 and October 2025. Of these, 222 were to FSBs and 16 to GLCs. The report reviewed 135 agencies and found that 78% of chairperson positions were filled based on political connections rather than competence. Most lacked clearly defined qualification criteria. The appointments were concentrated in key ministries, and the governance provisions around them were described as inconsistent and fragmented.

🔥 Why It Matters

This report is a direct, data-backed indictment of PH’s reform credentials. Anwar came to power promising meritocracy, transparency, and an end to the patronage politics that defined BN-era governance. Three years in, an independent, respected think tank has documented that political appointments haven’t just continued — they dominate. When 78% of the people running your statutory bodies got the job through connections rather than qualifications, you are not reforming governance. You are changing the names on the patronage list. This is not a fringe accusation — IDEAS is one of Malaysia’s most credible policy institutes, and its Pantau Kuasa initiative has tracked appointments across multiple administrations.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

PH sold voters the idea that they were fundamentally different from UMNO and BN. That the era of politically connected chairs presiding over government agencies was over. IDEAS’ numbers say otherwise: 238 appointments in under three years, with the overwhelming majority driven by political affiliation rather than professional merit. This is not reform — it is rotation. Different coalition, same playbook. The only thing that changed is the party logo on the appointment letters. Anwar has gone from demanding accountability for political appointments in opposition to treating them as a natural entitlement of power in government. If competence mattered, these numbers would look different. They do not.

📜 Previous Cases

Political appointments to statutory bodies and GLCs have been a recurring feature of Malaysian governance for decades. Under Najib Razak, the practice was widely criticised — particularly at 1MDB, Tabung Haji, and FELDA, where politically connected chairs presided over mismanagement and outright corruption. PH’s 2018 manifesto explicitly promised to end this, and the short-lived PH 1.0 government made some initial moves toward meritocratic appointments before collapsing in the Sheraton Move. Under PH 2.0, the pattern has returned with a vengeance. IDEAS’ Pantau Kuasa data shows that Anwar’s government is not the outlier it promised to be — it is the continuation of a structural problem that PH once made its name fighting.

🎯 PH Callout

You promised Malaysians a new Malaysia. You said the era of political cronies was over. You told voters that under Madani, competence would matter more than connections. IDEAS just put a number on that promise: 238 political appointments, 78% driven by politics, not merit. That is not reform — that is business as usual with better branding. If PH cannot even clean up the appointment process for statutory bodies — a basic, achievable governance reform with no legal barriers — why should anyone believe the bigger promises about institutional independence, anti-corruption, or meritocracy? The data speaks louder than the slogans.

Anwar Denied Snap Polls in December — Now His Government Is Quietly Preparing for Elections in 2026

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister / Unity Government sources

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-06

📌 What Happened

In December 2025, Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim told journalists that snap elections were not on the cards — his priority, he said, was implementing reforms and addressing cost of living. Fast forward to March 2026: multiple government insiders, party grassroots leaders, and civil servants have told CNA that preparations are quietly underway for a possible general election in the second half of 2026. A Johor politician from the unity government said grassroots have been told to ramp up preparations after Hari Raya Aidilfitri. A senior civil servant confirmed government administrative officers have been placed on alert and resources put “on standby” from June. EC deputy chairman Azmi Sharom said the commission is ready, including for concurrent state and parliamentary polls.

🔥 Why It Matters

This is the clearest signal yet that Anwar’s government is moving toward a snap election despite publicly denying it. The gap between what PH says and what PH does continues to widen. If elections are held in late 2026, it means the Madani government will have governed for roughly four years without delivering on major reform promises — the PM tenure bill just failed, the MACC chief is still in office, RON95 subsidy rationalisation is stalled, and the AG separation bill is under threat from within PH’s own ranks. Calling elections now is about timing, not achievement.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

Anwar said reforms first, elections later. Now it’s elections first, reforms never. The reason is transparent: the opposition is in disarray. Bersatu has split between Muhyiddin and Hamzah factions, Rafizi is floating a separate course, and PAS has not consolidated its post-Sabah strategy. This isn’t a government going to the people with a record of delivery — it’s a government rushing to the polls before the window of opposition weakness closes. That’s not reform politics. That’s the same opportunistic calculation every Malaysian PM before him has made.

📜 Previous Cases

Malaysian PMs have a long tradition of calling elections when the opposition is weak rather than when the government is strong. Mahathir did it. Najib tried it in 2018 and failed. Muhyiddin dissolved parliament in 2022 when he lost his majority, leading to the hung parliament that brought Anwar to power. Anwar himself campaigned against political opportunism and promised fixed parliamentary terms. The PM tenure bill — which just failed — was supposed to be the structural antidote. Instead, the government is reportedly considering the exact kind of snap-election manoeuvre the bill was designed to prevent.

🎯 PH Callout

You promised Malaysia institutional reform. You promised that power would not be exercised at the whim of one man’s political calculus. You said elections should be about the people’s readiness, not the PM’s convenience. Now your machinery is on standby, your grassroots are mobilising, and your EC is publicly confirming readiness — all while you tell the cameras snap polls aren’t happening. Either tell the truth and call the election, or stop preparing for one. The rakyat are not props in your re-election timeline.

Anwar Tells Bersih to Stop Criticising MACC 'During Ramadan' — Uses the Holy Month as a Shield Against Accountability

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-06

📌 What Happened

On March 6, 2026, Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim publicly dismissed the Coalition for Clean and Fair Elections (Bersih)’s accusation that the Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission (MACC) has become a political tool of the government. Speaking after the Public Service Department’s Ihya’ Ramadan programme in Putrajaya, Anwar called Bersih’s statement “unfair” and said the group was “not making clean statements.” He then added: “Do not attack people without first obtaining information, especially during Ramadan.” He pointed to the government’s investigative committee and the Attorney General as reasons to defer criticism, saying no reports had been received yet.

🔥 Why It Matters

Bersih’s criticism was not random — it was a direct response to mounting evidence that MACC under Azam Baki has been used to intimidate political opponents and journalists, including the case of James Chai, a former aide to ex-Economy Minister Rafizi Ramli. When Malaysia’s most prominent electoral reform NGO says your anti-corruption agency has gone rogue, the correct response is to take it seriously, not to invoke Ramadan as a reason to stop asking questions. Anwar’s framing — “wait for the reports” — is a bureaucratic stalling tactic that delays accountability indefinitely while the institution continues to operate without oversight.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

Using Ramadan to deflect accountability criticism is a cynical rhetorical move. The holy month is about reflection, justice, and moral discipline — not about shielding government agencies from scrutiny. Anwar’s line — “Do not attack people without first obtaining information, especially during Ramadan” — conflates legitimate institutional criticism with personal attacks, and then wraps the deflection in religious sentiment. Bersih isn’t attacking a person; it’s questioning whether the country’s anti-corruption agency is fit for purpose. And Anwar’s “I am not defending anyone” is the classic prelude to defending someone — which is exactly what he did by telling everyone to wait, trust the AG, and stop talking.

📜 Previous Cases

Anwar has a well-documented pattern of using moral framing to shut down criticism. When Bloomberg reported on Azam Baki’s shareholdings, the government’s first instinct was to frame it as a foreign plot rather than investigate the substance. When DAP and Amanah raised concerns, they were told to be patient. When Bersih pushed harder, they were told they were being unfair. This is the same playbook used by every Malaysian PM before Anwar — deflect, delay, and accuse the critics of bad faith. Mahathir did it. Najib did it. Now Anwar does it, just with better vocabulary.

🎯 PH Callout

You promised to be the government that listens to civil society. Bersih marched for you. Bersih fought for clean elections that put you in power. And now, when they raise legitimate concerns about the weaponisation of MACC, your response is to tell them to be quiet during Ramadan? Ramadan calls for justice, not silence. If the reports haven’t come in yet, that’s your government’s failure to produce them — not Bersih’s failure to wait politely. Stop hiding behind committees and holy months. Answer the question: Is MACC independent, or isn’t it?
