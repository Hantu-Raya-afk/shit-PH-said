Anwar's Coalition Can't Pass Its Own Reform Bill — Loses PM Term Limit Vote by Two, Promises to 'Try Again in June'

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister / Pakatan Harapan Coalition

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-02

📌 What Happened

On March 2, Anwar Ibrahim’s unity government failed to pass a constitutional amendment that would have limited the prime minister’s tenure to 10 years — a flagship reform promise from PH’s 2022 election manifesto. The bill received 146 votes, just two short of the 148 needed for a two-thirds majority. Forty-four MPs abstained and 32 were absent. Deputy Speaker Alice Lau confirmed eight government and independent MPs did not participate. The government has since pledged to reintroduce the bill in June, with coalition leaders promising their MPs will “actually attend this time.”

🔥 Why It Matters

This was supposed to be the easy win. The PM term limit was popular, symbolically loaded, and politically safe — designed to show that PH can still deliver on reform. Instead, Anwar’s coalition, which holds a supermajority in the 222-seat Dewan Rakyat, couldn’t even whip its own members into the chamber. The failure exposes a deeper rot: three years into government, PH cannot translate its parliamentary numbers into legislative outcomes. If the coalition can’t pass a bill it spent weeks championing, what hope is there for harder reforms — anti-hopping enforcement, IPCMC, procurement transparency, or judicial independence?

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

A government with a supermajority losing its own reform bill by two votes is not bad luck — it’s bad management. Either the whips failed to do their jobs, or parts of the coalition quietly sabotaged the vote. An unnamed PH lawmaker told the SCMP: “That bill should have easily passed. It feels like there was not enough effort to make sure everyone turned up.” That’s not an explanation — it’s a confession. And the government’s response? “We’ll try again in June.” That’s the reform equivalent of hitting snooze. Meanwhile, cameras caught Anwar mid-laugh after the vote was read out, chatting with his deputy as if his flagship bill hadn’t just died on the floor.

📜 Previous Cases

This is a pattern. PH promised IPCMC — shelved. Promised freedom of information — nothing. Promised anti-hopping law enforcement — selective at best. Promised political funding reform — in committee limbo. The 2022 manifesto was a 60-page wishlist; three years on, the hardest items remain untouched and even the easy ones are fumbled. The PM term limit was literally the lowest-hanging fruit — popular across party lines, personally backed by Anwar, and requiring only a supermajority PH already had on paper.

🎯 PH Callout

You told Malaysia you were different. You ran on “Reformasi.” You had the numbers, the mandate, and the public goodwill. And you couldn’t get your own MPs into the room to vote for a bill you spent weeks selling to the country. If you can’t pass popular, symbolic reforms with a supermajority, what exactly are you using that majority for? “We’ll try again in June” is not a reform agenda — it’s an admission that you don’t have control of your own coalition.

Anwar's Coalition Fails to Pass PM Term-Limit Bill — Two Votes Short, 32 MPs Missing, Zero Accountability

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister / Unity Government Leadership

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-02

📌 What Happened

On March 2, Anwar Ibrahim’s unity government failed to pass the Constitution (Amendment) Bill 2026, which would have capped the prime minister’s tenure at 10 years or two terms. The bill needed a two-thirds supermajority — 148 votes in the 222-seat Dewan Rakyat — and got 146. Thirty-two MPs were absent, 44 abstained, and none voted against. Among the absentees were several from coalition partner Barisan Nasional, including Sembrong MP Hishammuddin Hussein and Tapah MP Saravanan Murugan. The government has pledged to reintroduce the bill in June.

🔥 Why It Matters

This was supposed to be the easiest reform win in Anwar’s playbook — a popular, symbolically loaded proposal that PH had promised since 2018. Anwar commands a supermajority in Parliament. He failed to pass a bill that no one even voted against. The failure exposes a coalition that cannot enforce discipline on its own members, let alone deliver the institutional reforms it promised Malaysian voters. With a general election due by early 2028, the clock is ticking and the reform scorecard is bleeding red.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

A government with a supermajority that cannot whip its own MPs to show up for a vote it spent weeks championing is not a reform government — it is a performance. Thirty-two MPs did not bother to attend. Forty-four abstained. The opposition did not kill this bill; the coalition’s own inertia did. And the response? “We’ll try again in June.” That is not accountability. That is a government admitting it failed at the most basic function of coalition management and asking for a do-over like a student who forgot to study for the exam.

📜 Previous Cases

This is not the first time Anwar’s coalition has stumbled on its own reform promises. The Anti-Party Hopping Law, passed in 2022, was hailed as a landmark — until it was narrowed in scope and never seriously tested. The promised separation of the Attorney General and Public Prosecutor roles has been delayed repeatedly. The IPCMC (Independent Police Complaints Commission) was watered down into the toothless IPCC. Every time PH promises reform, the pattern is the same: grand announcement, quiet retreat, and a promise to revisit later.

🎯 PH Callout

You ran on reform. You said Mahathir’s 22 years “damaged the country.” You promised term limits in 2018. You promised them again in 2022. You had a supermajority. You had a bill. You had weeks to prepare. And you could not get 148 people to show up. If you cannot pass the easiest reform on your own agenda, what exactly is the unity government for? June is not a second chance — it is a test of whether this coalition has any discipline left at all. The rakyat is watching, and the clock does not reset.

PH's Own PM Term Limit Bill Fails by Two Votes — 8 Government MPs Didn't Even Show Up

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister / Fahmi Fadzil, Communications Minister / Anthony Loke, DAP Secretary-General

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-02

📌 What Happened

On March 2, 2026, Malaysia’s Dewan Rakyat voted on the Constitution (Amendment) Bill 2026 — PH’s long-promised reform to limit the prime minister’s tenure to 10 years (two terms). The bill needed 148 votes — a two-thirds supermajority — to pass. It got 146. Two votes short. Speaker Tan Sri Johari Abdul confirmed that 44 MPs abstained (mostly opposition), 32 were absent, and — in a telling detail — not a single MP voted against it. Among the absentees were 8 government-supporting MPs. Communications Minister Fahmi Fadzil later confirmed that only 2 of the 8 had submitted formal explanations; 6 had not even bothered. DAP Secretary-General Anthony Loke issued a strongly worded statement emphasising that all 40 DAP MPs were present, and pointedly called out the empty seats.

🔥 Why It Matters

This was supposed to be PH’s signature institutional reform — the centrepiece of a manifesto promise dating back to the 2022 general election, born directly from the trauma of Mahathir’s 22-year reign. A PM term limit is one of the most consequential democratic safeguards any government can enact. And PH couldn’t even get its own coalition to show up. The bill wasn’t defeated by opposition — it was killed by indifference, incompetence, and absenteeism from within the government’s own ranks. PKR MP Wong Chen later revealed that at least one absent MP said he “was simply not properly informed to turn up” and received no follow-up calls. This is not just a legislative failure — it is a governance failure that exposes the hollowness of PH’s reform brand.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

PH had three years to prepare for this vote. Three years to whip their own MPs, count heads, confirm attendance, and deliver the most basic requirement of parliamentary democracy — showing up. Deputy PM Fadillah Yusof is the government chief whip. His one job in this context was to ensure every government MP was present. He failed. And the excuse from absent MPs — “I wasn’t properly informed” — is breathtaking in its casualness. This was a constitutional amendment, not a backbench briefing on drainage policy. If PH cannot mobilise its own MPs for its own flagship reform bill, then the reform agenda is not a priority — it is a slogan. The opposition abstained, as expected. But the government’s absentees are the ones who killed this bill.

📜 Previous Cases

Malaysia has never had a statutory term limit for the prime minister. Mahathir served 22 years (1981–2003), then returned for another 22 months (2018–2020). The damage of unchecked executive tenure is well-documented in Malaysian governance — from the entrenchment of patronage networks to the erosion of institutional independence. PH’s 2022 manifesto explicitly promised this reform. In the 2024 Budget speech, Anwar reaffirmed the government’s commitment to institutional reform. Yet when it came time to deliver, PH’s coalition discipline — a perennial weakness since the Sheraton Move — once again proved inadequate. This is the same coalition that lost its own government in 2020 because it couldn’t hold its ranks together.

🎯 PH Callout

You made this promise. You put it in your manifesto. You told Malaysian voters that limiting PM tenure was essential to prevent another Mahathir-style power entrenchment. And when the moment came — when 148 votes were all you needed from a 222-seat house where you command a comfortable majority — you couldn’t even get your own people to walk into the chamber. Six of your eight absent MPs haven’t even explained why they weren’t there. Fahmi says “no action has been taken.” Of course it hasn’t. Because in PH, reform promises are for campaigns, not for parliament. You had one job. You failed. And every Malaysian who believed your reformasi rhetoric is now paying the price for your incompetence.

PKR Hired US Consultants to Lobby Washington Against Najib — Now Cries Foul When Others Do the Same

Said by: Sivarasa Rasiah, Former PKR Vice-President (confirmed); PKR leadership

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-02

📌 WHAT HAPPENED

In March 2026, MalaysiaNow published leaked emails revealing that PKR had engaged a Washington-based political consultancy firm in 2016 to lobby the United States to exert pressure on then-Prime Minister Najib Razak. The emails included an 84-page dossier making serious allegations against Najib, with the goal of getting Washington to publicly rebuke the Malaysian government.

Former PKR vice-president Sivarasa Rasiah confirmed the communications, saying party leaders including himself were involved. He framed it as legitimate advocacy to free Anwar Ibrahim, who was then a political prisoner.

The revelation came as Anwar’s government was simultaneously accusing former finance minister Daim Zainuddin’s family of using foreign agents to destabilise the current administration — calling it a national security threat.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS

This exposes a massive double standard at the heart of PH’s current political strategy. Anwar is framing Daim’s family’s alleged foreign lobbying as treasonous — a plot to topple the government using foreign influence. But his own party did the exact same thing when they were in opposition.

Opposition MP Shahidan Kassim pressed Anwar in Parliament to explain the difference. The answer matters because the government is using the Daim case to justify expanded national security rhetoric, deflect from the Azam Baki scandal, and paint critics as foreign agents. If PKR’s own lobbying was legitimate, then the same standard must apply to others.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT

Sivarasa’s defence — “we were trying to free a political prisoner” — is convenient but doesn’t hold up under scrutiny. The 84-page dossier didn’t just ask for Anwar’s release. It made sweeping allegations against Najib and explicitly sought US intervention in Malaysian domestic politics. That’s foreign lobbying. Period.

When PKR does it, it’s “advocacy.” When Daim’s family allegedly does it, it’s a “Zionist conspiracy” and a national security threat. The double standard is so blatant that even a casual observer can see through it.

Anwar’s government is essentially saying: foreign lobbying is patriotic when we do it, and treasonous when our opponents do it. That’s not a principle — it’s a convenience.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES

Malaysian political parties have a long history of international lobbying. UMNO maintained Washington lobbyists for decades. Anwar himself cultivated deep relationships with US policymakers, think tanks, and media during his years in opposition. None of this was considered problematic when PH was the beneficiary.

The selective outrage follows the same pattern as PH’s treatment of press freedom (Bloomberg good when exposing Najib, Bloomberg bad when exposing Azam Baki) and institutional accountability (MACC is weaponised under BN, MACC is independent under PH).

🎯 PH CALLOUT

You hired a US firm to lobby Washington against a sitting Malaysian PM, produced an 84-page dossier of allegations, and sought American intervention in Malaysian politics. Your own former vice-president confirmed it. Now you’re telling Malaysians that when others do the same thing, it’s a Zionist-backed conspiracy to topple the government? Pick a lane. Either foreign lobbying is a legitimate part of political advocacy — in which case stop criminalising your opponents for it — or it’s a national security threat, in which case PKR’s own leaders should be investigated too. You can’t have it both ways.

If Azam’s role is to fight corruption, and a credible international outlet alleges he is compromised, the correct institutional response is a transparent, independent investigation — not a defamation suit designed to chill future reporting. The RM100M figure is not about compensation; it is about deterrence. Bloomberg has stood by its reporting.
