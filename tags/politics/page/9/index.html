238 Political Appointments to FSBs and GLCs Under Anwar — IDEAS Report Exposes 'Connections Over Competence'

Said by: IDEAS Report / Anwar Ibrahim Administration

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-06

📌 What Happened

The Institute for Democracy and Economic Affairs (IDEAS) released a report titled Who Sits at the Table: From Political to Public Appointees in Malaysia’s Federal Statutory Bodies on March 6, 2026, revealing that 238 political appointments were made to federal statutory bodies (FSBs) and government-linked companies (GLCs) under Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim’s administration between November 2022 and October 2025. Of these, 222 were to FSBs and 16 to GLCs. The report reviewed 135 agencies and found that 78% of chairperson positions were filled based on political connections rather than competence. Most lacked clearly defined qualification criteria. The appointments were concentrated in key ministries, and the governance provisions around them were described as inconsistent and fragmented.

🔥 Why It Matters

This report is a direct, data-backed indictment of PH’s reform credentials. Anwar came to power promising meritocracy, transparency, and an end to the patronage politics that defined BN-era governance. Three years in, an independent, respected think tank has documented that political appointments haven’t just continued — they dominate. When 78% of the people running your statutory bodies got the job through connections rather than qualifications, you are not reforming governance. You are changing the names on the patronage list. This is not a fringe accusation — IDEAS is one of Malaysia’s most credible policy institutes, and its Pantau Kuasa initiative has tracked appointments across multiple administrations.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

PH sold voters the idea that they were fundamentally different from UMNO and BN. That the era of politically connected chairs presiding over government agencies was over. IDEAS’ numbers say otherwise: 238 appointments in under three years, with the overwhelming majority driven by political affiliation rather than professional merit. This is not reform — it is rotation. Different coalition, same playbook. The only thing that changed is the party logo on the appointment letters. Anwar has gone from demanding accountability for political appointments in opposition to treating them as a natural entitlement of power in government. If competence mattered, these numbers would look different. They do not.

📜 Previous Cases

Political appointments to statutory bodies and GLCs have been a recurring feature of Malaysian governance for decades. Under Najib Razak, the practice was widely criticised — particularly at 1MDB, Tabung Haji, and FELDA, where politically connected chairs presided over mismanagement and outright corruption. PH’s 2018 manifesto explicitly promised to end this, and the short-lived PH 1.0 government made some initial moves toward meritocratic appointments before collapsing in the Sheraton Move. Under PH 2.0, the pattern has returned with a vengeance. IDEAS’ Pantau Kuasa data shows that Anwar’s government is not the outlier it promised to be — it is the continuation of a structural problem that PH once made its name fighting.

🎯 PH Callout

You promised Malaysians a new Malaysia. You said the era of political cronies was over. You told voters that under Madani, competence would matter more than connections. IDEAS just put a number on that promise: 238 political appointments, 78% driven by politics, not merit. That is not reform — that is business as usual with better branding. If PH cannot even clean up the appointment process for statutory bodies — a basic, achievable governance reform with no legal barriers — why should anyone believe the bigger promises about institutional independence, anti-corruption, or meritocracy? The data speaks louder than the slogans.

Anwar Denied Snap Polls in December — Now His Government Is Quietly Preparing for Elections in 2026

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister / Unity Government sources

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-06

📌 What Happened

In December 2025, Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim told journalists that snap elections were not on the cards — his priority, he said, was implementing reforms and addressing cost of living. Fast forward to March 2026: multiple government insiders, party grassroots leaders, and civil servants have told CNA that preparations are quietly underway for a possible general election in the second half of 2026. A Johor politician from the unity government said grassroots have been told to ramp up preparations after Hari Raya Aidilfitri. A senior civil servant confirmed government administrative officers have been placed on alert and resources put “on standby” from June. EC deputy chairman Azmi Sharom said the commission is ready, including for concurrent state and parliamentary polls.

🔥 Why It Matters

This is the clearest signal yet that Anwar’s government is moving toward a snap election despite publicly denying it. The gap between what PH says and what PH does continues to widen. If elections are held in late 2026, it means the Madani government will have governed for roughly four years without delivering on major reform promises — the PM tenure bill just failed, the MACC chief is still in office, RON95 subsidy rationalisation is stalled, and the AG separation bill is under threat from within PH’s own ranks. Calling elections now is about timing, not achievement.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

Anwar said reforms first, elections later. Now it’s elections first, reforms never. The reason is transparent: the opposition is in disarray. Bersatu has split between Muhyiddin and Hamzah factions, Rafizi is floating a separate course, and PAS has not consolidated its post-Sabah strategy. This isn’t a government going to the people with a record of delivery — it’s a government rushing to the polls before the window of opposition weakness closes. That’s not reform politics. That’s the same opportunistic calculation every Malaysian PM before him has made.

📜 Previous Cases

Malaysian PMs have a long tradition of calling elections when the opposition is weak rather than when the government is strong. Mahathir did it. Najib tried it in 2018 and failed. Muhyiddin dissolved parliament in 2022 when he lost his majority, leading to the hung parliament that brought Anwar to power. Anwar himself campaigned against political opportunism and promised fixed parliamentary terms. The PM tenure bill — which just failed — was supposed to be the structural antidote. Instead, the government is reportedly considering the exact kind of snap-election manoeuvre the bill was designed to prevent.

🎯 PH Callout

You promised Malaysia institutional reform. You promised that power would not be exercised at the whim of one man’s political calculus. You said elections should be about the people’s readiness, not the PM’s convenience. Now your machinery is on standby, your grassroots are mobilising, and your EC is publicly confirming readiness — all while you tell the cameras snap polls aren’t happening. Either tell the truth and call the election, or stop preparing for one. The rakyat are not props in your re-election timeline.

Anwar Tells Bersih to Stop Criticising MACC 'During Ramadan' — Uses the Holy Month as a Shield Against Accountability

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-06

📌 What Happened

On March 6, 2026, Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim publicly dismissed the Coalition for Clean and Fair Elections (Bersih)’s accusation that the Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission (MACC) has become a political tool of the government. Speaking after the Public Service Department’s Ihya’ Ramadan programme in Putrajaya, Anwar called Bersih’s statement “unfair” and said the group was “not making clean statements.” He then added: “Do not attack people without first obtaining information, especially during Ramadan.” He pointed to the government’s investigative committee and the Attorney General as reasons to defer criticism, saying no reports had been received yet.

🔥 Why It Matters

Bersih’s criticism was not random — it was a direct response to mounting evidence that MACC under Azam Baki has been used to intimidate political opponents and journalists, including the case of James Chai, a former aide to ex-Economy Minister Rafizi Ramli. When Malaysia’s most prominent electoral reform NGO says your anti-corruption agency has gone rogue, the correct response is to take it seriously, not to invoke Ramadan as a reason to stop asking questions. Anwar’s framing — “wait for the reports” — is a bureaucratic stalling tactic that delays accountability indefinitely while the institution continues to operate without oversight.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

Using Ramadan to deflect accountability criticism is a cynical rhetorical move. The holy month is about reflection, justice, and moral discipline — not about shielding government agencies from scrutiny. Anwar’s line — “Do not attack people without first obtaining information, especially during Ramadan” — conflates legitimate institutional criticism with personal attacks, and then wraps the deflection in religious sentiment. Bersih isn’t attacking a person; it’s questioning whether the country’s anti-corruption agency is fit for purpose. And Anwar’s “I am not defending anyone” is the classic prelude to defending someone — which is exactly what he did by telling everyone to wait, trust the AG, and stop talking.

📜 Previous Cases

Anwar has a well-documented pattern of using moral framing to shut down criticism. When Bloomberg reported on Azam Baki’s shareholdings, the government’s first instinct was to frame it as a foreign plot rather than investigate the substance. When DAP and Amanah raised concerns, they were told to be patient. When Bersih pushed harder, they were told they were being unfair. This is the same playbook used by every Malaysian PM before Anwar — deflect, delay, and accuse the critics of bad faith. Mahathir did it. Najib did it. Now Anwar does it, just with better vocabulary.

🎯 PH Callout

You promised to be the government that listens to civil society. Bersih marched for you. Bersih fought for clean elections that put you in power. And now, when they raise legitimate concerns about the weaponisation of MACC, your response is to tell them to be quiet during Ramadan? Ramadan calls for justice, not silence. If the reports haven’t come in yet, that’s your government’s failure to produce them — not Bersih’s failure to wait politely. Stop hiding behind committees and holy months. Answer the question: Is MACC independent, or isn’t it?
