PH Backbenchers Call for Snap Election Dressed Up as 'Reform' — Hold GE16 With All State Polls, Save Money, They Say

Said by: Azli Yusof (Amanah-Shah Alam) / Jimmy Puah (PKR-Tebrau)

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-11

📌 What Happened

On March 11, two Pakatan Harapan backbenchers — Shah Alam MP Azli Yusof (Amanah) and Tebrau MP Jimmy Puah (PKR) — called for all state assembly elections to be held concurrently with the 16th General Election. Speaking to FMT, both MPs argued that separate elections lead to lower voter turnout, waste public money, and produce state governments with weaker mandates. Azli said separate polls are “unfair to voters, especially those who live far away from where they vote.” Puah called it “simply too tiring for people to come out and vote twice.” Putrajaya spent RM725 million on GE15 in 2022 and a further RM420 million on six subsequent state elections in 2023.

🔥 Why It Matters

This is not a neutral policy suggestion. It is PH’s latest move in a coordinated signal — following DAP secretary-general Anthony Loke’s identical proposal last year and PH Johor chairman Aminolhuda Hassan’s recent push — to align the political calendar for a snap general election. By calling for simultaneous polls, PH backbenchers are doing the government’s messaging work: normalizing the idea of an early dissolution while packaging it as cost-saving reform. The real audience is not voters who are tired of going to polling stations — it is UMNO, GPS, and other coalition partners who need to be brought on board before the PM pulls the trigger.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

PH did not care about “voter fatigue” when it suited them. In 2022, six PH-governed states chose to delay their state elections rather than hold them with GE15, citing monsoon concerns. Now, suddenly, the same coalition wants everything held together — but only because the timing benefits them. The opposition is fractured after the Bersatu-PAS split, the economy is holding, and Anwar’s approval is still intact. This is not reform. This is election engineering with a reformist paint job. If PH were serious about alignment, they would legislate fixed election dates — not send backbenchers to float trial balloons in FMT.

📜 Previous Cases

Malaysia has a long history of strategic election timing. Mahathir called snap elections when conditions favoured him. Najib dissolved Parliament early in 2013 and 2018. Even within the current cycle, the Sabah, Melaka, and Johor snap elections of 2020–2022 were all called for political advantage, not democratic principle. PH itself benefited from the chaos of misaligned polls — the August 2023 state elections became a de facto referendum on the unity government. The pattern is always the same: whoever controls the timing controls the narrative.

🎯 PH Callout

You promised institutional reform. You promised that governance would no longer be about political convenience. Yet here you are, using backbenchers to soften the ground for a snap election while pretending it is about saving the rakyat money. If you actually believe in synchronized elections, table a constitutional amendment to fix election dates — the way mature democracies do. But you will not, because the whole point is to keep the power to dissolve in the PM’s pocket. Stop dressing up electoral strategy as reform. Malaysians can tell the difference.

Police Question C4 Anti-Corruption CEO Under Section 124B — Anwar's Government Uses 'Plot to Topple' Probe to Interrogate Civil Society

Said by: Bukit Aman (Royal Malaysia Police) / C4 Center (Pushpan Murugiah, CEO)

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-11

📌 What Happened

On March 10, 2026, the Center to Combat Corruption and Cronyism (C4) CEO Pushpan Murugiah was summoned to Bukit Aman and questioned for approximately 90 minutes under Section 124B of the Penal Code — activities detrimental to parliamentary democracy. The questioning was linked to the police investigation into an alleged plot to topple the government, reportedly because Pushpan’s name appeared in an email deemed relevant to the probe. But the interrogation went far beyond that email. Police questioned C4’s sources of funding, whether the organisation had received instructions to issue statements, and — remarkably — challenged C4’s right to comment on matters of national governance. C4 confirmed that several other political commentators and academics had also been summoned to Bukit Aman under the same investigation.

🔥 Why It Matters

This is not a routine police inquiry. This is the machinery of state — operating under a government that came to power on a reform mandate — being deployed to scrutinise, pressure, and delegitimise an independent anti-corruption watchdog. Section 124B is a serious charge. Using it to question a civil society leader about their organisation’s funding, autonomy, and standing to comment on public affairs is a textbook chilling effect. It signals to every NGO, academic, and political commentator in Malaysia: criticise the government’s handling of the MACC scandal, and you may find yourself in a police interview room explaining why you think you have the right to speak. This is the same playbook that Malaysians rejected when BN did it. The fact that PH is now running it should alarm every voter who believed the reformasi promise.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

Anwar Ibrahim built his entire political career on the claim that he was persecuted by a government that weaponised institutions against critics. He spent years in prison. He rallied millions with the promise that under PH, the police would not be used to silence dissent. Now his government is hauling in the CEO of an anti-corruption NGO and asking — with a straight face — whether C4 has the standing to comment on corruption. The hypocrisy is breathtaking. C4 has been non-partisan since 2014. It criticised BN, it criticised PN, and now it criticises PH — and this is the administration that sends the police. The “plot to topple the government” narrative has become a catch-all justification for intimidating anyone who asks uncomfortable questions about Azam Baki, the MACC, and the corporate mafia allegations. If your anti-corruption credentials can’t survive scrutiny from an anti-corruption NGO, the problem is not the NGO.

📜 Previous Cases

This pattern is not new in Malaysian politics — it is simply new for PH. Under Najib, civil society leaders and journalists were routinely summoned under the Sedition Act, SOSMA, and the Communications and Multimedia Act for criticising 1MDB and government corruption. Under Muhyiddin, the Emergency Ordinance was used to suspend Parliament and silence dissent. Anwar himself was the most prominent victim of institutional weaponisation — twice jailed on charges widely seen as politically motivated. Now, under his own government, Section 124B is being stretched to cover the activities of NGOs whose only offence is demanding accountability from the MACC. Bersih was told to stop criticising MACC “during Ramadan.” DAP’s own youth wing warned of a “climate of fear.” And now C4’s CEO is sitting in Bukit Aman explaining why an anti-corruption organisation dares to talk about corruption.

🎯 PH Callout

You promised reformasi. You promised institutional independence. You promised that the police would never again be used as a political weapon. Now your police are questioning the CEO of an anti-corruption NGO about their right to speak — under the same “plot to topple” investigation that you’ve used to smear Daim’s family, attack Bloomberg, and deflect from the Azam Baki scandal. Every hour that Anwar’s government spends intimidating civil society instead of reforming the MACC is another hour that proves the Madani brand is hollow. If you want Malaysians to believe in your reform agenda, start by not behaving exactly like the governments you replaced.

Sabah Rejected PH at the Ballot Box — Now Mustapha Sakmud Says He'll Be Their Voice in Putrajaya Anyway

Said by: Mustapha Sakmud, Sabah PH Chairman / Sabah and Sarawak Affairs Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-11

📌 What Happened

On March 11, 2026, Sabah PH chairman and Sabah and Sarawak Affairs Minister Mustapha Sakmud told FMT that Sabah PH will serve as a “conduit” to ensure state issues are voiced at the federal level. He said he intends to leverage his ministerial appointment — made on February 15 — to bring Sabahan concerns to Cabinet meetings and government agencies. “This is the role we aim to play, and we hope the people of Sabah will appreciate it when their fundamental needs are addressed,” he said.

🔥 Why It Matters

PH was annihilated in the November 29, 2025 Sabah state election. PKR scraped through with a single seat. DAP won nothing. GRS took 29 seats, Warisan took 25, and Sabahans made it abundantly clear they did not want PH representing them at the state level. Yet barely three months later, Anwar handed Mustapha a federal ministerial portfolio — Sabah and Sarawak Affairs — effectively using the federal Cabinet to insert PH into Sabah’s political space through the back door. This is not democratic representation. This is patronage as a substitute for a mandate.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

You cannot lose an election this badly and then claim you are the people’s voice. PH won one seat in the entire Sabah state assembly. That is not a conduit — that is a rejection letter. Mustapha’s argument boils down to: “Sabahans didn’t vote for us, but we got a Cabinet post, so we’ll represent them anyway.” This is the exact kind of unearned political entitlement PH once accused BN of practising. The appointment rewards loyalty to Anwar, not service to Sabah. If Sabahans wanted PH to speak for them, they would have voted for PH. They didn’t.

📜 Previous Cases

This follows a long Malaysian tradition of using federal appointments to compensate for electoral losses at the state level. BN did it for decades in Sabah and Sarawak — parachuting federal figures into state politics through GLCs, agencies, and development corridors. PH, in opposition, railed against this exact practice. Now in government, they’ve adopted the same playbook. Anwar’s December 2025 Cabinet reshuffle — which created the Sabah and Sarawak Affairs portfolio — was widely seen as a damage-control exercise after PH’s Sabah wipeout. The pattern is clear: when voters reject you, buy your way back in through Putrajaya.

🎯 PH Callout

You promised reformasi. You promised that power would come from the people, not from Putrajaya. You told Sabahans for years that BN was using federal patronage to override their democratic choices. And now you’ve handed a Cabinet seat to a man whose coalition won one seat in an entire state election and told him to go represent the people who rejected him. If “the people of Sabah will appreciate it,” as Mustapha claims, then put it to a test — contest a by-election in Sabah on PH’s record. Otherwise, stop pretending a ministerial appointment is a mandate.

DAP Finally Demands RCI on MACC — Weeks Late, and PAS Correctly Calls It Political Theatre

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-10

📌 What Happened

On March 10, DAP — the largest party in the Pakatan Harapan coalition with 40 parliamentary seats — formally called for a Royal Commission of Inquiry (RCI) to investigate the Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission over allegations first reported by Bloomberg, including that MACC chief Azam Baki breached civil servant shareholding limits and that the agency was involved in corporate intimidation. PAS information chief Ahmad Fadhli Shaari responded by calling the demand “political theatre,” noting that DAP had remained silent when the same Bloomberg reports alleged that Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim personally interfered in MACC investigations.

🔥 Why It Matters

This is the clearest example yet of DAP’s credibility gap on institutional reform. The party spent weeks after the Bloomberg exposé refusing to directly challenge Anwar’s handling of Azam Baki, even as eight PKR MPs led by Rafizi Ramli publicly demanded Azam’s suspension in mid-February. DAP’s belated RCI call comes only after its catastrophic Sabah election loss and growing voter fury — not from genuine conviction. When even PAS can accurately identify your reform posture as performative, something has gone deeply wrong with your party’s moral compass.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

DAP had the numbers to force the issue from day one. Forty seats. The largest bloc in government. Instead, they sat on their hands for weeks while Bloomberg’s reporting exposed potential corruption at the highest levels of the anti-graft agency — and Anwar’s alleged interference in it. Now they want credit for demanding an RCI? That’s not accountability. That’s damage control dressed up as principle. And the most damning part: DAP still hasn’t addressed the Bloomberg allegation that their own Prime Minister interfered in MACC affairs. They’re demanding an investigation into everyone except the man they answer to.

📜 Previous Cases

This pattern is pure DAP under the unity government. After the Sabah election wipeout in November 2025, DAP pledged to “accelerate the reform agenda over the next six months.” Months later, the PM tenure limit bill failed partly because government MPs were absent, and DAP’s response was to blame the opposition rather than hold their own coalition accountable. On the Azam Baki shareholding scandal — which first surfaced in 2021 when DAP was in opposition and demanded action — DAP is now the party in government protecting the same man they once wanted removed. The pattern is clear: loud in opposition, silent in power.

🎯 PH Callout

DAP, you promised reformasi. You promised institutional accountability. You promised you were different. But when Bloomberg dropped the biggest anti-corruption story in Malaysian politics since 1MDB, your first instinct was to protect the Prime Minister, not the public. An RCI demand that comes weeks late and deliberately avoids the PM’s role isn’t reform — it’s theatre. If you want to prove PAS wrong, start by addressing the Bloomberg allegations about Anwar’s interference in MACC. Until then, Ahmad Fadhli is right: you are not qualified to champion this.

Rafizi Quietly Drops NFCorp Bankruptcy Bid — The Anti-Corruption Crusader Makes Nice With the People He Once Exposed

Said by: Rafizi Ramli, Pandan MP / Former Economy Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-10

📌 What Happened

Rafizi Ramli, the Pandan MP and former Economy Minister who built his political career on exposing the National Feedlot Corporation (NFCorp) scandal, has quietly dropped his bankruptcy and winding-up proceedings against NFCorp director Datuk Seri Dr Mohamad Salleh Ismail and the company itself. A Notice of Discontinuance was filed on March 3, and the High Court struck out the case on March 10 without an order as to costs. Rafizi’s lawyer Razlan Hadri Zulkifli confirmed the matter was “amicably resolved.” The case stemmed from court-ordered costs owed to Rafizi after the Federal Court upheld his appeal in April 2022 — NFCorp and Salleh had paid RM300,000 in December 2022 but stopped paying the outstanding interest balance.

🔥 Why It Matters

Rafizi’s entire political brand was built on the NFCorp exposé. In 2012, he blew the lid off a RM250 million government soft loan meant for a national cattle-rearing project that instead went toward luxury condominiums and personal expenses — a scandal that brought down UMNO Wanita chief Shahrizat Abdul Jalil and became a symbol of BN-era corruption. He was charged under the Official Secrets Act for it. He went to jail for it. The courts vindicated him all the way to the Federal Court. And now, in March 2026, with his own MACC investigation hanging over his head over the RM1.11 billion ARM Holdings chip deal, Rafizi quietly makes nice with the very people he once dragged through the courts. The timing is impossible to ignore.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

This is the man who told Malaysians he would never compromise on accountability. The man who said corruption must be pursued relentlessly, regardless of political cost. And now he’s walking away from money the courts said he was owed — not because he’s being magnanimous, but because the political calculus has changed. Rafizi is under MACC investigation. His former aide has fled to the UK. His political position within PKR is weakened after being dropped from Cabinet. When you’re no longer in a position of strength, you don’t pick fights — you settle. That’s not principle. That’s survival.

📜 Previous Cases

Rafizi has a pattern of selective aggression. He was relentless against BN-era scandals — NFCorp, 1MDB, Tabung Haji — when PH was in opposition. But once PH came to power, the fire dimmed. As Economy Minister, he championed the Central Database Hub (PADU) that drew criticism for data privacy concerns, and oversaw investment policies that favoured headline numbers over structural reform. His recent MACC trouble over the ARM Holdings chip deal, where he allegedly facilitated a RM1.11 billion investment in a company with questionable fundamentals, shows a man who has drifted far from his whistleblower roots. The NFCorp settlement is just the latest chapter in a long arc from crusader to career politician.

🎯 PH Callout

PH, you told Malaysians that Reformasi meant accountability without exception. Rafizi was your poster boy — the brave whistleblower who went to prison for exposing corruption. Now he’s settling with the people he exposed, dropping court-ordered debts, and making amicable deals while his own MACC probe gathers steam. If your best anti-corruption champion can’t even follow through on a case he already won, what exactly does PH stand for anymore? You don’t get to claim the moral high ground when your own people are cutting quiet deals in courtrooms.

Every Malaysian government, from Mahathir 1.0 to Muhyiddin to Anwar, has discovered that blaming racial tensions on shadowy outsiders is easier than fixing the system.

You ran on a platform of protecting civil liberties, repealing oppressive laws, and building a “Malaysia Madani” where citizens could speak freely. Now your ministers are holding press conferences warning Malaysians to shut up or face prosecution, while refusing to name the people they accuse. If these “agents” are real, name them, charge them, and let the courts decide — you said it yourself, Ramanan. If you cannot name them, then stop using vague threats to chill public discourse. You are not protecting national unity. You are protecting yourselves from accountability.

Your own backbencher called for a boycott of US embassy events. Now you’re calling Kuwait to condemn the same country you were defending. What is Malaysia’s actual position? Is it anti-aggression regardless of who the aggressor is? Then say that consistently. Is it pro-Muslim solidarity? Then explain why you’re condemning a Muslim-majority country attacking another Muslim-majority country. The rakyat deserves a foreign policy, not a mood board.

Anwar Repeats 'Aid Based on Need, Not Race' in Dewan Negara — Same Script, Different Session, Zero Structural Change

Said by: Anwar Ibrahim, Prime Minister

Category: Politics

Date: 2026-03-09

📌 WHAT HAPPENED

Prime Minister Datuk Seri Anwar Ibrahim told the Dewan Negara on March 9, 2026, that government aid and assistance are allocated based on need, not race. He affirmed that the Madani government would “remain fair to all states” and that assistance targets those most in need regardless of background.

This is not the first time Anwar has used this exact line. In August 2025, he told the same Dewan Negara the same thing — almost word for word — as reported by The Star and NST. Before that, the 13th Malaysia Plan (13MP) was framed around the same “needs-based, not race-based” rhetoric.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS

This is one of PH’s most repeated slogans. It was central to the Madani Economy pitch, and it resonates with voters who are tired of race-based distribution systems that benefit political insiders rather than the genuinely poor.

But slogans are not policy. The question is simple: has the government actually restructured any major Bumiputera-specific allocation program since taking power? Has any race-based quota been reformed? Has any procurement preference been revised to be purely needs-based?

The answer, by all available evidence, is no. The 13MP talked about shifting to needs-based frameworks, but core Bumiputera economic programs, GLICs, and procurement carve-outs remain structurally unchanged. The Poverty Line Income (PGK) thresholds have been updated — a technocratic adjustment — but the architecture of race-based distribution is untouched.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT

Saying “aid is based on need, not race” while leaving every race-based allocation structure intact is not reform. It is branding.

Anwar is not lying — technically, some aid programs like SARA and certain welfare payments do target low-income households regardless of race. But the big-ticket items — Bumiputera equity targets, MARA allocations, Bumiputera-only procurement set-asides, GLC preferential hiring — remain untouched. Nobody in the Madani government has proposed reforming any of these.

What Anwar is doing is classic misdirection: point to the small welfare programs that are needs-based, repeat the slogan, and hope nobody asks about the structural architecture that makes Malaysia’s economy one of the most racially stratified in the region.

Repeat it enough times in enough Dewan Negara sessions and maybe people will believe it. That is the strategy.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES

  • August 2025: Anwar told Dewan Negara that “allocations are based on needs, not race.” Same chamber, same script. (The Star, 25 Aug 2025)
  • July 2025: The 13MP was launched with the framing of shifting from race-based to needs-based policies. (Malay Mail, 31 Jul 2025)
  • Deputy Economy Minister Hanifah Hajar Taib (Dec 2025): Told Parliament the government was “reviewing” the use of disposable income as a fairer benchmark for aid eligibility — meaning even the basic measurement tools hadn’t been reformed yet, over two years into the administration. (The Star, 4 Dec 2025)
  • The core contradiction has existed since Anwar took office: the Madani government promises needs-based reform while relying on UMNO — the party whose entire political model depends on race-based distribution — as its coalition anchor.

🎯 PH CALLOUT

You ran on “Reformasi.” You promised a Malaysia where race would not determine access to opportunity. You told the rakyat that Madani meant fairness.

Three years later, every single race-based structural program remains intact. Not one has been reformed. Not one has been publicly reviewed. Not one has been opened to needs-based criteria.

And yet you stand in Dewan Negara — for the second time in less than a year — and say the same line: “based on need, not race.”

If that were true, show us the policy paper. Show us the program that was restructured. Show us the quota that was reformed.

Until then, this is not a policy. It is a press release.
