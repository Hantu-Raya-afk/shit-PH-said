99-Year-Old Kampung Lee Kong Chian Residents Told to Get Out — RM50K Per Family, Hannah Yeoh 'Unable to Attend'

Said by: Lo Su Fui, Deputy Minister (Federal Territories) / Hannah Yeoh, Minister (Federal Territories)

Category: Housing

Date: 2026-03-10

📌 What Happened

More than 150 residents of Kampung Lee Kong Chian in Setapak — Kuala Lumpur’s sole surviving private Chinese village, established in 1927 as quarters for Lee Rubber Group factory workers — held a peaceful rally on 8 March 2026 after 146 households received eviction notices in November 2025 ordering them to vacate by September. The land has been sold to a private company. Residents were offered RM50,000 per household in compensation, which they rejected, demanding People’s Housing Project (PPR) units instead. Federal Territories Minister Hannah Yeoh was absent from the village’s final Chap Goh Meh celebration on 10 March; Deputy Minister Lo Su Fui showed up, handed over RM10,000 for the dinner, and said he would “bring this matter back to the Federal Territories Department to explore how we can best provide assistance.” The government’s official position, per NST, is that it will “review the situation before deciding whether to intervene.”

🔥 Why It Matters

This is a textbook case of urban displacement dressed up as market transaction. Kampung Lee Kong Chian is not a squatter settlement — village chief Tan Lai Kee stressed the houses were built with formal approval from the original landowner. These are 230 households, many of them descendants of workers who built their lives around the Lee Rubber factory nearly a century ago. When the government allows a private sale to erase a community of over 1,000 people with six months’ notice and a RM50,000 cheque — roughly the price of a used Myvi — it tells you exactly where ordinary Malaysians sit in the hierarchy of development priorities. The Federal Territories Ministry, under PH’s Hannah Yeoh, is the body with direct jurisdiction over KL housing and land disputes. Yet the minister was absent, and the government’s posture is to “review” and “explore” — the bureaucratic language of stalling while the clock runs out on residents.

💩 Why It’s Bullshit

PH campaigned as the coalition that would protect communities from rapacious developers and crony capitalism. Anwar himself has spoken repeatedly about housing affordability, people-first governance, and the dignity of the rakyat. So when a 99-year-old village — with documented, legitimate housing built under formal approval — is handed a six-month eviction notice and a compensation offer that wouldn’t cover a deposit on a flat in Setapak, where is the reformist government? Hannah Yeoh sent a deputy with a RM10,000 dinner cheque. The deputy said he’d “bring this back” to the department. That’s not governance — that’s a holding pattern. If PH cannot protect a century-old KL community with clear historical legitimacy from being bulldozed by a private developer, then every “people first” slogan they’ve ever uttered is decoration.

📜 Previous Cases

This follows the same pattern as the Kampung Sungai Baru evictions in 2025, where residents were forced out amid redevelopment pressure with inadequate compensation and minimal government intervention. KL’s Kampung Baru — the last Malay enclave in the city centre — has faced decades of similar redevelopment threats, with successive governments oscillating between grand masterplans and inaction. The PH government has also pushed Nga Kor Ming’s Urban Renewal Bill, which would grant sweeping ministerial powers over property acquisition — a bill so controversial that even PH’s own backbenchers revolted. The pattern is consistent: communities with deep roots but no political leverage get treated as obstacles to development, while the government oscillates between performative sympathy and institutional passivity.

🎯 PH Callout

You promised Malaysia Madani — a civilised, compassionate Malaysia. A 99-year-old village with 230 families, descendants of workers who built this city, is being told to leave with RM50,000 and a handshake. Your Federal Territories Minister didn’t even show up to the last Chap Goh Meh these families will ever celebrate in their homes. You sent a deputy with dinner money. If this is what “reviewing the situation” looks like, then your review is a eulogy. These residents are not asking for handouts — they’re asking for replacement housing under PPR, a programme your own government administers. Either use the power you have to intervene, or stop pretending you govern for the people.

Nga Kor Ming's MyKiosk Project: No Needs Assessment, No KPIs, MACC Calls It a Governance Failure

Said by: Nga Kor Ming, Housing and Local Government Minister

Category: Housing

Date: 2026-02-10

📌 WHAT HAPPENED

On 10 February 2026, the Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission (MACC) revealed that the Housing and Local Government Ministry (KPKT) under Nga Kor Ming failed to carry out a proper needs assessment before launching the MyKiosk initiative — a nationwide programme to provide traders with modern kiosks.

MACC issued five governance improvement recommendations and stated: “The absence of a needs assessment and a comprehensive outcome-monitoring mechanism, including KPIs, hindered the evaluation of the project’s impact at the local authority level.”

Former MCA vice-president Ti Lian Ker immediately called for Nga’s resignation, saying: “When billions in public funds are at stake, failing to ask fundamental questions — who needs this, where it should be placed, and how success will be measured — is not just careless but irresponsible.”

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS

This is basic governance 101. You don’t launch a nationwide programme with public money without first asking: Does anyone need this? The MyKiosk project was described as a white elephant after failing to meet government objectives, with poorly placed kiosks causing public inconvenience rather than helping traders.

Ti Lian Ker noted this added to “a growing record of questionable judgments and misplaced priorities” — including Nga’s directive to turn four public toilets into smart toilets and public unease over the Urban Renewal Bill. The pattern is clear: tone-deaf decision-making, weak policy grounding, and public backlash after the fact.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT

Here’s the best part. In July 2025, when the MyKiosk project was first flagged as a white elephant, Nga invited MACC to investigate and then demanded an apology from critics when MACC found no corruption. He said: “To those who threw stones, don’t hide your hands.”

But no corruption doesn’t mean no failure. The MACC’s February 2026 report confirmed what everyone already knew: the project was poorly planned, poorly monitored, and poorly executed. Nga celebrated the absence of criminal intent while ignoring the presence of spectacular incompetence. That’s not governance — that’s a participation trophy.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES

Nga’s ministry has a pattern. The smart toilet initiative was mocked nationally. The Urban Renewal Bill drew public protest. Each episode follows the same arc: launch first, study later, apologise when caught, then demand credit for not being corrupt. PAS researcher Dr Nurul Islam called it the “execute first, study later” work culture of the Madani Government.

🎯 PH CALLOUT

You promised clean, competent governance. The MACC just told your ministry it didn’t even bother to check if anyone needed MyKiosk before spending public money on it. That’s not reform — that’s the same BN-era waste you used to condemn from the opposition bench. If a BN minister had done this, DAP would have held press conferences for weeks. When it’s your own minister? Silence.

Housing Affordability Crisis: B40 and M40 Locked Out as Prices Remain Beyond Reach

Said by: Multiple economists and NAPIC

Category: Housing

Date: 2025-10-07

The Promise of a Roof That Never Comes

Despite Anwar’s government capping affordable housing at RM300,000, the reality for B40 and M40 Malaysians remains bleak. The housing affordability crisis — one of the most serious socio-economic problems of the 21st century — has only deepened under PH.

The Facts

Fact: Using B40 median income as a benchmark, homes need to be priced below RM300,000 to be considered affordable. The government set this as the ceiling price.

Fact: National house prices increased by a cumulative 15.2% from 2019 to Q3 2025, but in real (inflation-adjusted) terms, the increase was just 4.2%.

Fact: 54.3% of KL residential transactions were below RM500,000, while 22.8% were above RM1 million — showing a deeply split market.

Fact: Research describes an “unsustainable dynamic, locking up a significant portion of the people — particularly the M40 and higher B40 — out of homeownership.”

Why It Matters

  • Setting a price cap is not the same as building homes at that price. Where are the units?
  • Young Malaysians are being priced out of their own cities. This is a generational crisis.

Nga Kor Ming's Urban Renewal Bill Gives One Minister Power to Override Property Rights — Even His Own MPs Revolt

Said by: Nga Kor Ming, Housing and Local Government Minister

Category: Housing

Date: 2025-08-25

📌 WHAT HAPPENED

In August 2025, Housing and Local Government Minister Nga Kor Ming tabled the Urban Renewal Bill 2025, designed to fast-track redevelopment of ageing urban housing. The bill proposed lowering the consent threshold for redevelopment — meaning property owners could be forced to sell or relocate even without unanimous agreement.

The backlash was immediate and bipartisan. Critics called the bill “defective, unlawful and devoid of legitimacy,” accusing Nga of concentrating too much power in one minister’s hands. The bill would allow redevelopment to proceed with just 80% resident consent, overriding the property rights of the remaining 20%.

Most remarkably, the revolt came from within PH’s own ranks. Seven PKR lawmakers and several BN MPs refused to support the bill, forcing the government to delay the second and third readings from August to October. By January 2026, Nga was still conducting “consultation sessions” with no clear timeline for retabling.

🔥 WHY IT MATTERS

This is a bill that would fundamentally alter property rights in Malaysia. Under the existing Strata Titles Act 1985, unanimous consent is required for redevelopment. Nga’s bill drops that to 80%, meaning one in five property owners can be forced out of their homes against their will.

The ISIS think tank warned the bill was “missing key components” and could lead to “displacement of local populations and gentrification of communities to make way for private development.” The Straits Times described mounting public backlash over fears the law would “force minority owners to give up their property rights.”

When your own coalition MPs refuse to back your bill, that’s not opposition mischief — that’s a governance failure.

💩 WHY IT’S BULLSHIT

Nga’s response to criticism was classic PH arrogance: he slammed the opposition for “not supporting” the bill, as if the problem were politics rather than substance. But it wasn’t just the opposition. His own coalition partners refused to vote for it.

The bill concentrates enormous power in the Housing Minister to determine which areas are “dilapidated” and eligible for forced redevelopment. Who benefits from that power? Developers. Who loses? Low-income urban residents — many of them elderly Malays in KL — who would be forced to accept whatever terms the government negotiates on their behalf.

Nga promised that residents would get “larger and better-quality homes” and temporary housing during construction. But promises from a minister whose MyKiosk programme failed MACC review and whose BMW toilets have produced zero results don’t carry much credibility.

📜 PREVIOUS CASES

Malaysia has a long and ugly history of forced urban displacement. Kampung Baru residents have fought for decades against redevelopment pressure. The Kampung Sungai Baru evictions in 2025 — where residents were forced out amid the Urban Renewal Bill controversy — showed exactly what critics feared.

Under BN, PH screamed about developer-friendly policies that displaced communities. Now PH is writing laws that make it even easier for developers to override property rights. The difference? PH calls it “urban renewal” instead of “development.”

🎯 PH CALLOUT

You promised to protect the rakyat from predatory development. Then your minister tabled a bill that lets the government force people out of their homes with 80% consent, and slammed anyone who objected. When your own MPs refuse to support your bill, that’s not a communication problem — it’s a substance problem. Fix the bill or drop it. Don’t bulldoze people’s property rights and call it reform.
